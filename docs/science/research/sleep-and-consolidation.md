# Sleep, Overnight Consolidation & Multi-Day Spacing — Research Note

**Topic:** Sleep-dependent memory consolidation and why it makes *spacing across days* (not just within a day) the right grain for the Hifz Companion schedule. Covers: the classic sleep-vs-wake protection of verbal memory; the active, slow-wave-sleep "system consolidation" account; the meta-analytic size of the overnight benefit for declarative/episodic memory; sleep's specific power to protect memories *against interference* (directly relevant to mutashābihāt); the multi-session result that *sleeping between two practice sessions* halves the relearning effort and durably improves retention; and the bridge to the spacing-effect literature — a study gap that *contains a night of sleep* is what converts in-session fluency into long-term retention. This is the empirical reason the engine schedules in **whole days** with a daily review session, never in sub-day micro-sessions, and why catch-up is re-spread across nights rather than crammed.

**Compiled:** 2026-06-16 (science research agent, Hifz Companion)

> **Bottom line for the engine.** Newly recited (or re-recited) Quran is *labile* for hours after the session; a night of sleep actively stabilises it, redistributes it from hippocampus toward cortex, strengthens it against later interference, and even integrates it with related material. The practical consequences are concrete: (1) the natural scheduling unit is the **day**, because the consolidation event that matters happens overnight; (2) **spacing reviews across days** beats compressing the same effort into one long sitting, partly *because* a night intervenes between sessions; (3) the cheapest large win in all of memory science here is *sleeping between two study sessions* — Mazza et al. found it **halved** the relearning effort and roughly **tripled** 6-month retention; and (4) since sleep is what protects similar passages from blurring together, mutashābihāt drilling should be spaced *across nights*, not only massed within a session. None of this requires the app to track sleep — it requires the app to *schedule in days and never penalise a user for revising once daily*.

> Evidence grades (blueprint §3, best → weakest among empirical): **[MA]** meta-analysis / systematic review · **[RCT]** randomized experiment · **[EXP]** controlled cognitive experiment · **[CS]** classic foundational study · **[OBS]** observational / applied / field study · **[TEXT]** textbook / expert review · **[TRAD]** traditional / scholarly Islamic source.

## What the evidence says

### 1. The century-old finding: a memory survives better across sleep than across an equal interval awake
The oldest experimental result in this literature is also the cleanest. Jenkins & Dallenbach had two observers learn lists of nonsense syllables and tested recall after intervals of 1, 2, 4, or 8 hours filled *either* with sleep *or* with ordinary waking activity; **forgetting was consistently slower across sleep than across waking**, and the gap widened with the longer intervals ([Jenkins & Dallenbach, 1924, *Am. J. Psychol.* 35:605–612](https://www.jstor.org/stable/1414040)) **[CS]**. Their own interpretation was *passive* — sleep merely shields a fading trace from the interference of waking experience — and for eighty years that "passive protection" account stood. It matters to us for two reasons: first, it establishes that *verbal* memory (the closest list-learning analogue to recited text) is specifically sleep-sensitive; second, even on the most conservative (passive) reading, the lesson is identical — **a memory laid down before sleep is in better shape the next day than one laid down to be tested the same day.** That is already an argument for a once-daily revision rhythm.

### 2. The modern correction: sleep *actively* consolidates declarative memory — it is not just passive shelter
The contemporary account replaces "shelter" with "processing." During **slow-wave sleep (SWS)**, the hippocampus repeatedly *reactivates* newly encoded memories and, in a coordinated dialogue of slow oscillations, thalamo-cortical spindles, and hippocampal sharp-wave ripples at minimal cholinergic tone, *redistributes* those memories toward neocortical long-term stores — a process termed **active system consolidation** ([Diekelmann & Born, 2010, *Nat. Rev. Neurosci.* 11:114–126](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrn2762)) **[TEXT]**. The mechanistic claims are testable and have held up: artificially boosting slow oscillations or reactivating specific traces during SWS *improves* later recall of word pairs, while raising acetylcholine during SWS (which signals "encode," not "consolidate") *blocks* declarative consolidation ([Gais & Born, 2004, *PNAS* 101:2140–2144](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0305404101)) **[EXP]**. For the engine the upgrade from passive to active matters: consolidation is not merely "avoiding new interference," it is a *constructive* overnight event that makes yesterday's review qualitatively more durable — which is exactly why review effort placed *before* a night pays off more than the same effort placed to be tested within the same day.

### 3. The overnight benefit for declarative memory is real, moderate, and meta-analytically established
Two large quantitative syntheses pin the size of the effect. The most comprehensive, covering **823 effect sizes from 271 independent samples across 177 articles (1967–2019)**, found a **moderate overall sleep benefit for episodic memory of g ≈ 0.44**, largest in the canonical night-sleep-vs-day-wake design, and also clearly present for naps ([Berres & Erdfelder, 2021, *Psychol. Bull.* 147(12):1309–1353](https://doi.org/10.1037/bul0000350)) **[MA]**. A targeted-reactivation meta-analysis (cueing specific memories during sleep to force their reactivation) found a reliable **g ≈ 0.29** improvement for cued over un-cued items, and crucially located the effect in **NREM / slow-wave sleep** — *"TMR was not effective during REM sleep nor during wakefulness"* — across **91 experiments, 212 effect sizes, N = 2,004** ([Hu, Cheng, Chiu & Paller, 2020, *Psychol. Bull.* 146(3):218–244](https://doi.org/10.1037/bul0000223)) **[MA]**. The convergent reading: the overnight benefit is robust and reproducible, it is a *moderate* (not miraculous) effect on top of practice, and it is driven by deep NREM sleep reactivating the trace — not by wishful "learning in your sleep." This calibrates our copy honestly: sleep helps consolidation meaningfully, but it is the *daily recitation* that does the heavy lifting; sleep finishes the job.

### 4. Controlling for time-of-day and interference, sleep still improves vocabulary retention
A frequent objection to sleep studies is confounding: people who "sleep" also learn in the evening and are tested after a day's normal forgetting. Gais, Lucas & Born built a within-subject vocabulary design (high-school students learning **24 English–German word pairs**) that crossed learning time (morning vs evening) with retention interval, so sleep-soon-after-learning could be compared against equal time awake *with time-of-day and interfering daytime activity balanced*; **learning shortly before sleep produced significantly less forgetting than learning followed by a full day awake**, and a second experiment showed **sleep deprivation on the first post-learning night impaired recall even after two recovery nights** ([Gais, Lucas & Born, 2006, *Learn. Mem.* 13(3):259–262](https://learnmem.cshlp.org/content/13/3/259.full.html)) **[EXP]**. This is the single most domain-relevant controlled demonstration for us: the task is *paired-associate foreign vocabulary* — structurally close to binding an Arabic phrase to its position and continuation — and it says the *first* night after acquiring material is special and not recoverable later. Implication: a page recited today benefits most from tonight's sleep; deferring its first revision by many days forfeits that window.

### 5. Sleep protects memories *against interference* — the mechanism that matters most for mutashābihāt
This is the finding with the sharpest bearing on hifz. Ellenbogen et al. taught participants a list of **12 A–B word pairs**, imposed a 12-hour interval of either overnight sleep or daytime wake, then — just before testing — taught an **interfering A–C list** (the same cues mapped to new responses, the classic retroactive-interference design). Without interference, sleep and wake groups recalled the original list about equally (**~80% vs ~79%**). *With* interference, the wake group collapsed to **56%** while the **sleep group held at 68%** — sleep had made the original memory *resistant* to the new, competing material ([Ellenbogen, Hulbert, Stickgold, Dinges & Thompson-Schill, 2006, *Curr. Biol.* 16(13):1290–1294](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0960982206016071)) **[EXP]**. The hifz parallel is direct: a near-identical passage learned later (or a similar verse encountered in the same session) is precisely an "A–C list" competing with the "A–B" the ḥāfiẓ already holds. The evidence says a **consolidated** (slept-on) passage is far harder to overwrite by a confusable sibling than a freshly-recited, not-yet-consolidated one — so *when* in the day/week confusable material is reviewed, and whether the target has had a night to set, is a real variable, not folklore.

### 6. Sleep doesn't just preserve — it *integrates* related items into a structure
Beyond protection, sleep actively *binds* separately-learned facts into a relational whole. Ellenbogen et al. trained participants on overlapping premise pairs (A>B, B>C, … E>F) and tested transitive inference (e.g. is B>E?); those tested **20 minutes** later were essentially at chance on the hardest, most-distant inferences, whereas those given **12 hours including sleep** could make them — sleep had knitted the isolated premises into a single ordered hierarchy ([Ellenbogen, Hu, Payne, Titone & Walker, 2007, *PNAS* 104(18):7723–7728](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0610863104)) **[EXP]**. For a ḥāfiẓ this maps onto the lived experience that a page recited in isolation today is, after a night, easier to chain into the surrounding context (the prior page's ending cueing the next page's opening) — serial recall is exactly such relational binding. It reinforces scheduling in *days*: the integration that makes a page flow into its neighbours is an overnight product, not a within-session one.

### 7. The headline applied result: *sleeping between two sessions* halves relearning effort and tripled long-term retention
The most actionable study in this note tests not "sleep vs wake after learning" but "sleep *between* two learning sessions" — the structure of every multi-day revision plan. Mazza et al. had **40 adults** learn **16 Swahili–French word pairs** by retrieval-restudy to criterion, then relearn the same list **12 hours later**; one group slept between sessions (evening→morning), the other stayed awake (morning→evening), with sleep quality, chronotype and time-of-day controlled ([Mazza, Gerbier, Gustin, Kasikci, Koenig, Toppino & Magnin, 2016, *Psychol. Sci.* 27(10):1321–1330](https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797616659930)) **[EXP]**. The numbers are striking:
- **Relearning was roughly twice as efficient with sleep between:** the sleep group needed **≈3.05 list-trials** to re-reach criterion vs **≈5.80** for the wake group (*d* = 2.00) — *all* sleep-group participants relearned within five trials, while **75% of the wake group needed more**.
- **Long-term retention was far higher.** One week later the sleep group recalled **15.20 of 16** vs the wake group's **11.25** (*d* = 2.93); at **six months** the gap not only held but widened in proportional terms — **8.67 vs 3.35** correct (*d* = 2.46). Of items recalled at one week, **60% survived to six months in the sleep group versus only 30% in the wake group.**
- The authors' summary: *"sleeping after learning is definitely a good strategy, but sleeping between two learning sessions is a better strategy"* — and the relearning saving plus retention gain suggest a *super-additive* interaction between practice and sleep, not two independent effects added.

This is the empirical heart of a *daily* murājaʿa rhythm: the same total recitation buys dramatically more durable hifz when its repetitions straddle nights rather than pile into one sitting.

### 8. The bridge to spacing: a study gap "works" partly because it contains a night of sleep
The sleep literature and the spacing literature meet here. Bell, Kawadri, Simone & Wiseheart isolated the role of sleep *within* a spacing design: they varied the gap between an initial study and a restudy of Swahili–English pairs across **massed, 12-hours-same-day (no sleep), 12-hours-overnight (with sleep), and 24-hours**, then tested long-term retention. The result: **a 12-hour gap that *included sleep* produced long-term retention comparable to the full 24-hour gap, and better than 12 daytime hours without sleep** — *"the findings support the importance of sleep to the long-term benefit of the spacing effect"* ([Bell, Kawadri, Simone & Wiseheart, 2014, *Memory* 22(3):276–283](https://doi.org/10.1080/09658211.2013.778294)) **[EXP]**. In other words, part of *why* spacing across a day beats massing within a day is that the across-day gap banks an overnight consolidation event. This dovetails with the sibling spacing note (the optimal review gap grows with the retention horizon; [Cepeda, Pashler, Vul, Wixted & Rohrer, 2006, *Psychol. Bull.* 132(3):354–380](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.132.3.354)) **[MA]** — sleep supplies a concrete mechanism for the lower end of those gaps, where the *day* is the meaningful quantum.

### 9. Why this licenses scheduling in whole days (and not sub-day micro-sessions)
Putting §§1–8 together yields a design principle, not just a fact: the consolidation that converts a recitation into durable hifz is largely an **overnight, NREM-sleep event**, so the smallest scheduling interval that captures one full consolidation cycle is **one day/night**. Splitting the same review into several within-day micro-sessions does not buy extra consolidation cycles — it only re-massing inside a single waking window. Conversely, *adding nights* between repetitions is the cheap, reliable lever (Mazza 2016; Bell 2014). This is consistent with — and supplies an independent justification for — the engine's day-grained scheduling: cards carry a stability in **days**, `due_at` is a **calendar date**, and "today" is injected as a date (PRD §7.2, §19.3). The app neither needs nor wants finer-than-daily timing, because biology does not reward it.

### Where the evidence is consistent vs. unsettled
- **Consistent across every source:** verbal/declarative memory is retained better across sleep than across equal waking time; the effect is meta-analytically moderate (g ≈ 0.4 for episodic memory; g ≈ 0.3 for targeted reactivation) and reproducible; it is driven by deep NREM/slow-wave sleep, not REM and not wakefulness; sleep specifically protects memories *against later interference* and integrates them relationally; and inserting sleep *between* practice sessions both speeds relearning and durably improves long-term retention.
- **Qualified / debated:** whether the benefit is best described as *active* system consolidation (Diekelmann & Born) or partly *passive* protection from interference (the Jenkins–Dallenbach lineage) — current evidence favours an active, multicausal account, but both readings give the *same* day-grained scheduling advice. The relative contributions of slow oscillations, spindles, and ripples, and exactly which memories get prioritised for reactivation, remain active research.
- **Thin spots for our domain:** the controlled studies use word-pairs, nonsense syllables, and short inference hierarchies over hours-to-months — *not* multi-year maintenance of an over-learned sacred text under heavy interference. We therefore treat "sleep consolidates" as a robust *mechanism* that justifies day-grained, across-night scheduling, **not** as a quantitative promise about a specific page. The retention guarantee remains structural (the cycle ceiling, PRD §7.6), not biological.

## Implications for Hifz Companion

Numbered, concrete rules the engine and copy should implement. Items marked **MUST / MUST NOT** are hard constraints traceable to the PRD.

1. **Schedule in whole days; never in sub-day micro-sessions.** The consolidation cycle that durably fixes a recitation is overnight (§§2, 9). The engine **MUST** keep stability `S` in *days*, set `due_at` as a *calendar date*, and inject "today" as a date (PRD §7.2, §19.3) — there is no biological payoff to scheduling reviews hours apart within one day, so the app should not invite it.

2. **Make once-daily revision the honoured rhythm, and never shame a single daily pass.** Mazza et al. (2016) and Bell et al. (2014) show the same effort spread across nights vastly out-retains the same effort massed in a day. So a user who does *one* calm daily session is doing the *optimal* thing, not the minimal thing. The daily-session design (PRD §12.2) and calm notification copy (PRD §14, R3) are therefore not just gentle — they are *correct*. **MUST NOT** push guilt-driven "do more today" nudges that implicitly favour same-day re-massing.

3. **Frame "revise daily, let it set overnight" as the mechanism behind the schedule.** The in-app science screen (PRD §12, planned `science/08-sleep-consolidation-and-scheduling`) may state, in calm non-promotional terms, that *"a night's sleep helps fix what you revised today into lasting memory, which is why revision is spread across days rather than crammed."* **MUST** carry a grade and source (Diekelmann & Born 2010 **[TEXT]**; Mazza et al. 2016 **[EXP]**) per blueprint §2; **MUST NOT** claim the app "uses your sleep," track sleep, or promise a retention percentage (PRD §7.5, C2).

4. **Re-spread missed-day catch-up across nights — never dump or cram it.** The biology says cramming a reactivated juz into one sitting wastes the across-night multiplier that makes it stick (§§7, 8). The catch-up planner (PRD §7.9) **MUST** re-flow a backlog over several days ("you missed 3 days — here is a 5-day catch-up plan"), and the copy should explain plainly that *spreading it over nights is what makes it hold*, turning a constraint into a feature.

5. **Space mutashābihāt practice across nights, not only within a session.** Ellenbogen et al. (2006) show a *consolidated* (slept-on) passage resists being overwritten by a confusable sibling far better than a fresh one. This refines — it does not contradict — the PRD §9.2 rule to interleave siblings *within* a session for discriminative contrast: massed contrast teaches the distinction *now*, but **the distinction is locked in across nights**. So a confusion pair flagged today should be **re-surfaced on subsequent days** (its difficulty bump already shortens intervals, PRD §7.7) so each sibling gets repeated overnight consolidation against the other, not a one-off drill.

6. **Treat the first night after (re)learning a page as a high-value, non-deferrable window.** Gais et al. (2006) show the *first* post-learning sleep is special and not fully recoverable later. So when a page is newly memorised or reactivated from a lapse, its first follow-up review should land **the next day**, not many days out — the conservative New-phase target and short post-lapse stability already do this (PRD §7.5, §7.7); this is the biological justification for keeping them short rather than a tunable nicety.

7. **Recite *before* the day ends, not the schedule's only constraint, but worth a gentle default.** Because consolidation rides on the night *after* a session, the optional daily reminder (PRD §14) defaulting to a time that leaves the session *before* the user's sleep is mildly better than a late-night-only nudge. This is a soft default, **MUST** remain fully user-set and silenceable (R3), and **MUST NOT** become a sleep-tracking or "recite before bed or lose your hifz" guilt mechanic.

8. **Keep the retention promise structural, not biological.** The honesty value (science §5; PRD §7.5–§7.6) holds here: sleep is a moderate, real *mechanism* (g ≈ 0.4) studied on word-lists, not a guarantee about a specific sacred page over years. The app's near-100% promise comes from the **cycle ceiling** (every page re-recited at least once per cycle), and sleep merely explains *why the daily, across-night cadence is efficient*. State this where the science screen explains the schedule; **MUST NOT** overstate sleep into a retention claim.

9. **Let the across-night spacing argument carry the load-balancer's honest framing.** When the budget can't fit everything (PRD §7.9, §12.2), the right message is "a longer cycle spreads revision across more nights and retains *better*, not worse" — Bell et al. (2014) and Mazza et al. (2016) make widening the cadence an *efficiency* gain, not laziness. This is the non-coercive framing for budget feedback for the late-starter and busy ḥāfiẓ (P1/P3): fewer pages per day, more nights between, is a feature.

10. **Add the sleep findings to the CLAIMS register, graded and scoped.** Any user-facing statement that "sleep helps fix today's revision" or "spreading revision across days retains better than cramming" **MUST** appear in `CLAIMS.md` (group: *Spacing & scheduling* / *Memory & forgetting*) with ≥1 verified source and a grade, and **MUST** be scoped as a mechanism, never a percentage or a sleep-tracking feature (C2, R3).
