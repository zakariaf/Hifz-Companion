# Interference Theory & the Mutashābihāt Problem — Research Note

**Topic:** Interference theory as the cognitive basis of the *mutashābihāt* (similar-verse) problem in ḥifẓ: proactive vs. retroactive interference; why similarity between materials — not the passage of time — drives the worst forgetting; response competition and retrieval-induced (cue-competition) forgetting; and the discrimination/contrast training (interleaving, juxtaposition) that the experimental literature shows actually cures confusion between near-identical items. The aim is to ground the PRD's first-class *mutashābihāt* subsystem (§9) and its "interference is the enemy, not just decay" design principle in real cognitive science.

**Compiled:** 2026-06-16

> Evidence grades (blueprint §3): `[MA]` meta-analysis/review · `[RCT]` randomized · `[EXP]` controlled cognitive experiment · `[CS]` classic foundational study · `[OBS]` observational/applied/field · `[TEXT]` textbook/expert review · `[TRAD]` traditional/scholarly Islamic source.

This note distils into the *mutashābihāt* synthesis doc (`science/05-interference-and-mutashabihat.md`) and is a sibling to the forgetting-curve, spacing-effect, and retrieval-practice notes; where those explain ordinary decay and spacing, this one explains why **similarity** — not time — is the dominant adversary for a finished ḥāfiẓ, and what training actually fixes it.

## What the evidence says

### 1. Forgetting is caused by *interference*, not the mere passing of time

The decisive break with the idea that memories "fade" with time came from John McGeoch, who in *Forgetting and the law of disuse* (1932) dismantled the "law of disuse" (that memories decay through mere lack of use) and argued instead that forgetting is "due to what intervenes between learning and recall" — other learning and altered cues, not the clock. His positive account is **associative** and **cue-driven**: a single cue can become attached to several competing responses, and recall of the wanted one suffers **response competition** ("momentary dominance" of one association over another) from the rest ([McGeoch, 1932, *Psychological Review* 39:352–370](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1932-04263-001)) **[CS]**. This is precisely the ḥifẓ situation: the shared opening of a *mutashābih* passage is one cue pointing at two (or more) continuations, and the brain retrieves the dominant-but-wrong branch. McGeoch's two governing principles — *interpolated activity* and *altered stimulating conditions* — are still treated as the foundation of interference theory in modern reviews ([Anderson & Neely, in *Memory* (Bjork & Bjork, eds.), 1996; summarized in the Waterloo Memory Lab review of interference theory](https://uwaterloo.ca/memory-attention-cognition-lab/sites/default/files/uploads/files/interference_theory_4_final_revised.pdf)) **[TEXT]**. The contrast with decay matters for product framing: a page is rarely lost because it sat idle — it is lost because a *similar* page overwrote the cue.

### 2. Two directions of interference — proactive and retroactive — and a ḥāfiẓ suffers both

- **Retroactive interference (RI):** newly learned material disrupts the recall of older material. It is the classically demonstrated direction (named by [Müller & Pilzecker, 1900](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interference_theory)) **[CS]**.
- **Proactive interference (PI):** older material disrupts the recall of newer material. Benton Underwood's landmark re-analysis of why Ebbinghaus-style lists are forgotten showed PI is far more important to everyday forgetting than had been assumed: he found that **forgetting of a freshly learned list rose with the number of lists the person had learned beforehand** — a learner who had memorized many prior lists forgot the newest one far faster than a first-time learner. Much of what looks like simple "decay" is actually intrusion from everything learned before ([Underwood, 1957, *Psychological Review* 64:49–60](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1958-01239-001)) **[CS]**.

For the ḥāfiẓ both directions run at once. A newly strengthened *sabaq* page can overwrite an old *manzil* twin (RI); a deeply over-learned early-juz wording can intrude when reciting its later-memorized counterpart (PI). Underwood's result is especially pointed for ḥifẓ: the *more* a person has already memorized, the heavier the proactive load on each new passage — so PI predicts that a near-complete ḥāfiẓ adding the last juz fights more interference than a beginner did on the first. The practical reading is that **every member of a similar group is at risk whenever any one of them is touched** — which is why the PRD treats interference as a property of the *pair/group*, not the single page (PRD §9.2).

### 3. The more *similar* two passages are, the worse the interference — this is quantified, not folklore

McGeoch & McDonald had participants learn a 10-item list to perfect recall, then learn a second, interpolated list of varying similarity before being tested on the first. Recall of the original list fell as the interpolated list grew more similar in meaning: **synonyms produced the most forgetting (lowest recall), then antonyms, then words merely related, then unrelated words, then nonsense syllables and three-digit numbers, with the rest-only control group forgetting least** ([McGeoch & McDonald, 1931, *American Journal of Psychology* 43:579–588](https://www.jstor.org/stable/1415159)) **[CS]**. This **similarity gradient** is the single most important empirical fact for *mutashābihāt*: passages differing by one or two words sit at the extreme high-similarity end of exactly this curve, so they are predicted to interfere near-maximally — which is why ordinary repetition (more exposure) does not fix them and may even deepen the confusion if the twins are never contrasted.

Charles Osgood organized the full pattern into his **transfer-and-retroaction surface** (1949), a three-dimensional map of transfer/interference as a joint function of *stimulus* similarity and *response* similarity. Its key regularity: when stimuli stay similar but the required responses **diverge**, you move from helpful transfer into **maximum interference**. That is the exact structure of a *mutashābih* pair — an identical or near-identical opening (the "stimulus" cue) demanding a *different* continuation (the "response") in each location ([Osgood, 1949, *Psychological Review* 56:132–143](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1949-05293-001)) **[CS]**. Osgood's "similarity paradox" also carries the seed of the cure: identical responses *facilitate*, so the way out of interference is to make the learner treat the two passages as clearly different responses — i.e., to train the discrimination (see §5).

### 4. Interference is *cue-competition*, and it can be triggered or relieved by what you study together

Two complementary phenomena pin down the mechanism as competition among responses sharing a cue:

- **Build-up and release from proactive interference.** When successive items come from the same category, PI accumulates and recall worsens trial by trial; the moment the category *changes*, performance springs back to its initial level — "release from PI." Crucially, the release is driven by **meaningful/semantic** difference, not surface features, showing that interference tracks how items are *encoded and discriminated*, not how they look ([Wickens, 1973, *Memory & Cognition* 1:485–492](https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/BF03198132); [Wickens, Born & Allen, 1963, *Journal of Verbal Learning and Verbal Behavior*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delos_Wickens)) **[EXP]**. Implication: confusion between two passages is not fixed — it depends on whether the learner has built a discriminating cue.

- **Retrieval-induced forgetting (RIF).** Repeatedly retrieving some members of a category actively *suppresses* the unpracticed members of that **same** category, and this happens **only when the items compete for a shared cue** ([Anderson, Bjork & Bjork, 1994, *Journal of Experimental Psychology: LMC* 20:1063–1087](https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2016/07/Anderson_RBjork_EBjork_1994.pdf)) **[EXP]**. The ḥifẓ warning is sharp: **drilling one verse of a confusable pair can degrade its twin.** A scheduler that surfaces *mutashābihāt* siblings independently risks strengthening one branch at the silent expense of the other — the opposite of what is wanted.

### 5. Discrimination is the cure — but only when the confusable items are *juxtaposed* (interleaved), not studied in isolation

The interference literature's solution is **discriminative contrast**: put the confusable items next to each other so the learner is forced to notice what distinguishes them.

- Kornell & Bjork showed that **interleaving** exemplars of different (confusable) categories beat **blocking** them for inductive learning — participants who saw artists' paintings shuffled together later classified novel paintings better than those who saw each artist's work massed, *even though massing felt more effective* (a metacognitive illusion) ([Kornell & Bjork, 2008, *Psychological Science* 19(6):585–592](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2008.02127.x)) **[EXP]**.
- Birnbaum, Kornell, E. Bjork & R. Bjork isolated *why*: interleaving helps because it allows **discrimination** — juxtaposing categories highlights their differences. When they spaced items in time *without* juxtaposing the categories, the benefit vanished; the gain came specifically from the **contrast between confusable items**, not from spacing per se. This is the **discriminative-contrast hypothesis** ([Birnbaum, Kornell, Bjork & Bjork, 2013, *Memory & Cognition* 41:392–402](https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/s13421-012-0272-7)) **[EXP]**.
- Carvalho & Goldstone confirmed and bounded it: interleaving wins **precisely when categories are highly similar/confusable** (it emphasizes between-category differences), while blocking is better for low-similarity categories (it emphasizes within-category commonalities). Temporal spacing that *interrupted* the juxtaposition removed the discrimination benefit ([Carvalho & Goldstone, 2014, *Memory & Cognition* 42(3):481–495](https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/s13421-013-0371-0)) **[EXP]**.

The directional headline — **spacing is the friend of recall but the enemy of induction** (Rothkopf, 1977), overturned for confusable categories by the work above — is the exact tension a ḥifẓ scheduler must resolve: ordinary pages want *spacing*; *mutashābihāt* siblings want **back-to-back contrast**.

### 6. The Quran-memorization literature independently arrives at the same diagnosis and cure

Practitioner and academic sources on ḥifẓ describe the *mutashābihāt* problem in terms that map cleanly onto interference theory, without using its vocabulary:

- A field study of tahfiz students at Darul Quran (JAKIM) and KUIS found the dominant coping methods were **identifying the similar verses and noting the differences, consulting the meaning (tafsīr/translation) to differentiate them, and repeating with focus on the similar pairs** — and noted that no institution had a dedicated reference for the problem despite its documented severity ([Ahmad, Saleh, Musa, Alias & Muhammad, 2021, *Jurnal Islam dan Masyarakat Kontemporari* 22(3):77–85](https://doi.org/10.37231/jimk.2021.22.3.525)) **[OBS]**. "Noting the difference" and "focusing on the similar pairs" is discriminative contrast by another name.
- A practitioner analysis argues that the standard method — revising each sūrah in isolation — *causes* the confusion, because "your brain never has to distinguish between the similar versions"; it recommends **collecting the variants, shuffling them out of sūrah order, and drilling the confusable pairs back-to-back**, focusing on the distinguishing word ([Qari Mubashir, "You're Studying the Similar Verses Wrong," howtomemorisethequran.com / Substack](https://qari.substack.com/p/youre-studying-the-similar-verses)) **[TRAD]**. This is interleaving plus anchor-word discrimination, derived purely from ḥifẓ experience.
- The traditional corpus recognizes the problem's scale: classical and contemporary teachers document **300+ near-identical passage groups**, and "dropping the manzil/dhor causes the most loss," with *mutashābihāt* repeatedly named as a leading failure mode for advanced ḥuffāẓ (RESEARCH-FINDINGS §1; [getitqan.com: *What is Mutashabihat*](https://getitqan.com/blog/what-is-mutashabihat)) **[TRAD]**.

### 7. Why this matters more as a ḥāfiẓ grows — interference scales with corpus size, opposite to decay

Independent-item decay is roughly constant per item; **interference is combinatorial**. Every newly mastered passage can become a competing response to some existing cue, so the number of confusable pairs grows with the corpus — a 30-juz ḥāfiẓ holds far more *mutashābih* conflicts than a 5-juz student. This is consistent across the interference literature (similarity-driven competition; [Underwood, 1957](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1958-01239-001)) and the ḥifẓ accounts (errors increase "as more is memorized"; RESEARCH-FINDINGS §3). The design consequence is structural: a maintenance/retention app for *finished* ḥuffāẓ — the PRD's primary persona (P1) — is exactly the population for whom interference, not decay, is the dominant adversary.

## Implications for Hifz Companion

1. **Treat interference as a first-class subsystem distinct from decay (PRD §9).** The FSRS-style backbone models decay of independent items; it does **not** model cross-item competition. The *mutashābihāt* layer is therefore a genuine extension, not a parameter tweak — it must exist alongside the scheduler, holding a graph of confusable groups. Evidence: no classic SR algorithm represents interference (RESEARCH-FINDINGS §3); interference is cue-competition between items (McGeoch, 1932; Anderson et al., 1994).

2. **Schedule confusable siblings *together*, interleaved back-to-back — never in isolation.** When any page in a *mutashābih* group is due, pull its sibling(s) into the **same session**, adjacent, so the user is forced to discriminate them. This is the directly evidenced cure (Kornell & Bjork, 2008; Birnbaum et al., 2013; Carvalho & Goldstone, 2014). The PRD already specifies this (§9.2 "discrimination interleaving"); the science confirms it is the *mechanism*, not a nicety.

3. **Do not let ordinary spacing rules apply to similar pairs.** Spacing helps plain recall but is the *enemy* of discrimination when items are confusable (Birnbaum et al., 2013; Carvalho & Goldstone, 2014). The trust-clamp/ordering logic (PRD §7.6, §7.8) must carve out *mutashābihāt*: their value comes from **temporal juxtaposition**, so the engine should resist spreading siblings across different days. Spacing them apart actively worsens the problem.

4. **Beware retrieval-induced forgetting: drilling one sibling can suppress the other.** Because retrieval of one competitor inhibits the unpracticed competitor (Anderson et al., 1994), a *mutashābih* drill must exercise **all** members of the group, not just the one that was due. Concretely: a "wrong-branch" stumble bumps difficulty and review frequency on **every** group member, and discrimination drills always present the contrasting pair, never one side alone (PRD §9.2 "confusion-aware grading"). Surfacing one branch on its own is a hidden trap.

5. **Make the distinguishing word the unit of practice (anchor hinting).** The interference is localized to where the responses diverge (Osgood, 1949); the cure is attention to that divergence (Birnbaum et al., 2013; the tahfiz "note the difference" method, Ahmad et al., 2021). The trainer should render the **distinguishing phrase** as a highlight overlay on the immutable glyph page (PRD §9.2 anchor hinting, §11.2 overlay rendering) and build micro-drills around it — never re-typesetting the sacred text.

6. **Grade and log confusion at the *pair*, and let it raise difficulty for the whole group.** Capture "swap" errors (page A's wording recited in page B's location) in the personal confusion log (PRD §9.1, `confusion_edge` table §10.2). A logged swap should raise `D` (difficulty) on **both** members, which the FSRS math automatically converts into shorter intervals — denser review for the pair, no special-case scheduler needed (RESEARCH-FINDINGS §3; PRD §7.7). The error is a property of the edge, not the node.

7. **Seed the confusable graph from a scholar-reviewed, objective dataset; grow it from the user's own swaps.** Interference is predictable from *objective* wording overlap (the high-similarity end of McGeoch & McDonald's gradient), so the bundled dataset must be scoped to **objective near-identical/identical wording only** — never interpretive or thematic groupings (PRD R4, §9.1). Personalization comes solely from the user's logged confusions: plain bookkeeping, no AI (PRD C2).

8. **Frame *mutashābihāt* drills as discrimination, not extra repetition — and expect them to feel harder.** The "blocked" isolation method *feels* smooth precisely because it removes the discrimination challenge (Kornell & Bjork's metacognitive illusion, 2008; the practitioner account). Product copy and the dedicated Mutashābihāt screen (PRD §9.3, §12.4) should set the expectation that interleaved contrast is meant to feel effortful, and that this difficulty is the point — without gamifying it or weaponizing guilt (PRD C6, R3).

9. **Surface, honestly, that this risk grows with how much one has memorized.** Because confusable pairs scale with corpus size (Underwood, 1957; RESEARCH-FINDINGS §3), the app should treat the near-complete ḥāfiẓ (P1) as the *most* interference-exposed user and make the Mutashābihāt subsystem prominent for them — consistent with the positioning that "interference is the enemy, not just decay" (PRD §2 design principle 4). This is honest, not alarmist: it explains why a finished ḥāfiẓ still stumbles, and what the app does about it.
