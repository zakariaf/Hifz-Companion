# Flutter state management for Hifz Companion: Riverpod (and Bloc) — research note

**Topic:** Flutter state-management patterns evaluated for Hifz Companion — Riverpod vs Bloc/Cubit, their trade-offs, dependency injection, and testability; weighed against the official Flutter app-architecture guidance and the project's hard constraints (offline, no backend/telemetry, RTL, a **pure-Dart deterministic scheduling engine** that must stay framework-free). Survey of official docs (Flutter, Riverpod, Bloc), `pub.dev` package metadata, and credible 2024–2026 community analysis.
**Compiled:** 2026-06-16 · research agent · Scope: official docs (Flutter app-architecture & state-mgmt pages, Riverpod 3.x docs, Bloc README + flutter_bloc API), `pub.dev` package metadata, and 2025–2026 community analysis. Status: primary-source survey; every quotation and URL re-fetched and verified against the source, package versions and adoption counts confirmed on pub.dev (June 2026).

Context from the PRD that constrains every choice below: a single Flutter codebase (iOS + Android), local-first Drift/SQLite, **no network except a one-time checksum-verified asset download**, **no analytics/ads/backend SDKs linkable in the release binary**, three RTL locales (fa/ckb/ar), and — most importantly — a **`/engine` package of pure Dart with zero I/O and zero Flutter imports**, deterministic and golden-tested ([PRD §19.1, §7.12](../../PRD.md)). State management is therefore a *shell* concern: it wires the engine, the Drift DAOs, and the localized RTL UI together. It must never leak into the engine. The PRD already names the decision as "Riverpod (or Bloc) — pick one and keep it consistent" ([PRD §19.1](../../PRD.md)); this note supplies the evidence to pick.

---

## What the evidence says

### 1. The official Flutter direction is architectural (MVVM), not a mandated package

Flutter's own guidance has shifted from "here is a list of options" to a prescriptive **architecture**, while deliberately staying package-agnostic.

- The official **Guide to app architecture** recommends MVVM: *"MVVM is an architectural pattern that separates a feature of an application into three parts: the `Model`, the `ViewModel` and the `View`. Views and view models make up the UI layer of an application. Repositories and services represent the data … the model layer of MVVM."* It splits an app into a **UI layer** (views + view models, one-to-one) and a **data layer** (repositories as the *single source of truth* + services that wrap external sources), with **unidirectional data flow** and an optional domain (use-case) layer for complex logic ([Flutter: Guide to app architecture](https://docs.flutter.dev/app-architecture/guide)).
- Crucially, the guide **does not mandate a state-management package** — it is framework-agnostic and tells you to adapt the principles ([Flutter: Guide to app architecture](https://docs.flutter.dev/app-architecture/guide)).
- The older **Approaches to state management** page is now low-level only: it documents `setState` ("widget-specific, ephemeral state"), `ValueNotifier`/`InheritedNotifier`, and `InheritedWidget`/`InheritedModel` (*"what `package:provider` and many other approaches use under the hood"*), and explicitly defers package choice to the `#state-management` topic on pub.dev, noting *"the best choice … depends on the app's complexity, your team's preferences, and the specific problems you need to solve"* ([Flutter: Approaches to state management](https://docs.flutter.dev/data-and-backend/state-mgmt/options)).

**Takeaway:** the contract Flutter actually cares about — views are dumb projections, business logic lives in view-models/controllers, repositories are the single source of truth, data flows one way — is satisfiable by *either* Riverpod or Bloc. The package is an implementation detail of the UI layer.

### 2. Riverpod is "Provider, redesigned to be compile-safe" — by the same author

Riverpod is written by **Remi Rousselet**, who also authored the original `provider` package; he *"strongly suggests new projects use Riverpod, which is being actively developed, rather than Provider, which is simply in maintenance mode"* ([rrousselGit GitHub](https://github.com/rrousselGit); [Schwartz, on Rousselet's recommendation](https://medium.com/@realmerlyn/remi-the-author-of-both-provider-package-and-riverpod-strongly-suggests-new-projects-use-9c8a18849dae)). The headline property is compile-time safety:

- Because providers are declared as **top-level globals living outside the widget tree**, they are reachable without a `BuildContext`; a missing provider becomes a **compile error, not a runtime `ProviderNotFoundException`**. As Code With Andrea puts it: *"Riverpod is compile-safe since all providers are declared globally and can be accessed anywhere … you can create providers to hold your application state and business logic outside the widget tree,"* whereas Provider *"depends on the widget tree. This is an unfortunate design decision that can lead to the common `ProviderNotFoundException`"* ([Code With Andrea: Riverpod guide](https://codewithandrea.com/articles/flutter-state-management-riverpod/)). The legacy `provider` package, by contrast, does a *runtime lookup*: *"A misconfigured Provider throws `ProviderNotFoundException` at runtime, often only on the screen where it is missing. Riverpod and Bloc both surface that class of mistake before the app runs"* ([Start Debugging: Provider vs Riverpod vs Bloc 2026](https://startdebugging.net/2026/06/provider-vs-riverpod-vs-bloc-for-flutter-state-management-in-2026/)). For a single-developer *ṣadaqah* project with no QA team, eliminating a whole class of "works until you open *that* screen" bugs at compile time is a real safety margin.
- The official package describes itself as *"a reactive caching and data-binding framework"* whose selling points are handling errors/loading states by default, supporting advanced scenarios (pull-to-refresh), **separating logic from UI**, and *"ensuring your code is testable, scalable and reusable"* ([pub.dev: flutter_riverpod](https://pub.dev/packages/flutter_riverpod)).
- The two are close in adoption, with a telling split between *likes* (a long-run reputation signal) and *30-day downloads* (current momentum): `flutter_riverpod` shows **~2.88k likes, ~2.22M downloads, current v3.3.2** vs `flutter_bloc` **~8.04k likes, ~1.57M downloads, current v9.1.1** — likes favor Bloc's longevity, recent download volume favors Riverpod ([pub.dev: flutter_riverpod](https://pub.dev/packages/flutter_riverpod); [pub.dev: flutter_bloc](https://pub.dev/packages/flutter_bloc)). Neither is a niche bet; both are first-tier, actively-maintained packages, so the decision rests on *fit*, not survival risk.

### 3. Riverpod's provider taxonomy maps cleanly onto our shell needs

Riverpod exposes a small set of provider kinds; the relevant ones for an offline app are:

- `Provider` — expose dependencies/objects that don't change (DAOs, the engine, config) — i.e., **dependency injection**.
- `NotifierProvider` / `AsyncNotifierProvider` — synchronous and asynchronous mutable state with built-in loading/error handling; introduced in Riverpod 2.0 as the modern replacement for `StateNotifierProvider` ([Code With Andrea: Riverpod guide](https://codewithandrea.com/articles/flutter-state-management-riverpod/)).
- `FutureProvider` / `StreamProvider` — derive UI state from a `Future`/`Stream` (e.g., **watch a Drift query stream** for "today's revision list") ([Code With Andrea: Riverpod guide](https://codewithandrea.com/articles/flutter-state-management-riverpod/)).

Reading is via three `ref` methods with distinct semantics: `ref.watch()` (observe and rebuild on change — the default in `build`), `ref.read()` (read once, e.g., in a callback), and `ref.listen()` (run a side-effect on change) ([Code With Andrea: Riverpod guide](https://codewithandrea.com/articles/flutter-state-management-riverpod/)). Two modifiers matter for a memory-conscious offline app: **`autoDispose`** disposes a provider "once all listeners are removed (when the widgets are unmounted)," and **`family`** parameterizes a provider (e.g., a per-`profileId` or per-`pageId` provider) ([Code With Andrea: Riverpod guide](https://codewithandrea.com/articles/flutter-state-management-riverpod/)).

### 4. Testability is Riverpod's strongest objective argument — no widget tree, no `BuildContext`

This is the deciding factor for a project whose release gate is property-based and golden tests.

- The official testing guide: create an isolated `ProviderContainer.test()` per test (auto-disposed after the test, no shared state), then `container.read(provider)` / `container.listen(provider, …)`. *"All providers can be mocked by default, without any additional setup."* Any dependency is swapped with `exampleProvider.overrideWith((ref) => …)` (or `overrideWithValue`) — **no mocking of the widget hierarchy** ([Riverpod: Testing your providers](https://riverpod.dev/docs/how_to/testing); [Code With Andrea: Riverpod guide](https://codewithandrea.com/articles/flutter-state-management-riverpod/)).
- The guide's own discipline note is directly relevant to our layering: *"It is generally discouraged to mock Notifiers … Instead, you should likely introduce a level of abstraction in the logic of your Notifier, such that you can mock that abstraction. For instance, rather than mocking a Notifier, you could mock a 'repository' that the Notifier uses to fetch data from"* ([Riverpod: Testing your providers](https://riverpod.dev/docs/how_to/testing)). That is exactly the "controller depends on an injectable repository" shape of the layered Riverpod architecture ([Code With Andrea: Flutter App Architecture with Riverpod](https://codewithandrea.com/articles/flutter-app-architecture-riverpod-introduction/)) — and for us it means a Today-controller test fakes the `cardDao`/engine and asserts the produced UI state, never touching SQLite or the render tree.
- Independent comparisons quantify the ergonomic gap: Riverpod provider tests run *"30–40% fewer lines"* than Bloc's `blocTest` because there are no streams or async event queues to drive, and overrides let you *"override any provider in the tree without mocking the entire widget hierarchy"* ([Flutter Studio: BLoC vs Riverpod 2026](https://flutterstudio.dev/blog/bloc-vs-riverpod.html)).

### 5. Bloc's competing strength is an explicit, traceable event→state contract

Bloc is not a fallback — it is the better fit for a *different* problem profile, and the contrast clarifies our choice.

- The official goal, verbatim from the package: *"The goal of this library is to make it easy to separate **presentation** from **business logic**, facilitating testability and reusability."* Its principles are separation of concerns, testability, and reusability ([bloc GitHub README](https://github.com/felangel/bloc)).
- Bloc is **event-driven and unidirectional by construction**: the UI dispatches events, a Bloc maps each event to a new state via a documented contract, and `BlocObserver` gives a single observability hook for every transition. `bloc_test` provides `blocTest()` — declare the bloc, the events to `act`, and the `expect`ed state sequence ([Flutter Studio: BLoC vs Riverpod 2026](https://flutterstudio.dev/blog/bloc-vs-riverpod.html); [bloc GitHub README](https://github.com/felangel/bloc)). Blocs and Cubits are plain Dart classes, so they test in isolation without the render engine — Bloc's whole stated purpose is to *"make it easy to separate presentation from business logic, facilitating testability and reusability"* ([bloc GitHub README](https://github.com/felangel/bloc)).
- `Cubit` is Bloc's lighter sibling: it *"manages state through direct method calls … by calling `emit()`. No need to define events — only states matter,"* with *"minimal boilerplate and faster prototyping,"* suited to *"toggles, counters, form inputs."* Full `Bloc` *"provides more predictability and traceability, making it better for larger, more complex features,"* and the two *"share the same foundation and can even be mixed within the same app"* ([Medium: Bloc vs Cubit when to use each](https://medium.com/@wassimsakri/bloc-vs-cubit-in-flutter-when-should-you-use-each-5dc21c20c053)).
- The honest community verdict on *when* to prefer Bloc converges across independent 2026 surveys: it *"remains the enterprise standard for large teams — its strict separation of events, states, and business logic makes code predictable and reviewable even when 20+ developers are working on the same codebase"* ([Flutter Studio: BLoC vs Riverpod 2026](https://flutterstudio.dev/blog/bloc-vs-riverpod.html)), and the trade is stated plainly: *"Choose Bloc 9.1.1 when an enforced, event-driven structure and a fully traceable state history are worth more to your team than terseness, which is most often true on large teams and complex flows"* ([Start Debugging: Provider vs Riverpod vs Bloc 2026](https://startdebugging.net/2026/06/provider-vs-riverpod-vs-bloc-for-flutter-state-management-in-2026/)).

The cost is verbosity: even Cubit adds an `emit`-based state class per feature, and full Bloc adds an event hierarchy on top — boilerplate that buys an audit log of state transitions we mostly do not need, because our auditable trail is the **append-only `review_log` in Drift** ([PRD §10.2](../../PRD.md)), not a runtime event stream. And the two structural advantages reviewers cite for Bloc on big teams — guardrails for many developers, and a reviewable event contract — buy little on a **solo *ṣadaqah* codebase** whose discipline is enforced by the pure engine boundary and CI, not by a 20-person review process.

### 6. Dependency injection: Riverpod folds DI and state management into one mechanism

A real architectural difference, not a preference:

- Riverpod *"is a dependency injection framework"* with built-in provider resolution, whereas Bloc *"is not — you need a separate DI solution alongside it"* ([Flutter Studio: BLoC vs Riverpod 2026](https://flutterstudio.dev/blog/bloc-vs-riverpod.html)). In practice: a `Provider` declares the dependency, `ref.watch`/`ref.read` consume it without a `BuildContext`, and `overrideWith` substitutes it in tests ([Riverpod: Testing your providers](https://riverpod.dev/docs/how_to/testing)). Code With Andrea calls Riverpod *"a complete solution for dependency injection and state management"* ([Code With Andrea: comparison of Flutter app architectures](https://codewithandrea.com/articles/comparison-flutter-app-architectures/)).
- Bloc *does* ship a DI mechanism, but only for the two things it knows about: `BlocProvider` injects blocs and `RepositoryProvider` injects repositories down the widget tree, *"so that a single instance of a repository can be provided to multiple widgets within a subtree"* ([flutter_bloc: RepositoryProvider](https://pub.dev/documentation/flutter_bloc/latest/flutter_bloc/RepositoryProvider-class.html)). It is **`BuildContext`-bound** (you read via `context.read`) and does not cover non-bloc/non-repository objects — so teams routinely add the `get_it` service locator alongside Bloc for the rest ([Simform: get_it DI guide](https://medium.com/simform-engineering/mastering-get-it-a-comprehensive-guide-to-dependency-injection-in-flutter-db99bfb305bb)). For us that means either threading DI through `BuildContext` (which our widget-tree-free engine wiring and tests want to avoid) or pulling in **a second dependency** to vet against the no-extra-SDK gate ([PRD §20, gate 6](../../PRD.md)). Riverpod's single mechanism makes both unnecessary.
- The same comparison notes the layering implication: Riverpod draws *"a clearer distinction between controllers (which manage the widget state) and services (which manage the application state),"* whereas in Bloc *"blocs can depend on other blocs in order to react to their state changes"* ([Code With Andrea: comparison of Flutter app architectures](https://codewithandrea.com/articles/comparison-flutter-app-architectures/)).

### 7. Riverpod 3.0 (Sept 2025) is a meaningful, mostly-favorable change — with sharp edges

Riverpod 3.0 shipped **2025-09-10** as a unified package with new capabilities, several of which intersect our constraints ([Riverpod: What's new in 3.0](https://riverpod.dev/docs/whats_new); [Code With Andrea newsletter, Sept 2025](https://codewithandrea.com/newsletter/september-2025/)):

- **Automatic retry** with exponential backoff for failing providers — useful for the one-time asset download, but it must be *bounded/disabled* for anything that could mask a checksum failure (R1 forbids rendering unverified Quran assets) ([Riverpod: What's new in 3.0](https://riverpod.dev/docs/whats_new); [PRD §11.1.1](../../PRD.md)).
- **Automatic pause/resume**: provider listeners pause when their widget leaves the screen (via `TickerMode`) and resume on return — a free performance win for the heat-map and reader ([Riverpod: What's new in 3.0](https://riverpod.dev/docs/whats_new)).
- **`Ref.mounted`** — guard against state updates after async disposal, analogous to `BuildContext.mounted` ([Riverpod: What's new in 3.0](https://riverpod.dev/docs/whats_new)).
- **Experimental offline persistence and mutations** — Riverpod ships only the *interfaces* to a database, not a database; *"It does not include a database itself."* We already own persistence via Drift, so this experimental layer is **not needed and not stable enough to adopt** ([Riverpod: What's new in 3.0](https://riverpod.dev/docs/whats_new)).
- **Breaking changes to internalize before locking a version**: `StateProvider`, `StateNotifierProvider`, and `ChangeNotifierProvider` are now **legacy**, moved to a separate `legacy.dart` import; `valueOrNull`'s behaviour is now assigned to the name `value` (the old `.value` was *"removed, as its behavior related to errors was odd"*); and a subtler trap — because Riverpod 3 **pauses providers that nothing is watching/listening**, calling `ref.read()` on an un-watched provider can *"do nothing"* unless you switch to `watch`, set `keepAlive: true`, or read it asynchronously ([Riverpod: What's new in 3.0](https://riverpod.dev/docs/whats_new); [Zenn: Migration guide to Riverpod 3](https://zenn.dev/harx/articles/d8c49cdec0ab1d?locale=en)). New code should target the modern `Notifier`/`AsyncNotifier` API, avoid the legacy providers entirely, and prefer `watch` over fire-and-forget `read`.

### 8. The honest criticisms of Riverpod are real and must be designed against

The strongest dissent comes from Michael Lazebny's critique, and it is worth taking seriously precisely because our app values auditability ([Lazebny: Riverpod's flaws](https://lazebny.io/riverpod/)):

- **Global scope / service-locator feel:** *"Each provider stores its state in the root, regardless of whether that provider is used in the root or somewhere very deep in the application"* — the global declaration that buys compile-safety also tempts "read anything from anywhere" coding ([Lazebny: Riverpod's flaws](https://lazebny.io/riverpod/)).
- **Implicit coupling & SRP erosion:** providers that `watch` other providers create dependency webs that *"cascade unpredictably,"* and *"instead of testing a single provider, we end up evaluating an entire network of interconnected providers"* ([Lazebny: Riverpod's flaws](https://lazebny.io/riverpod/)).
- **"Magic":** automatic caching, disposal, and inter-provider reactions can introduce *"unpredictability or opacity into the flow of the application"* ([Lazebny: Riverpod's flaws](https://lazebny.io/riverpod/)).
- Community comparisons echo the friction cost: code generation *"adds a `build_runner` step to your workflow, and the generated files can produce noisier diffs in code review,"* and Riverpod *"introduces `ref`, code generation, and a new mental model that takes a week to feel natural"* ([Bacancy: Bloc vs Riverpod vs Provider 2026](https://www.bacancytechnology.com/blog/flutter-bloc-vs-riverpod-vs-provider)).

The mitigation is the layered architecture, not a different package: confine providers to controllers and repositories, keep business logic in the pure engine and in plain services, and never let a widget reach past its controller.

### 9. Code generation is optional and conditional — not a default for us

Riverpod's `@riverpod` annotation (via `riverpod_generator` + `build_runner`) reduces provider boilerplate and gives better syntax plus parameter passing without the `family` modifier ([Riverpod: About code generation](https://riverpod.dev/docs/concepts/about_code_generation); [Code With Andrea: Riverpod guide](https://codewithandrea.com/articles/flutter-state-management-riverpod/)). The official stance is explicitly conditional: *"When using Riverpod, code generation is completely optional. It is entirely possible to use Riverpod without"*; it *"currently is still fairly slow"*; and the recommendation is to adopt it *"only if you already use code-generation for other things"* — bluntly, *"if you are not already using code generation in your project, it is probably not worth it to start using it just for Riverpod"* ([Riverpod: About code generation](https://riverpod.dev/docs/concepts/about_code_generation)). Since the project already runs `build_runner` for **Drift** ([PRD §10](../../PRD.md)), the precondition is *already met*, so adding `riverpod_generator` is a low-marginal-cost, defensible (not mandatory) choice — and whichever way it is decided, it must be applied uniformly, because a half-generated/half-hand-written provider set is the worst of both.

## Implications for Hifz Companion

1. **Adopt Riverpod 3.x as the single shell state-management + DI solution.** It satisfies the official MVVM contract ([Flutter: Guide to app architecture](https://docs.flutter.dev/app-architecture/guide)), gives the best widget-tree-free testability for our test-gated release ([Riverpod: Testing your providers](https://riverpod.dev/docs/how_to/testing)), and folds dependency injection into the same mechanism so we add **zero extra DI library** (no `get_it`, no `BuildContext`-bound lookups), keeping the no-extra-SDK gate clean ([PRD §20, gate 6](../../PRD.md); [Code With Andrea: comparison of Flutter app architectures](https://codewithandrea.com/articles/comparison-flutter-app-architectures/)). Bloc is the credible runner-up but its event-stream verbosity and need for a companion DI library buy a runtime audit trail we already get from the append-only `review_log`.

2. **The pure-Dart `/engine` package must import nothing from Riverpod.** The scheduler stays a framework-free, deterministic, golden-tested package ([PRD §7.12, §19.1](../../PRD.md)); Riverpod lives only in the shell, where a `Provider` *injects* the engine into controllers. This is the "functional core, imperative shell" split the architecture guide encodes as model/data layer vs UI layer ([Flutter: Guide to app architecture](https://docs.flutter.dev/app-architecture/guide)).

3. **Map the modules to layers explicitly** (mirroring the layered Riverpod architecture, [Code With Andrea](https://codewithandrea.com/articles/flutter-app-architecture-riverpod-introduction/)):
   - **Data:** Drift DAOs + the reference/asset loaders, each exposed through a `Provider` (e.g., `cardDaoProvider`, `reviewLogDaoProvider`). Repositories are the single source of truth ([Flutter: Guide to app architecture](https://docs.flutter.dev/app-architecture/guide)).
   - **Application/engine:** the pure scheduler, injected via `Provider`; plain service classes for cross-DAO logic (e.g., building "today").
   - **Presentation:** one `AsyncNotifier`/`Notifier` controller per feature (Today, Mušḥaf reader, Mutashābihāt, Progress, Onboarding, Settings), exposing immutable UI state; widgets are dumb projections that `ref.watch` it.

4. **Standardize on the modern API; ban the legacy providers.** Use `Notifier`/`AsyncNotifier` and `Future/StreamProvider`; do **not** import `flutter_riverpod/legacy.dart` (`StateProvider`, `StateNotifierProvider`, `ChangeNotifierProvider`) ([Riverpod: What's new in 3.0](https://riverpod.dev/docs/whats_new)). Add a lint/CI grep forbidding that import so a single convention holds across the codebase ([PRD §19.1 "pick one and keep it consistent"](../../PRD.md)).

5. **Stream Drift queries through `StreamProvider`** so the Today list, heat-map, and per-juz health recompute reactively when a review is logged — `card.R` roll-ups are derived, not stored ([PRD §10.3](../../PRD.md)), which fits Riverpod's "reactive caching" model exactly ([pub.dev: flutter_riverpod](https://pub.dev/packages/flutter_riverpod)).

6. **Use `family` + `autoDispose` for per-profile and per-page state.** Multi-profile (teacher/halaqa, child) means provider state must be keyed by `profileId`; the heavy reader/page providers should `autoDispose` when their screen unmounts to bound memory on low-end Android ([Code With Andrea: Riverpod guide](https://codewithandrea.com/articles/flutter-state-management-riverpod/); [PRD §15.3](../../PRD.md)).

7. **Design against the global-scope critique, structurally.** Enforce the rule "widgets read only their feature's controller; controllers read repositories; nothing reaches across features" ([Lazebny: Riverpod's flaws](https://lazebny.io/riverpod/)). Keep business logic in the engine and services, not inside providers, so a provider stays a thin wire — this directly answers the SRP/coupling and "magic" objections.

8. **Bound `autoRetry`; never let it mask an integrity failure.** Riverpod 3.0 auto-retries failing providers ([Riverpod: What's new in 3.0](https://riverpod.dev/docs/whats_new)); for the asset-download provider, configure a finite retry and surface a clear "needs one-time download / retry" state, and **disable retry** on any provider whose failure means a checksum mismatch — the app must *refuse* to render unverified Quran assets, not silently retry ([PRD §11.1.1, R1](../../PRD.md)).

9. **Do not adopt Riverpod's experimental offline-persistence/mutations layer.** Persistence is owned by Drift/SQLite with transactional writes ([PRD §10, §19.1](../../PRD.md)); the experimental Riverpod cache ships no database and is not release-grade ([Riverpod: What's new in 3.0](https://riverpod.dev/docs/whats_new)).

10. **Testing strategy: lean on `ProviderContainer.test()` + `overrideWith`.** Test each controller by overriding its repository/DAO with a fake — no widget pumping, mock the abstraction not the Notifier ([Riverpod: Testing your providers](https://riverpod.dev/docs/how_to/testing)). The pure engine keeps its own golden tests; controller tests verify only the wiring and UI-state mapping. This satisfies the release gates for engine golden tests and feature/widget tests at low ceremony ([PRD §20](../../PRD.md)).

11. **Code generation is optional, and reasonable here.** Because Drift already pulls in `build_runner`, adding `riverpod_generator` for the `@riverpod` syntax is low marginal cost and reduces provider boilerplate ([Riverpod: About code generation](https://riverpod.dev/docs/concepts/about_code_generation); [PRD §10](../../PRD.md)). Decide once and apply uniformly; a mixed hand-written/generated codebase is the worst outcome.

12. **Record the decision in the engineering decision log** as: *State management — Riverpod 3.x (modern `Notifier`/`AsyncNotifier` API), no `get_it`, no Bloc; engine stays Riverpod-free.* The synthesis doc `04-flutter-and-state-patterns.md` should cite this note and the decision-log entry.
