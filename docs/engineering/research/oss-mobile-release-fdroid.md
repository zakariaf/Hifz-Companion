# OSS Mobile Release, Licensing & F-Droid Distribution — Research Note

**Topic:** Open-sourcing and releasing a free (*ṣadaqah jāriyah*) mobile app: license choice (GPL / AGPL / MIT / MPL) for a charitable, fully-offline project; distributing through F-Droid alongside Google Play and the Apple App Store; the documented GPL-vs-App-Store conflict and its cure; privacy declarations (Apple privacy nutrition labels + `PrivacyInfo.xcprivacy` manifests, Google Play Data safety); and the auditability story an offline, no-AI, no-telemetry app can honestly tell.

**Compiled:** 2026-06-16

This note is the evidence dossier behind the engineering synthesis doc *13-oss-repo-and-release*, and behind PRD §17 (Privacy & Security), §19.3 (offline guarantees), §20.6 (network-restraint release gate), and Open-Decision §21.6 ("Open-source license intent"). It complements the sibling note *asset-integrity-and-distribution.md*, which covers the **data** repo (Quran asset packs); this note covers the **app code** repo and the three stores it ships through. Two facts shape everything below. First, the app is built **free, as *ṣadaqah jāriyah*** — there is no monetization to protect, so the license question is purely about *trust, auditability, and freedom*, not revenue. Second, the app is **fully offline, account-free, telemetry-free, and contains no proprietary SDKs** — which, as the evidence shows, removes nearly the entire compliance surface that normally makes store releases painful, and qualifies the app for the strongest privacy posture every store offers.

---

## What the evidence says

### 1. F-Droid requires *everything* be FLOSS — the whole app, every dependency, and a 100% free build toolchain

F-Droid is the canonical free-software Android store, and its bar is the strictest of the three channels — clearing it effectively guarantees the other two. Its Inclusion Policy states that all applications "must be Free, Libre and Open Source Software (FLOSS) – for example, released under a GPL or Apache license," and — critically — that "for software to be FLOSS, the software in its entirety must be so - including all libraries and dependencies used, and it must be buildable with only FLOSS tools" ([F-Droid: Inclusion Policy](https://f-droid.org/en/docs/Inclusion_Policy/)). The build constraint is concrete: "The complete application building process requires a 100% FLOSS toolchain including Debian-packaged tools. The use of proprietary build tools are strictly forbidden, including Oracle's JDK," and prebuilt binaries are accepted only from trusted sources (Debian main, Maven Central, Google Maven, OSS Sonatype, JitPack, and named SDKs including **Flutter**) ([F-Droid: Inclusion Policy](https://f-droid.org/en/docs/Inclusion_Policy/)). For license validity, F-Droid "defers to widely trusted organizations to determine which licenses are FLOSS, specifically acknowledging DFSG, FSF, GNU, and OSI standards" ([F-Droid: Inclusion Policy](https://f-droid.org/en/docs/Inclusion_Policy/)). The takeaway for Hifz Companion: GPL-3.0, AGPL-3.0, MPL-2.0, Apache-2.0, and MIT are all explicitly acceptable; the gate is not *which* free license but that the *entire dependency tree and toolchain* is free.

### 2. F-Droid bans exactly the SDKs the PRD already forbids — so the offline/no-telemetry constraint *is* the F-Droid entry ticket

F-Droid's prohibition list reads like a restatement of PRD C1 and §17: "The implementation of proprietary tracking or advertising libraries and analytics tools such as Google Play Services and Firebase and Crashlytics and proprietary ad/tracking SDKs are strictly forbidden in all applications" ([F-Droid: Inclusion Policy](https://f-droid.org/en/docs/Inclusion_Policy/)). PRD §19.3 already mandates a build-time assertion that "no analytics, ads, or backend SDKs are linked." This is a happy coincidence of values: the same constraints that make the app trustworthy to huffaz (no telemetry, works in airplane mode) make it F-Droid-eligible by construction. The app does not need to *strip anything* to qualify — it must merely *stay clean*, which is already a release gate (§20.6).

### 3. F-Droid's Anti-Features are the warning labels we must avoid earning — and our architecture earns none of them

F-Droid still admits some FLOSS apps that have undesirable traits, flagging them with **Anti-Features** "which serve as warning indicators about user freedom, privacy or etc. without necessarily disqualifying applications from inclusion" ([F-Droid: Inclusion Policy](https://f-droid.org/en/docs/Inclusion_Policy/)). The relevant flags and their exact definitions: **NonFreeNet** — "apps that promote or depend entirely on a proprietary network service"; **NonFreeDep** — "apps that require things that are not Free Software in order to run"; **NonFreeAssets** — "apps that contain and make use of Non-Free assets … artwork - images, sounds, music, etc. - under a license that restricts commercial usage or making derivative works"; **Tracking** — "apps that track you and/or report your activity to somewhere"; **Ads** — "an app that contains advertising"; and **NonFreeAdd** — apps that "promote other Non-Free applications" ([F-Droid: Anti-Features](https://f-droid.org/en/docs/Anti-Features/)). Tracking-flagged apps are even **hidden by default** in the client ([F-Droid: Anti-Features](https://f-droid.org/en/docs/Anti-Features/)). Two of these are live design risks for us: **NonFreeNet** (the one-time GitHub asset download is to a public service, not a *dependency on a proprietary service to run* — and the app works fully offline afterward, so it does not qualify), and **NonFreeAssets** (the KFGQPC QPC glyph fonts and reciter audio must be under licenses that permit redistribution and derivative works, or the build inherits a NonFreeAssets flag — a concrete reason to verify font terms per PRD §11.1 and the sibling asset note).

### 4. F-Droid can ship the *developer's own signed binary* when the build is reproducible — turning "trust F-Droid" into "verify the code yourself"

F-Droid is explicit that "Reproducible builds are not a requirement for apps being on F-Droid. But we do consider their use best practice" ([F-Droid: Inclusion How-To](https://f-droid.org/docs/Inclusion_How-To/)). Their payoff is signature provenance: F-Droid normally signs APKs with *its own* key, but with reproducible builds "F-Droid ships APKs that are signed by the upstream developer(s)," and on fetching a new APK F-Droid "confirms, too, that this is the APK built from the very code the developer provided" — if a fetched APK "was not [signed with the key of its author], it will never be included in the repository's index (so it will not be shipped to you and thus cannot endanger you)" ([F-Droid: Reproducible builds, signing keys, and binary repos](https://f-droid.org/2023/09/03/reproducible-builds-signing-keys-and-binary-repos.html)). The two-party guarantee is the whole point: the developer's signature attests "this is what I intended to publish," while F-Droid's independent rebuild attests the binary was built from exactly the published source. Android (unlike iOS) is genuinely reproducible, so this is achievable for a Flutter app and is the strongest auditability claim available on any platform — an auditor can independently rebuild the exact bytes from source.

### 5. The GPL/AGPL-vs-App-Store conflict is real, narrow, and has a documented cure — and the App Store does not check your license

The folklore that "you can't put GPL apps on the App Store" overstates a narrow legal mechanic. The conflict is between a distribution channel's terms (which impose restrictions on recipients) and the GPL's "no further restrictions" principle — the GPL's "primary objective" is "unwaivable" because "users' freedoms cannot be protected if parties can add restrictive terms to the copyleft," and GPLv3 §7 enumerates the only permissible additional terms ([SFLC: Guide to GPL Compliance, 2nd ed.](https://softwarefreedom.org/resources/2014/SFLC-Guide_to_GPL_Compliance_2d_ed.html)). The doctrinal origin is the FSF's 2010 GNU Go enforcement action, which named the conflict precisely: "Apple imposes numerous legal restrictions on use and distribution of GNU Go through the iTunes Store Terms of Service, which is forbidden by section 6 of GPLv2" ([FSF: GPL Enforcement in Apple's App Store](https://www.fsf.org/news/2010-05-app-store-compliance)). The cure is an **App Store exception** (an additional permission GPLv3 §7 expressly allows); a real Flutter precedent spells it out: "you are allowed to distribute the software through an app store, even if that store has restrictive terms and conditions that are incompatible with the [GPL/AGPL], provided that the source is also available … through a channel without those restrictive terms and conditions" ([wger: Add app store exception to license, Issue #10](https://github.com/wger-project/flutter/issues/10)). The canonical precedent is Signal's June 2016 relicensing of `libsignal-protocol-c`: "Applications that comply with the GPL are now explicitly authorized to be distributed through the Apple App Store and remain in compliance with the license" ([Signal: License update](https://signal.org/blog/license-update/)). Two facts make the App Store risk manageable: only a *copyright holder* has standing to object (a sole rights-holder cannot sue themselves), and **App Review itself does not inspect or care about your OSS license** — the risk is downstream copyright enforcement, entered only through (a) third-party GPL code you vendor or (b) outside contributors who become co-copyright-holders.

### 6. For a sadaqah project the real license trade-off is *freedom-preservation vs. permissiveness*, and existing Quran apps split exactly along that line

Because there is no revenue to protect, the license decision is about whether to *guarantee the work stays free* (copyleft) or *maximize reuse* (permissive). The existing free Quran-app ecosystem demonstrates both choices, verified live via the GitHub API on 2026-06-16:

| Project | License (verified GitHub API, 2026-06-16) | Stance |
|---|---|---|
| **quran/quran_android** | **GPL-3.0** ([quran/quran_android](https://github.com/quran/quran_android)) | Copyleft: "modifications to this code [must] be open sourced as well" |
| **MahmoudMabrok/QuranyApp** | **Apache-2.0** ([QuranyApp](https://github.com/MahmoudMabrok/QuranyApp)) | Permissive |
| **QuranMajeed** (Haseeb Iqbal) | MIT stated in README; no SPDX license file detected via API ([muhammadhaseebiqbal-dev/QuranMajeed](https://github.com/muhammadhaseebiqbal-dev/QuranMajeed)) | Permissive; framed as a source of *Sadaqah Jariyah* |
| **Quranic Arabic Corpus** | GNU (GPL) ([corpus.quran.com license](https://corpus.quran.com/license.jsp)) | Copyleft |

The charitable framing is itself a community convention: **Open Waqf** is "Building Digital Waqf for the modern age" — "Privacy-first, ad-free Islamic apps designed for the global Ummah," with "Zero tracking. No data selling," "No 'premium' paywalls or hidden costs," and "Auditable code. Community owned. Built as Sadaqah Jariyah for everyone," invoking *waqf* (perpetual endowment) for the code itself ([Open Waqf](https://open-waqf.org/)). The argument for **copyleft (GPL-3.0)** over permissive for a *ṣadaqah* project is direct: a *waqf* is meant to remain a perpetual public benefit, and GPL-3.0 structurally prevents a third party from taking the work proprietary, paywalling it, or bolting ads/telemetry onto a closed fork — i.e. copyleft encodes the *ṣadaqah-jāriyah* intent in law. Permissive (MIT/Apache-2.0) maximizes adoption but permits exactly the closed, monetized derivative the project's values reject.

### 7. AGPL-3.0 buys this app nothing over GPL-3.0 — its only distinguishing clause is a no-op offline

AGPL-3.0 differs from GPL-3.0 in exactly one place, §13, which "effectively define[s] network deployment of software as a form of distribution that invokes copyleft license conditions" — designed "to close a perceived loophole in the ordinary GPL in environments where users make use of functionality provided as a web service" ([AboutCode: GPL 3.0 compliance](https://aboutcode.org/2021/gpl-3-0-license-compliance/)). Crucially, "running unmodified AGPL software over a network doesn't automatically trigger the disclosure requirement — the modifications must have been made by the party providing the network service" ([AboutCode: GPL 3.0 compliance](https://aboutcode.org/2021/gpl-3-0-license-compliance/)). Hifz Companion has **no network service** — it is fully offline (PRD C1), with no server anyone interacts with remotely. §13 therefore never fires. AGPL adds the same App-Store friction as GPL while delivering no extra protection for an offline app, so GPL-3.0 is the better-fitting copyleft choice.

### 8. Distributing on the App Store as a *free* app is fully permitted — and IAP rules are irrelevant because nothing is sold

Apple's guidelines do not require pricing; multiple categories are explicitly allowed to be free, and the only metadata duty is that "if your business model isn't obvious, make sure to explain in its metadata and App Review notes" ([Apple: App Review Guidelines](https://developer.apple.com/app-store/review/guidelines/)). Guideline **3.1.1** forces in-app purchase *only when you "unlock features or functionality"* — "subscriptions, in-game currencies, game levels, access to premium content, or unlocking a full version" ([Apple: App Review Guidelines](https://developer.apple.com/app-store/review/guidelines/)); a fully-free app with no locked content never touches it. Guideline **2.5.2** (no downloading/executing code that "introduces or changes features or functionality") is satisfied by design: the one-time asset download is *static data* (Quran text, fonts, datasets), not executable code, and the scheduling engine is plain compiled Dart arithmetic — no remote code, no interpreter, no AI model (PRD C2). Guideline **5.1.1(v)** rewards the account-free design: "If your app doesn't include significant account-based features, let people use it without a login … Apps may not require users to enter personal information to function" ([Apple: App Review Guidelines](https://developer.apple.com/app-store/review/guidelines/)) — the app has no accounts at all, so the account-deletion duty never arises.

### 9. Every store still demands a privacy declaration — even "we collect nothing" must be *stated*, and our architecture lets us state the strongest version truthfully

Zero collection is not zero paperwork. On Apple's side, a **privacy policy is mandatory for all apps**: "All apps must include a link to their privacy policy in the App Store Connect metadata field and within the app in an easily accessible manner" (Guideline 5.1.1(i)) ([Apple: App Review Guidelines](https://developer.apple.com/app-store/review/guidelines/)). For the **privacy nutrition label**, Apple's controlling definition is generous to local-first apps: "'Collect' refers to transmitting data off the device in a way that allows you and/or your third-party partners to access it … Data that is processed only on device is not 'collected' and does not need to be disclosed in your answers" ([Apple: App Privacy Details](https://developer.apple.com/app-store/app-privacy-details/)). An offline app that never transmits qualifies truthfully for **"Data Not Collected."** On Google's side the same logic applies but the form is unskippable: "If your app doesn't collect data, you still declare 'no data collected' — there is no way to skip the section" ([Google Play: Data safety](https://support.google.com/googleplay/android-developer/answer/10787469)). Both stores put the burden of accuracy on the developer — Apple: "You're responsible for keeping your responses accurate and up to date" ([Apple: App Privacy Details](https://developer.apple.com/app-store/app-privacy-details/)). The open repo is what upgrades the declaration from *aspirational* to *auditable*: anyone can read the source and confirm the single network code path.

### 10. Google Play now runs *automated binary checks* against your declaration — a clean app passes, a hidden SDK gets flagged

A 2025 Play policy update added enforcement teeth: "Play Console now runs automated checks against your APK/AAB before you can submit. If your binary accesses data types you didn't declare, you'll get flagged before human review," and Android ID is now explicitly a declarable device identifier ([Respectlytics: Google Play Data Safety guide](https://respectlytics.com/blog/google-play-data-safety-guide/)). The Data safety obligation explicitly "includes data collected and handled through any third-party libraries or SDKs used in their apps" ([Google Play: Data safety](https://support.google.com/googleplay/android-developer/answer/10787469)). For a no-SDK, no-telemetry app this automated scan is an *ally*: it will find nothing to flag, which is itself corroborating evidence for the "Data Not Collected" claim — but it also means a careless future dependency that quietly reads a device ID would be caught and could block a release, reinforcing the §20.6 dependency-minimalism gate.

### 11. Apple privacy manifests (`PrivacyInfo.xcprivacy`) are mandatory and now well-supported in Flutter — required-reason APIs must be declared

Since spring 2024, Apple requires every app (and each "commonly used" third-party SDK) to ship a privacy manifest declaring tracking, collected data types, and **required-reason API** usage with approved reason codes: "Starting in spring 2024, in order to upload your new app or app update to App Store Connect, you'll be required to include an approved reason in the app's privacy manifest which accurately reflects how your app uses the API" ([Apple via Sentry: Apple Privacy Manifest for Flutter](https://docs.sentry.io/platforms/dart/guides/flutter/data-management/apple-privacy-manifest/)). Flutter now supports this natively: `flutter create --template plugin` adds "a blank xcprivacy … correctly hooked up in the template podspec," and plugins declare it via `s.resource_bundles = {'package_name_privacy' => ['Resources/PrivacyInfo.xcprivacy']}` ([flutter/flutter Issue #140013](https://github.com/flutter/flutter/issues/140013); [freeCodeCamp: Flutter package privacy manifest](https://www.freecodecamp.org/news/how-to-make-your-flutter-package-privacy-manifest-compatible/)). Realistically the app will touch at least the **UserDefaults** required-reason category (settings) and possibly **file timestamp / disk space** (handling the downloaded packs and backup files), so an app-level manifest with correct reason codes — `NSPrivacyTracking = false`, empty `NSPrivacyCollectedDataTypes` — is required, not optional, and publishing it in the open repo makes a compliance artifact double as an auditable trust artifact.

### 12. The Android distribution landscape is shifting under a 2025 Google "developer registration decree" — a real threat to free, open distribution that this project should track

A free, open-source app's natural home is sideloading and F-Droid, but Google's August 2025 policy directly threatens that path. F-Droid warns: "If it were to be put into effect, the developer registration decree will end the F-Droid project and other free/open-source app distribution sources as we know them today" ([F-Droid: Google's Developer Registration Decree](https://f-droid.org/en/2025/09/29/google-developer-registration-decree.html)). The decree requires all Android developers to register centrally with Google, pay fees, complete identity verification "including government ID," and enumerate "all the unique application identifiers" they distribute — which F-Droid cannot satisfy without seizing "exclusive distribution rights" to the apps it hosts ([F-Droid: Google's Developer Registration Decree](https://f-droid.org/en/2025/09/29/google-developer-registration-decree.html)). Independent of this, alternative open channels exist and are worth supporting directly: **Obtainium** installs and updates apps straight from their "releases pages" (e.g. GitHub Releases) and "receive[s] notifications when new releases are made available," and **Accrescent** is a security-focused store positioned as "a more secure alternative than F-Droid" ([AlternativeTo: Accrescent / Obtainium](https://www.alternativeto.net/software/accrescent/)). For Hifz Companion these matter because they let a privacy-respecting *ṣadaqah* app reach users via signed GitHub Releases even if F-Droid or Play access narrows.

### 13. Store registration is cheap and one-time — the cost of releasing is overwhelmingly *engineering discipline*, not money or fees

For a free charitable project the monetary barrier is negligible. Google Play charges "a one-time $25 USD payment — there is no annual renewal" ([Google Play Console: Get started](https://support.google.com/googleplay/android-developer/answer/6112435); [iconikai: Play fee 2026](https://www.iconikai.com/blog/google-play-developer-account-fee-2026)); F-Droid is free to publish to (the project absorbs build and hosting); Apple's Developer Program is its standard annual fee. The expensive parts are non-monetary and are the parts this note's other findings address: keeping the dependency tree FLOSS and SDK-free (Findings 1–3), maintaining reproducible builds (Finding 4), placing the App Store exception before merging any outside PR (Finding 5), writing accurate privacy declarations and manifests on three platforms (Findings 9–11), and tracking a hostile distribution-policy environment (Finding 12).

## Implications for Hifz Companion

1. **License the app code GPL-3.0-or-later, with an explicit App Store additional permission, and accept contributions under a DCO.** Copyleft encodes the *ṣadaqah-jāriyah* / *waqf* intent in law — it keeps the work perpetually free and forecloses a closed, paywalled, or telemetry-laden fork (Finding 6), matching the precedent of `quran/quran_android` and the Quranic Arabic Corpus. The §7 additional permission (modeled on Signal's 2016 grant — "GPL-compliant applications are explicitly authorized to be distributed through the Apple App Store") closes the only documented App-Store legal hole (Finding 5). A DCO (not a CLA) lets outside contributions arrive under the project license *including the exception*, without the suspicion CLAs draw in this community. **Avoid AGPL** — its only distinguishing clause is dead for an offline app (Finding 7).

2. **Treat the existing offline / no-SDK constraints as the F-Droid entry ticket, and make F-Droid eligibility a release gate.** The PRD's ban on analytics/ads/backend SDKs (C1, §19.3) is verbatim F-Droid's prohibition list (Finding 2). Clearing F-Droid's FLOSS-everything bar (Finding 1) effectively guarantees Play and App Store acceptance too, so target F-Droid first and keep the whole dependency tree and build toolchain free.

3. **Do not earn any Anti-Feature — especially NonFreeAssets and NonFreeNet.** Verify the KFGQPC QPC font and any reciter-audio licenses *permit redistribution and derivative works*; a restrictive font license would tag the build **NonFreeAssets** (Finding 3, and the asset-integrity note). Frame the one-time GitHub asset download as fetching *static public data the app then runs without* — the app works fully offline forever (PRD C1), so it does not "depend entirely on a proprietary network service" and avoids **NonFreeNet**.

4. **Ship reproducible Android builds signed with our own key.** This is achievable for Flutter on Android and is the single strongest auditability claim available (Finding 4): F-Droid then distributes *our* signed APK and any auditor can rebuild the exact bytes from source. Pair this with the reproducible *asset-pack* pipeline from the sibling note so both code and data are independently re-derivable — the concrete meaning of the engineering pillar "open-source & auditable."

5. **Declare the strongest truthful privacy posture on every store, and gate it.** Apple: **"Data Not Collected"** nutrition label (truthful — nothing transmits off device; on-device processing is not "collection" — Finding 9) plus the still-mandatory in-app + App Store Connect **privacy policy** (5.1.1(i)). Google Play: **"no data collected"** in the unskippable Data safety form (Finding 9). Make "label/declaration stays at zero collection" a release-checklist gate — any future feature that transmits anything (even an opt-in diagnostic) breaks it (ties to PRD §20.6).

6. **Ship an app-level `PrivacyInfo.xcprivacy` with `NSPrivacyTracking=false`, empty collected-data types, and accurate required-reason codes; audit plugins for their own manifests.** It is mandatory since spring 2024 and now first-class in Flutter (Finding 11). Expect at minimum the UserDefaults reason category, and audit the asset-download and backup code for file-timestamp/disk-space use. Publish the manifest in the open repo so the compliance file doubles as a public, auditable trust artifact.

7. **Lean into Google Play's automated binary scan as corroboration, and keep dependencies minimal.** The 2025 pre-submission scan (Finding 10) will find nothing to flag in a clean app — that is positive evidence for the privacy claim. Conversely it will catch a careless future dependency that reads a device ID, so commit a written dependency-minimalism policy and a lockfile, and justify every new dependency against the FLOSS + zero-collection rules.

8. **Publish signed GitHub Releases and support Obtainium / Accrescent as first-class channels; track the Google registration decree.** A *ṣadaqah* app should maximize reach and resilience (Finding 12): signed GitHub Releases let Obtainium users install and auto-update directly from source, independent of any store's gatekeeping, and Accrescent offers a hardened path. Monitor the 2025 developer-registration decree, since it could constrict both F-Droid and sideloading — the multi-channel posture (Play + App Store + F-Droid + GitHub Releases/Obtainium) is the hedge.

9. **Keep the repo's trust pack complete: LICENSE (GPL-3.0 + App Store exception), CONTRIBUTING + DCO, SECURITY.md, a "verify us yourself" doc.** The "verify us yourself" doc should walk a reader through reproducible-build verification on Android (Finding 4), the empty Data-safety / Data-Not-Collected declarations (Finding 9), and airplane-mode operation (PRD C1) — converting the offline, no-AI design into a *checkable* claim rather than a promise. Never merge an outside PR before the App Store exception text is in `LICENSE` (Finding 5) — that is the literal VLC/GNU-Go failure mode.

10. **Extend store trust exactly once — at install — then never phone home.** The release story mirrors the asset story: the user trusts a signed binary from a store (or a signed GitHub Release) once; from then on the app extends *zero* further trust to any network or remote-config service. There is no IAP, no account, no telemetry, no AI endpoint — so the App Store's IAP/account rules (Finding 8) and the stores' collection-disclosure machinery (Findings 9–11) reduce to their simplest, most honest answers, which is precisely the trust advantage a free, offline *ṣadaqah* app holds over every commercial competitor.
