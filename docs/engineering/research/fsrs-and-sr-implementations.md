# FSRS in Practice — the Open Spec, Parameters, and a Deterministic Scheduler — Research Note

**Topic:** The Free Spaced Repetition Scheduler (FSRS) as a *buildable* artifact for Hifz Companion: the open DSR model and its formulas, the published default parameters, the reference implementations across languages (py-fsrs, ts-fsrs, rs-fsrs / fsrs-rs, go-fsrs, dart-fsrs, fsrs-rs-dart), and exactly how to implement a **deterministic, I/O-free, golden-testable** page scheduler in pure Dart that obeys the PRD's trust-clamp contract.
**Compiled:** 2026-06-16 · **Status:** Every implementation, version number, formula, license, and default-parameter vector below was verified against its primary source (the open-spaced-repetition GitHub org, pub.dev, the KDD 2022 paper, and Expertium's algorithm write-up). Claims that could not be pinned to a primary source were dropped or marked as an assumption.

This note is the evidence dossier behind engineering decision **§06 (Scheduling engine)**. It deliberately stays at the level of *what the algorithm actually is and how to ship it deterministically*; the product-shaping (page-as-card, three tracks, trust clamp, cold start) lives in `PRD.md §7` and the synthesis doc `06-scheduling-engine.md`, and the *memory-science* justification lives in `science/03-spaced-repetition-algorithms`.

## What the evidence says

### 1. FSRS is an open, MIT-licensed family of implementations, not a single library

1.1 **FSRS is a community ("Open Spaced Repetition") that maintains a canonical algorithm spec plus parallel implementations in many languages, all permissively licensed.** The umbrella repo `free-spaced-repetition-scheduler` describes FSRS as "a spaced repetition algorithm based on [the] DSR model" and is **MIT-licensed**; it links reference implementations in Python, TypeScript, Rust, Go, Dart, Ruby, Swift, Clojure, Elixir, Kotlin and more ([open-spaced-repetition/free-spaced-repetition-scheduler](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/free-spaced-repetition-scheduler)). The curated `awesome-fsrs` index is the authoritative roster ([open-spaced-repetition/awesome-fsrs](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/awesome-fsrs)).

1.2 **The roster is uneven: most ports are "scheduler-only," and only two carry the optimizer/trainer.** Per `awesome-fsrs`, only **py-fsrs** (Python) and **fsrs-rs** (Rust) bundle both the scheduler *and* the parameter optimizer; every other binding (ts-fsrs, go-fsrs, dart-fsrs, swift-fsrs, the rs-fsrs family, etc.) is **scheduler-only** ([awesome-fsrs](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/awesome-fsrs)). This split matters for us: a scheduler is pure arithmetic and trivially portable; the *optimizer* is gradient descent over review logs (PyTorch in py-fsrs, Burn in fsrs-rs) and is the heavy part.

1.3 **Implementations lag the spec by whole versions, so "FSRS" is ambiguous without a version.** Snapshotting `awesome-fsrs` (2026-06): `ts-fsrs`, `fsrs-rs`, `py-fsrs`, `fsrs4s`, `lisp-fsrs`, `FSRS-Kotlin` are at **v6**; `rs-fsrs`, `go-fsrs`, `swift-fsrs`, `ocaml-fsrs`, the `rs-fsrs-*` ports are at **v5**; and **`dart-fsrs` is at v4.5** ([awesome-fsrs](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/awesome-fsrs)). Anything you adopt must be pinned to a stated FSRS version, because the parameter count and formulas differ across versions (see §3).

### 2. The DSR model: three per-item state variables and a power-law forgetting curve

2.1 **FSRS models each item with Difficulty (D), Stability (S), and Retrievability (R) — the DSR model originally due to Woźniak, refined via MaiMemo's DHP variant.** The canonical repo states FSRS "is based on the DSR (Difficulty, Stability, Retrievability) model" where **Stability** is storage strength (days for recall probability to fall to 90%), **Retrievability** is current recall probability, and **Difficulty** inversely scales stability gains ([free-spaced-repetition-scheduler README](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/free-spaced-repetition-scheduler)). The empirical backbone is the KDD 2022 paper by MaiMemo, fitted on **~220 million review logs** ([Ye, Su & Cao, 2022, ACM SIGKDD](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3534678.3539081)).

2.2 **The forgetting curve is a power law, not an exponential — this is the single most load-bearing formula.** In FSRS-4.5/5 form it is `R(t,S) = (1 + FACTOR·t/S)^DECAY` with `DECAY = -0.5` and `FACTOR = 0.9^(1/DECAY) - 1 = 19/81 ≈ 0.2346`, which forces `R(S,S) = 0.9` by construction ([Borretti, "Implementing FSRS in 100 Lines"](https://borretti.me/article/implementing-fsrs-in-100-lines); [Expertium, "A technical explanation of FSRS"](https://expertium.github.io/Algorithm.html)). FSRS-6 generalizes the exponent into a *learnable* parameter: `R(t,S) = (1 + factor·t/S)^(-w₂₀)`, with `factor` chosen so `R(S,S)=0.9` still holds ([awesome-fsrs Wiki: The Algorithm](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/awesome-fsrs/wiki/The-Algorithm)). Note PRD §7.3 already pins `DECAY=-0.5, FACTOR=19/81` — i.e. our app fixes `w₂₀` rather than learning it.

2.3 **The interval that achieves a desired retention `r` is a closed-form inversion of the curve.** Solving `R = r` for `t` gives `I(r,S) = (S/FACTOR)·(r^(1/DECAY) - 1)`, so at `r=0.9`, `I=S`; raising `r` *shortens* the interval by a fixed multiplier (≈0.45·S at 0.95, ≈0.27·S at 0.97, ≈0.09·S at 0.99) ([Borretti](https://borretti.me/article/implementing-fsrs-in-100-lines); [Expertium](https://expertium.github.io/Algorithm.html)). This is exactly the multiplier table PRD §7.5/§7.6 relies on, and it is *pure arithmetic* — no training needed to compute an interval once D/S are known.

2.4 **Stability compounds multiplicatively on success and is clamped down on lapse.** On a successful review, `S' = S·SInc` with `SInc ≥ 1`, where the multiplier rises when difficulty is low, when prior stability is low, and — critically — **when retrievability R is low at review time** (the desirable-difficulty / spacing term `e^{w·(1-R)}`) ([Expertium](https://expertium.github.io/Algorithm.html)). On a lapse, post-lapse stability `S_f` is computed and then **clamped: `S' = min(S_f, S)`** so a forgotten item can never become *more* stable than before ([Expertium](https://expertium.github.io/Algorithm.html); [Borretti](https://borretti.me/article/implementing-fsrs-in-100-lines)). This is the mechanism by which a lapsed manzil page legitimately demotes (PRD §6.2, §7.7).

2.5 **Difficulty updates by a grade-dependent step plus mean-reversion toward the "Easy" baseline.** Initial difficulty is `D₀(G) = w₄ - e^{w₅·(G-1)} + 1` clamped to [1,10]; the update applies a linear-damped step `D' = D + ΔD(G)·(10-D)/9` and then reverts toward `D₀(4)`: `D'' = w₇·D₀(4) + (1-w₇)·D'` ([Borretti](https://borretti.me/article/implementing-fsrs-in-100-lines); [Expertium](https://expertium.github.io/Algorithm.html)). Mean-reversion keeps long-lived items from drifting to permanent max difficulty — relevant because our cards live for years, far longer than a typical flashcard.

### 3. Versions, parameter vectors, and what "21 weights" means

3.1 **FSRS-6 is the current spec and uses 21 trainable parameters (w₀…w₂₀).** The widely-deployed default vector, verified identically across py-fsrs, the dart `fsrs` package, and the Algorithm wiki, is **`[0.212, 1.2931, 2.3065, 8.2956, 6.4133, 0.8334, 3.0194, 0.001, 1.8722, 0.1666, 0.796, 1.4835, 0.0614, 0.2629, 1.6483, 0.6014, 1.8729, 0.5425, 0.0912, 0.0658, 0.1542]`** — exactly 21 values ([awesome-fsrs Wiki: The Algorithm](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/awesome-fsrs/wiki/The-Algorithm); [py-fsrs](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/py-fsrs); [fsrs (pub.dev)](https://pub.dev/packages/fsrs)). FSRS-4.5/5 used **19** weights (w₀…w₁₈); the v6 additions are `w₁₉` (a stability-saturation term, role analogous to `w₉`) and `w₂₀` (the learnable curve exponent) ([Expertium](https://expertium.github.io/Algorithm.html)). A later experimental **"FSRS-7"** expands to ~35 weights with a dual short/long-term curve, but is not the shipped default and is out of scope for us.

3.2 **The default parameters are a *prior*, and the docs say not to optimize until you have ~1,000 reviews.** Open-source guidance is explicit that "until you have plenty of data for the optimizer to work with, the default weights will be more effective than ones based on your study history" — i.e. defaults are deliberately good across users, and personal optimization needs ~1,000+ reviews to beat them ([fsrs4anki tutorial](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/fsrs4anki/blob/main/docs/tutorial.md)). For a brand-new ḥāfiẓ with zero history, defaults are the only sane start — which aligns with PRD §7.3's "starting prior only."

3.3 **`desired_retention` is the one user-facing knob; everything else is internal.** Every scheduler (py-fsrs, ts-fsrs, dart-fsrs, fsrs-rs) takes `desired_retention` (default **0.9**), plus `learning_steps` (default 1 min, 10 min), `relearning_steps` (default 10 min), and `maximum_interval` (default **36500** days) ([py-fsrs](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/py-fsrs); [fsrs pub.dev](https://pub.dev/packages/fsrs)). Raising `desired_retention` raises workload super-linearly; the optimizer's notion of *optimal* retention is the value that minimizes **workload ÷ knowledge retained**, not workload alone ([fsrs4anki Wiki: The Optimal Retention](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/fsrs4anki/wiki/The-Optimal-Retention)). Hifz Companion overrides this default per-phase (PRD §7.5) and never exposes the raw slider.

3.4 **Same-day / short-term reviews use a separate stability formula, easy to get wrong.** FSRS-6 introduced an explicit same-day update `S'(S,G) = S·e^{w₁₇·(G - 3 + w₁₈)}·S^{-w₁₉}` for multiple reviews within one day ([awesome-fsrs Wiki](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/awesome-fsrs/wiki/The-Algorithm); [Expertium](https://expertium.github.io/Algorithm.html)). Because our scheduling unit is a once-or-twice-daily page recitation (not rapid flashcard drilling), the short-term path is mostly irrelevant to FAR/NEAR cards but *is* exercised by the sabaq "repeated to sign-off" loop (PRD §7.8) — so a port that omits short-term handling will mis-state new-lesson stability.

### 4. Reference implementations: what to read, port, or vendor

4.1 **There are two genuinely Dart-callable options, and they are different in kind.** `dart-fsrs` is a **pure-Dart, MIT** package "ported from the Python reference implementation (py-fsrs@6fd0857)" exposing `Scheduler`, `Card`, `Rating` (Again/Hard/Good/Easy), `State` (Learning/Review/Relearning), and `ReviewLog`, with `toMap()`/`fromMap()` JSON serialization and a stated **UTC requirement** ([open-spaced-repetition/dart-fsrs](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/dart-fsrs); published on pub.dev as `fsrs`, v2.0.1, platforms Android/iOS/Linux/macOS/web/Windows ([pub.dev/packages/fsrs](https://pub.dev/packages/fsrs))). By contrast, `fsrs-rs-dart` is **not pure Dart** — it is "a Rust implementation … with Flutter/Dart bindings using flutter_rust_bridge" (~56% Dart / 43% Rust), giving native FSRS-rs (scheduler **and** training) at the cost of a Rust toolchain and per-platform native builds ([open-spaced-repetition/fsrs-rs-dart](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/fsrs-rs-dart)).

4.2 **The `dart-fsrs` package is scheduler-only and pinned to FSRS-4.5, which is a feature, not a bug, for us.** `awesome-fsrs` lists `dart-fsrs` as "Scheduler only (v4.5)" ([awesome-fsrs](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/awesome-fsrs)). v4.5 is the version whose curve is fully specified by fixed constants (`DECAY=-0.5`, `FACTOR=19/81`) — the exact constants PRD §7.3 already hardcodes — so a v4.5 scheduler needs **no optimizer, no training, no learnable `w₂₀`** and is trivially deterministic. We don't need v6's learnable curve because we deliberately fix the curve and rely on the cycle ceiling, not parameter precision, for the retention guarantee (PRD §7.3, §7.6).

4.3 **The cleanest *spec to implement against* is the 100-lines write-up plus Expertium's formula page, cross-checked against py-fsrs.** Borretti's "Implementing FSRS in 100 Lines" assembles every formula (curve, interval, S₀ table, D₀, stability-on-success, post-lapse, difficulty update) into one auditable place and is explicitly **pedagogical, not an official reference** ([Borretti](https://borretti.me/article/implementing-fsrs-in-100-lines)); Expertium's page is the most complete prose derivation of each weight's role ([Expertium](https://expertium.github.io/Algorithm.html)). py-fsrs is the de-facto behavioral oracle since several ports (including `dart-fsrs`) are line-ported from it ([py-fsrs](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/py-fsrs)).

4.4 **The KDD 2022 paper and the algorithm wiki are the citable "why it works."** For any in-app or doc claim that the scheduler is evidence-based, the primary academic source is [Ye, Su & Cao (KDD 2022)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3534678.3539081); the living algorithm spec is the [awesome-fsrs Algorithm wiki](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/awesome-fsrs/wiki/The-Algorithm). Both are stable enough to cite in `science/` and `REFERENCES.md`.

### 5. Determinism: the schedulers are deterministic *except* for fuzzing

5.1 **Every reference scheduler is pure arithmetic and reproducible — the *only* nondeterminism is opt-in interval "fuzz."** py-fsrs and the dart `fsrs` package expose `enable_fuzzing` (default **True**), which "applies a small amount of random 'fuzz' to calculated intervals" (a 50-day interval may become 49 or 51); the docs state you set `enable_fuzzing=False` for reproducibility ([py-fsrs](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/py-fsrs); [fsrs pub.dev](https://pub.dev/packages/fsrs)). With fuzz off and "today" supplied as an input, identical inputs yield identical outputs — exactly the determinism PRD §7.12 and §19.3 require for golden tests.

5.2 **Load-balancing / fuzz is a *separate* concern from the core scheduler and belongs to us, not the library.** In the Anki ecosystem, load-balancing and "disperse siblings" live in a *helper add-on*, not the scheduler core ([fsrs4anki-helper](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/fsrs4anki-helper)); the scheduler computes an ideal interval and a post-hoc pass nudges it for workload smoothing ([fsrs4anki Wiki: The Optimal Retention](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/fsrs4anki/wiki/The-Optimal-Retention)). Our PRD §7.9 load-balancer is the same architecture: keep the interval math deterministic and pure, then do peak-smoothing as an explicit, *seedable* step (so it stays golden-testable) rather than via hidden random fuzz.

5.3 **FSRS can simulate future workload for a given retention and corpus — the basis for honest budget feedback.** The fsrs4anki simulator projects reviews/day over an N-day span for a deck and retention target ([fsrs4anki Wiki: The Optimal Retention](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/fsrs4anki/wiki/The-Optimal-Retention); [fsrs4anki tutorial](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/fsrs4anki/blob/main/docs/tutorial.md)). Because the curve and interval formulas are closed-form, we can run the same projection over 604 page-cards purely in Dart to power the "this scope won't fit your budget" honesty in PRD §12.2 — no service, no ML.

## Implications for Hifz Companion

1. **Build a pure-Dart scheduler in `engine/`; do not take a runtime dependency on a library as the source of truth.** The math is ~100 lines and we must own its determinism, version, and golden fixtures (PRD §19.1). Use `dart-fsrs` / `fsrs` (pub.dev) and py-fsrs as **behavioral oracles** in tests (port a handful of their published vectors), not as the shipped engine. This also sidesteps `fsrs-rs-dart`'s Rust toolchain and the F-Droid reproducible-build friction it would add (`oss-mobile-release-fdroid.md`).

2. **Pin FSRS-4.5 semantics with fixed constants `DECAY=-0.5, FACTOR=19/81`.** We deliberately do *not* adopt v6's learnable `w₂₀` or its optimizer: the retention guarantee comes from the **cycle ceiling (PRD §7.6)**, not from parameter precision, and a fixed curve is simpler to golden-test and audit. Keep the door open by storing the curve constants as named engine parameters, not literals scattered through code.

3. **Implement, in order: `retrievability(t,S)`, `interval(S, r)`, `initialStability(grade)`, `initialDifficulty(grade)`, `stabilityOnSuccess(D,S,R,grade)`, `postLapseStability(D,S,R)` with the `min(S_f, S)` clamp, and `difficultyUpdate(D,grade)` with mean-reversion.** Each is a pure function of `(D, S, R, grade)` plus weights — no clock, no I/O. Inject `today` as a parameter so the whole pipeline is reproducible (PRD §7.12, §19.3).

4. **Disable fuzzing entirely and make load-balancing an explicit, seeded, separately-tested pass.** Random interval fuzz would break golden tests and is unnecessary; the PRD §7.9 peak-smoothing already serves the same purpose deterministically. If any jitter is ever wanted, derive it from a seeded PRNG keyed on `(page_id, day)` so it stays reproducible.

5. **Override `desired_retention` per phase, never expose it.** Wire the §7.5 tiers (New 0.90, Near 0.94, Far 0.95, prayer-critical/weak 0.97+) straight into `interval(S, r)`; the closed-form multiplier (≈0.45·S at 0.95, 0.27·S at 0.97, 0.09·S at 0.99) lets us *show the workload cost* of each tier honestly (PRD §12.2) without a slider that would leak the algorithm to the user (PRD §7.1).

6. **Map the recitation signal onto the four FSRS grades and let Difficulty absorb interference.** Use Again=lapse, Hard=clustered stumbles, Good=clean, Easy=effortless; route the sacred-text guard (a dropped/swapped word caps the grade at Hard, PRD §7.7) *before* the FSRS update; and bump `D` toward 10 for mutashābihāt slips so the standard `(11-D)` term automatically shortens intervals on confusable pages (PRD §7.7, §9.2) — no special-case scheduler.

7. **Handle the short-term/same-day path only for the sabaq loop.** FAR/NEAR cards are reviewed at most daily, so the v6 same-day stability formula is largely moot for them; but the sabaq "repeat to sign-off" flow (PRD §7.8) reviews a new lesson several times in one day, so the new-card path must use within-day stability semantics or it will over-state new-lesson strength. Golden-test this case explicitly.

8. **Carry full audit fields in `review_log` so the engine is debuggable and the curve is later fittable.** Persist `s_before/s_after`, `d_before/d_after`, `r_predicted`, `elapsed_days`, and `grade` (already in PRD §10.2). This append-only log is exactly the input shape a future, optional, on-device curve-fit (plain least-squares, no ML service, PRD §7.3) would consume — and it makes every scheduling decision reproducible from history.

9. **Cite the spec, not a blog, for product/science claims.** In-app and `science/` claims about FSRS rest on the [KDD 2022 paper](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3534678.3539081) and the [awesome-fsrs Algorithm wiki](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/awesome-fsrs/wiki/The-Algorithm); the Borretti/Expertium write-ups are excellent implementation references but are secondary and should be graded as such in `REFERENCES.md`.
