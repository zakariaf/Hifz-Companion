# Calendars & i18n — Hijri (Umm al-Qurā), Solar-Hijri/Jalālī, and Gregorian in Dart/Flutter

**Topic:** Calendar correctness for a fully-offline, RTL Quran-retention app: how Dart/Flutter model dates, which calendar algorithms and packages are trustworthy (`hijri`, `shamsi_date`, `timezone`, `intl`), the *date-vs-instant* class of bugs, the irreducible ambiguity of the Islamic calendars, and the display conventions (numerals, era, week-start) the three locales (fa, ckb, ar) demand.
**Compiled:** 2026-06-16

This note is the evidence dossier behind engineering synthesis doc `07-dates-calendars-and-correctness.md` and the engineering non-negotiable value **"Date & calendar correctness — calendar-date value type, no instant bugs"** (Decision log: *dates & calendars*). It also feeds the localization implementation doc `12-localization-rtl-accessibility-impl.md`. The driving requirement is PRD §13.3: the app must render "next due / last reviewed" dates in **Hijri (Umm al-Qurā)**, **Solar Hijri / Jalālī** (default for `fa`), and **Gregorian**, in locale-appropriate numerals — while the scheduling engine (PRD §7, §19.3) stays pure, deterministic, and calendar-agnostic, with **"today" injected** and "all timestamps stored UTC; displayed in the locale's calendar/numerals" (PRD §10.3).

---

## What the evidence says

### 1. Dart has no date-only type: a `DateTime` is an instant, always (proleptic) Gregorian, and not internationalized

A Dart `DateTime` "represent[s] **an instant in time**, such as July 20, 1969, 8:18pm GMT," storing only a microsecond count from the Unix epoch plus an `isUtc` flag — there is no civil-`LocalDate` analogue ([Dart: DateTime class](https://api.dart.dev/dart-core/DateTime-class.html)). Two consequences matter for us:

- **It is calendar-bound.** "The `DateTime` class does not provide internationalization. To internationalize your code, use the `intl` package" ([Dart: DateTime](https://api.dart.dev/dart-core/DateTime-class.html)). The Dart team's own tracking issue lists what a single `DateTime` conflates: "an 'instant'/'timestamp' (number of milliseconds since epoch), a calendar date (in the proleptic Gregorian calendar, year-month-date) and calendar date arithmetic, a wall-time…, a time zone (-ish, 'local time' or UTC only)" — concluding "it's quite easy to make mistakes, especially around local-time `DateTime` objects" because "the type system cannot help you when everything is a `DateTime` object" ([dart-lang/sdk #44300, *There should be more specialized alternatives to DateTime*](https://github.com/dart-lang/sdk/issues/44300)). So the `(year, month, day)` fields are **proleptic Gregorian**; there is **no built-in support for Hijri or Persian calendars**. Any Hijri/Jalālī rendering therefore comes from a *separate library*, not from `DateTime` fields.
- **It is an instant, not a day.** A bare "2026-06-16" cannot be stored as a `DateTime` without *choosing a clock instant* for it — and that choice (local midnight? UTC midnight?) is where the bugs live (§3). The same SDK issue proposes the specialized types Dart lacks (`Date`, `Instant`, `LocalDateTime`, …) precisely to make this distinction representable ([dart-lang/sdk #44300](https://github.com/dart-lang/sdk/issues/44300)).

This is the Dart-flavoured version of the `Instant` vs `LocalDate` split that JSR-310/`kotlinx-datetime` make explicit: kotlinx-datetime "puts a clear boundary between the physical time of an instant and the local, time-zone-dependent civil time," and intentionally omits `LocalDateTime` arithmetic because it is ambiguous across DST, steering calendar math onto an `Instant` plus an explicit `TimeZone` ([kotlinx-datetime](https://github.com/Kotlin/kotlinx-datetime)). Dart did *not* make that distinction in its core type, so **we must impose it ourselves**.

### 2. Calendar arithmetic on `DateTime` is unsafe across DST — "+1 day" ≠ "+24 h"

Dart's own documentation warns, verbatim: "The difference between two dates in different time zones is just the number of nanoseconds between the two points in time. It doesn't take calendar days into account. That means that the difference between two midnights in local time may be **less than 24 hours times the number of days between them**, if there is a daylight-saving change in between" ([Dart: DateTime](https://api.dart.dev/dart-core/DateTime-class.html)). The corollary is that `add(Duration(days: 1))` on a *local* `DateTime` adds exactly 24 h of physical time, which on a DST-transition day lands an hour off — and, if that hour straddles local midnight, on the **wrong calendar day**. For an app whose entire premise is "a page is due every N days" (PRD §7.6 cycle ceiling), a `due_at` computed by `Duration`-adding to a local instant is a latent off-by-one-day generator. This is one entry in the canonical catalogue of temporal mistakes — "there are always 24 hours in a day," "any 24-hour period will always begin and end in the same day" — that this design must avoid ([Sussman, *Falsehoods programmers believe about time*, 2012](https://infiniteundo.com/post/25326999628/falsehoods-programmers-believe-about-time)).

### 3. The date-vs-instant pitfall is the central correctness hazard, and storage policy resolves it

The canonical mistake is conflating *physical time* (an absolute moment) with *civil time* (a human calendar date). `Instant` "represents a single moment on the timeline in UTC… a machine-facing view suitable for database storage, business logic, and data exchange," whereas `LocalDate` is "ideal for scenarios without time-zone consideration" — a calendar fact ([Baeldung: Instant vs LocalDateTime](https://www.baeldung.com/java-instant-vs-localdatetime)). The cross-industry rule is: **store one canonical representation; format per-locale only at the display boundary**. ISO 8601's `full-date` (`YYYY-MM-DD`) is defined on the proleptic Gregorian calendar and carries no time, zone, or locale ([Wikipedia: ISO 8601](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_8601); [Wikipedia: Proleptic Gregorian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proleptic_Gregorian_calendar)). For Hifz Companion the two storable quantities are different in kind and should be stored differently:

- **Event instants** (when a review actually happened: `review_log.reviewed_at`, `last_review_at`) are physical time → store **UTC `DateTime`** (PRD §10.3: "all timestamps stored UTC").
- **Scheduling days** (`due_at`, the cycle ceiling, "today") are civil calendar days, not instants — the engine reasons in *integer day counts* (`elapsed_days`, interval-in-days; PRD §7.3) and "today" is injected (PRD §19.3). These should be a **date value type** (a proleptic-Gregorian serial day or a `(y,m,d)` triple), never a local-midnight `DateTime`, so DST and zone changes cannot perturb a day-count.

This keeps the pure engine free of any `Calendar` object and makes its golden tests reproducible (PRD §7.12, §19.3): identical injected "today" + state → identical schedule, on every device in every timezone.

### 4. The Islamic calendars are genuinely several different calendars, disagreeing by up to ~2 days

There is no single "Hijri date." Unicode/CLDR define multiple Islamic calendar *types*, and a given Gregorian day maps to a slightly different Hijri date under each:

- **Umm al-Qurā (`islamic-umalqura`)** — the calendar "used by the government of Saudi Arabia for civil purposes," whose month starts are "determined at the Institute of Astronomical & Geophysical Research" from "modern astronomical theories of the sun and the moon" (whether moonset at Mecca follows sunset after geocentric conjunction) — i.e. **astronomical calculation, not a fixed arithmetic table** ([van Gent, *The Umm al-Qura Calendar of Saudi Arabia — astronomical rules*](https://webspace.science.uu.nl/~gent0113/islam/ummalqura_rules.htm); [hijridate / dralshehri](https://github.com/dralshehri/hijridate)). This is what most "Hijri date" UIs show.
- **Sighting-based (`islamic` / `islamic-rgsa`)** — meant to track an *actual* crescent (*hilāl*) sighting; because real sighting depends on weather, sky brightness, and observer location, it is **not fully deterministic** and cannot be predicted by calculation with certainty.
- **Tabular / arithmetic (`islamic-civil`, `islamic-tbla`)** — a fixed **30-year cycle** in which, "in its most common form, there are 11 leap years" ([Wikipedia: Tabular Islamic calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tabular_Islamic_calendar)); the two CLDR variants differ only by a one-day epoch shift (the civil-Friday vs astronomical-Thursday 15/16 July 622 CE Julian start) and so run exactly **one day apart**. Useful for cheap reversible conversion, but "dates predicted by the tabular Islamic calendar can occur one or two days too early or too late" relative to observation, which is why "virtually all Muslims use an observation-based calendar for religious purposes… and use the tabular calendar only for approximation" ([Wikipedia: Tabular Islamic calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tabular_Islamic_calendar)).

**The honesty caveat (critical for an Islamic app):** even Umm al-Qurā is a *civil* tool, not a religious authority. The "three most sensitive months" — **Ramaḍān, Shawwāl, and Dhū al-Ḥijjah** — have their start confirmed by sighting, and the announced (sighting-based) date can diverge from the pre-computed civil date by a day in either direction; "disagreement usually clusters around the start of the month" ([prayertimesksa: *Umm al-Qura vs moon sighting*](https://prayertimesksa.com/umm-al-qura-vs-moon-sighting/)). The most accurate Dart-side library for this calendar, `hijridate`, states the limitation outright: "Not intended for religious purposes where lunar crescent sighting is preferred over astronomical calculations" ([hijridate](https://github.com/dralshehri/hijridate)). So a Hijri *display date* is approximate, by construction, and must never be presented as the authoritative date of an observance.

### 5. Dart's `hijri` package: Umm al-Qurā by default, with a bounded year range and a sighting-adjustment hook

The widely-used `hijri` package (current `3.0.1`, BSD-2-Clause) converts Gregorian↔Hijri using **Umm al-Qurā** data ("From Gregorian to Ummalqura"), exposing `HijriCalendar.now()`, `HijriCalendar().fromDate(DateTime)`, `hijriToGregorian(y,m,d)`, component getters `hYear/hMonth/hDay`, `lengthOfMonth`, `toFormat(pattern)`, the locale helpers `setLocal/addLocale/getLongMonthName`, and comparison helpers `isBefore/isAfter/isAtSameMomentAs` ([pub.dev: hijri](https://pub.dev/packages/hijri)). Two engineering-relevant properties:

- **Bounded validity.** The Umm al-Qurā data underpinning the Dart hijri family is tabulated, not open-ended; the comparable `hijri_calendar` package documents a supported Gregorian range of roughly **1937–2076 CE** ([pub.dev: hijri_calendar](https://pub.dev/documentation/hijri_calendar/latest/)) — the same `1356 AH (14 March 1937) – 1500 AH (16 November 2077)` window the related `hijri_date_time` package states explicitly ([pub.dev: hijri_date_time](https://pub.dev/packages/hijri_date_time)) — and `dralshehri/hijridate` covers **1343–1500 AH (1 Aug 1924 – 16 Nov 2077 CE)** ([hijridate](https://github.com/dralshehri/hijridate)). This is *far* beyond any plausible due-date the scheduler will display, but it means Hijri conversion must be guarded for out-of-range inputs rather than assumed total.
- **A sighting-adjustment escape hatch.** These libraries expose an **`adjustments` map keyed by Julian Day Number (JDN)** whose values are `+1`/`0`/`-1` day — e.g. `{16800: 1, 16801: -1}`, used "to fine-tune the dates according to local moon sighting or specific regional differences" ([pub.dev: hijri_calendar docs](https://pub.dev/documentation/hijri_calendar/latest/)); sibling packages bundle named regional configs (e.g. a `Global` vs `India` adjustment, the latter typically one day offset) ([pub.dev: hijri_date_time](https://pub.dev/packages/hijri_date_time)). This is exactly the mechanism a community would use to reconcile the civil table with a local moon-sighting decision — but it is *manual* and per-region, reinforcing §4: no automatic library is "correct" for everyone.

### 6. Dart's `shamsi_date` package implements Jalālī via a verified, well-tested algorithm with a huge valid range

For the Solar Hijri / Jalālī calendar (the `fa` default, PRD §13.3), the de-facto Dart package is **`shamsi_date`** (pure Dart, BSD-3-Clause, null-safe, high test coverage). Its algorithm is "based on the popular JavaScript library `jalaali-js`," whose lineage traces to the same equinox-based leap logic as Borkowski's work ([pub.dev: shamsi_date](https://pub.dev/packages/shamsi_date); [GitHub: FatulM/shamsi_date](https://github.com/FatulM/shamsi_date)). Practically important:

- **API parallels `DateTime` cleanly.** It ships `Jalali` and `Gregorian` (both subclasses of a common `Date`), with `toJalali()`, `toGregorian()`, `toDateTime()`, `fromDateTime()`, `formatter` (a `DateFormatter` with skeleton-like fields), `julianDayNumber`, month-length getters, immutable `copy(...)`, arithmetic, and comparison operators ([pub.dev: shamsi_date](https://pub.dev/packages/shamsi_date)). Conversion is exact and reversible within range.
- **Range vastly exceeds need.** Valid from `Gregorian(560,3,20)` / `Jalali(-61,1,1)` up to `Gregorian(3798,12,31)` / `Jalali(3177,10,11)` ([pub.dev: shamsi_date](https://pub.dev/packages/shamsi_date)) — effectively unbounded for our use.

### 7. The Jalālī calendar is solar, equinox-locked, and the day-count is calendar-invariant

The Solar Hijri (Jalālī) calendar is "the official calendar of Iran and the most widely-used calendar in Afghanistan" ([Wikipedia: Solar Hijri calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_Hijri_calendar)) — which matters for `fa` and for the Kurdish regions that straddle that solar tradition. It is solar: each year "begins on the day of the March equinox," celebrated as **Nowruz**, and "the first six months (Farvardin–Shahrivar) have 31 days, the next five (Mehr–Bahman) have 30 days, and the last month (Esfand) has 29 days in common years or 30 days in leap years" ([Wikipedia: Solar Hijri calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_Hijri_calendar)). Because it "uses astronomical observations and calculations for determining the vernal equinox, it theoretically has no intrinsic error in matching the vernal-equinox year" ([Wikipedia: Solar Hijri calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_Hijri_calendar)). The authoritative algorithmic treatment is **Borkowski (1996)**, who showed the leap pattern is not a clean 33-year cycle but runs in groups of 28/32/36 years (the 33-year remainder rule — leap when year mod 33 ∈ {1,5,9,13,17,22,26,30} — dominates but "breaks intermittently"), captured by `MOD(MOD(N+1,33)−1, 4) == 0`, and that the simple rule is reliable only **AD 1799–2256 (1178–1634 AP)** before ΔT uncertainty forces astronomical computation ([Borkowski 1996, *EMP* 74:223–230](https://www.astro.uni.torun.pl/~kb/Papers/EMP/PersianC-EMP.htm)).

The engineering payoff: because Jalālī (and Hijri) differ from Gregorian only in *how a day is labelled*, **the serial-day distance between two dates is identical in every calendar**. A "due in 7 days" interval is 7 days whether the user reads it in Jalālī, Hijri, or Gregorian. So we can — and must — keep all interval math in Gregorian-serial integers and let the calendar affect *only the rendered label*.

### 8. `intl` localizes numerals and bidi but does **not** render non-Gregorian calendars

Flutter's localization stack is `flutter_localizations` + `intl` + `gen_l10n` (PRD §19.1). The `intl` package (current `0.20.2`, BSD-3-Clause, published by the verified `dart.dev` publisher) provides `NumberFormat` with locale-aware digit shaping, `DateFormat` skeletons (`yMMMMEEEEd()`, `EEEEE`, …), and `BidiFormatter` for bidirectional-text wrapping ([pub.dev: intl](https://pub.dev/packages/intl)). Two limits shape our design:

- **`DateFormat` is Gregorian-only.** Its documentation describes only ICU/CLDR Gregorian skeletons and even notes "time zones are not yet supported" because "Dart `DateTime` objects don't have a time zone"; it makes **no mention of Hijri, Persian, or other non-Gregorian calendar systems** ([pub.dev: intl](https://pub.dev/packages/intl)). So month *names*, era, and day-of-week for a Hijri/Jalālī date come from the calendar package (`hijri`'s localized month tables, `shamsi_date`'s formatter) — `intl` formats only the Gregorian path.
- **Numerals are a separate, locale-driven transform.** `NumberFormat(locale)` is the supported way to emit locale digits; per PRD §13.3, `fa`/`ckb` use **Extended Arabic-Indic** (۰۱۲۳۴۵۶۷۸۹) and `ar` uses **Arabic-Indic** (٠١٢٣٤٥٦٧٨٩), and one must "never concatenate raw ASCII digits into localized strings." Critically, the calendar packages tend to emit **Latin digits in `toFormat`/`formatter` output**, so the day/month numbers they return must be re-mapped to the locale digit set before display — the numeral transform sits *downstream* of the calendar conversion, not inside it.

### 9. Time-zone correctness, when it is needed, requires the `timezone` package — but the engine must not depend on it

Core `DateTime` "lacks time-zone context and DST handling" beyond the device's single local zone ([pub.dev: timezone](https://pub.dev/packages/timezone)). The `timezone` package (current `0.11.0`, BSD-2-Clause, `labs.dart.dev`) bundles the **IANA tz database** (2025c) and adds `TZDateTime` (a `DateTime`-compatible, zone-aware instant), `initializeTimeZones()`, `getLocation('Asia/Tehran')`, and `setLocalLocation(...)` ([pub.dev: timezone](https://pub.dev/packages/timezone)). For Hifz Companion the honest scoping is:

- The **only** place a real timezone matters is deciding *which civil day "today" is* for the user (so the daily session and the local notification fire on the correct local date), and possibly midnight rollover for the reminder. That is a thin display/notification concern at the app edge.
- The **engine never touches it** (PRD §19.3: "no wall-clock inside; 'today' is injected"). "Today" is computed once at the app boundary from the device's local zone and passed in as a plain calendar date. This keeps determinism intact and avoids dragging the IANA database into the pure module. Whether we even need the full `timezone` package, versus `DateTime.now().toLocal()` for "what local day is it," is a scope question — the device's own local zone is sufficient to name the local day, and we never need to convert across *arbitrary* zones because nothing syncs between devices (PRD §17).

## Implications for Hifz Companion

1. **Define a `CalendarDate` value type; keep it the single stored/computed unit for scheduling.** Represent `due_at`, the cycle ceiling, and injected "today" as a date-only value (a proleptic-Gregorian serial day, or an immutable `(y,m,d)` with integer day-distance), **never** a local-midnight `DateTime`. This is the Dart fix for the missing `LocalDate` (§1) and forecloses the DST off-by-one class (§2–3). The engine imports no calendar library and no `DateTime.now()` — "today" is injected (PRD §7.12, §19.3).

2. **Store the two date kinds differently, per PRD §10.3.** Event instants (`reviewed_at`, `last_review_at` in `review_log`) → UTC `DateTime`. Scheduling days (`due_at`, cycle ceiling) → `CalendarDate`. Never round-trip a scheduling day through a local `DateTime` instant, or a zone/DST change can shift the day (§3).

3. **All interval and cycle math stays Gregorian-serial integers; the calendar is display-only.** Day-counts are calendar-invariant (§7), so compute `interval(S, R)`, `elapsed_days`, and the trust-clamp ceiling (PRD §7.6) entirely in integer days, then map only the *rendered label* into the chosen calendar. No leap-year or month-length logic ever runs inside the engine.

4. **Use `shamsi_date` for Jalālī and `hijri` for Umm al-Qurā — at the display boundary only.** Both are pure-Dart, permissively licensed (BSD), and well-tested (§5–6); both bundle offline (no network — satisfies C1). Wrap each behind one presentation helper (`CalendarDate → localized String`), the display analogue of the engine: views never construct a calendar object themselves.

5. **Default the `fa` calendar to Jalālī, offer Hijri (Umm al-Qurā) and Gregorian, and make it an explicit user choice — not locale-inferred.** Per PRD §13.3 Jalālī is the Persian default and is offered to Kurdish; expose a Settings picker (Jalālī / Hijri / Gregorian) rather than silently switching off `Locale.current`. Pin the **Hijri variant to Umm al-Qurā** and label it as such (§4).

6. **Show a one-line honesty note wherever a Hijri date appears, and never tie a promise to it.** Hijri variants disagree by up to ~2 days and religious months can shift ±1 day by sighting (§4); a Hijri display is approximate by construction. This matches the app's "honest about decay / never fake precision" stance. The neutral daily notification ("Your revision for today is ready," PRD §14) must key off the *local civil day*, not a Hijri date being exact — and the app must never imply a Hijri date is the authoritative date of an observance (madhhab/sect-neutral, PRD R2).

7. **Re-map calendar-package output to locale numerals downstream.** `hijri.toFormat` and `shamsi_date.formatter` emit Latin digits; pass their numeric fields through a `NumberFormat`/digit-map step so `fa`/`ckb` show Extended Arabic-Indic and `ar` shows Arabic-Indic (§8, PRD §13.3). Month *names* and era come from the calendar package's localized tables, since `intl`'s `DateFormat` is Gregorian-only (§8).

8. **Guard Hijri conversions against their bounded range.** Umm al-Qurā tables in the Dart packages cover roughly **1343/1356 AH → 1500 AH (≈1924/1937 → 2077 CE)** (§5). Real due-dates fall comfortably inside this, but conversion calls should fail safe (fall back to Gregorian display, log nothing off-device) rather than throw, for any out-of-range edge case.

9. **Compute "today" once at the app edge; keep `timezone` optional and out of the engine.** Deriving the user's local civil day from the device's local zone is enough to name "today" and to fire the local reminder on the right date (§9); the full `timezone`/IANA database is only warranted if a concrete cross-zone need appears (none does, since nothing syncs — PRD §17). The engine receives "today" as a `CalendarDate` and stays pure.

10. **Cover it with round-trip and determinism tests (release gate, PRD §20.5).** Golden tests: (a) `CalendarDate → Jalālī/Hijri/Gregorian label → CalendarDate` is identity for the pinned display variant; (b) numeral mapping is correct per locale; (c) feeding the same injected "today" across simulated timezones/DST transitions yields the *same* schedule (the engine is calendar- and zone-independent); (d) a known Umm al-Qurā reference date (e.g. a documented 1 Ramaḍān) renders as expected, with the sighting caveat noted in copy.
