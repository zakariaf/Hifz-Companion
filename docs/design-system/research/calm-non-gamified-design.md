# Calm Technology & Anti-Gamification — The Evidence Against Streaks, Badges, and Points

**Topic:** Why streaks, badges, points, and leaderboards can *reduce* intrinsic motivation and manufacture stress, guilt, and compulsion; what "calm technology" is and how designing for peace and focus (rather than engagement) is the evidence-based — and, for an act of worship, the *obligatory* — choice for Hifz Companion. This is the research dossier behind design-system principle 1.2 ("Calm, not cute") and PRD constraints C6 and R3 ("No gamification of worship").

**Compiled:** June 2026. Every source below was verified against a primary or authoritative page (peer-reviewed venues — *Psychological Bulletin*, *American Psychologist*, *Journal of Consumer Research*, *Computers in Human Behavior*, *Games and Culture*, ACM/IEEE proceedings — plus the canonical calm-technology texts) before inclusion. Where a venue paywalls the full text, the open-access preprint or the author's posted copy is cited and noted. Sibling notes: `behavior-habit-design.md` (habit formation without coercion), `voice-tone-religious-adab.md` (non-coercive copy), `privacy-trust-ux.md` (trust as structure). The science-side mirror is `science/research/motivation-habit-noncoercive.md`.

This note is unusual among design dossiers because its conclusion is **a refusal**: the strongest evidence here is evidence for *not building* a whole class of features the rest of the app industry treats as table stakes.

## What the evidence says

### 1. Tangible, expected rewards reliably undermine intrinsic motivation — the overjustification effect

- The foundational quantitative result is **Deci, Koestner & Ryan's (1999)** meta-analysis of **128 controlled experiments** in *Psychological Bulletin*. It found that **engagement-contingent, completion-contingent, and performance-contingent tangible rewards all significantly undermined free-choice intrinsic motivation**, as did *expected* tangible rewards generally ([Deci, Koestner & Ryan, 1999](https://psycnet.apa.org/doi/10.1037/0033-2909.125.6.627)). This is the single most-cited body of evidence against bolting reward mechanics onto an activity people already value.
- The mechanism is the **overjustification effect**: when an already-intrinsically-rewarding activity is given an expected external incentive, people re-attribute their behaviour to the reward rather than to their own interest, and intrinsic motivation drops ([overjustification effect overview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overjustification_effect); [Deci, Koestner & Ryan, 1999](https://psycnet.apa.org/doi/10.1037/0033-2909.125.6.627)). For an app whose core activity is *worship the user already wants to do*, this is the central risk: a points-for-recitation system can convert "I revise because it is the Quran" into "I revise to keep my number."
- The why is specified by **Cognitive Evaluation Theory** (Deci & Ryan, 1985), a sub-theory of self-determination theory: external events have a **controlling** aspect and an **informational** aspect. Rewards experienced as *controlling* shift the perceived locus of causality outward and **reduce autonomy and intrinsic motivation**; only *informational*, non-pressuring feedback preserves it ([Cognitive Evaluation Theory overview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_evaluation_theory)). Streaks and badges are, almost by construction, on the controlling side.

### 2. Self-determination theory: motivation needs autonomy, competence, and relatedness — gamification is not automatically good for any of them

- **Ryan & Deci's (2000)** self-determination theory — among the most-cited frameworks in psychology — holds that intrinsic motivation and well-being depend on satisfying three basic psychological needs: **autonomy** (volition), **competence** (effectiveness), and **relatedness** (connection) ([Ryan & Deci, 2000](https://selfdeterminationtheory.org/SDT/documents/2000_RyanDeci_SDT.pdf)). Features support motivation only insofar as they *feed* these needs without undermining autonomy.
- A controlled experiment by **Sailer et al. (2017)** disaggregated game elements and found they have **specific, not general, effects**: badges, leaderboards, and performance graphs raised *competence* need-satisfaction and perceived task meaningfulness, while avatars/stories/teammates raised *relatedness* — but **perceived decision freedom (autonomy) could not be moved as intended** ([Sailer et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chb.2016.12.033)). The lesson is precise: the very elements people reach for (points, badges, leaderboards) push on competence and *leave autonomy untouched or threatened* — the opposite of what an act of worship needs.
- The empirical literature as a whole is equivocal, not enthusiastic. **Hamari, Koivisto & Sarsa's (2014)** review of empirical gamification studies (HICSS) concluded that **gamification's effects are highly dependent on context and the users involved**, with several studies reporting no effect or negative effects — gamification "works" only conditionally ([Hamari, Koivisto & Sarsa, 2014](https://doi.org/10.1109/HICSS.2014.377)).

### 3. Leaderboards and competition can actively demotivate the people who most need support

- In competitive elements, **for users who are performing poorly the leaderboard is a stream of negative feedback and social pressure**, generating a sense of incompetence and *lowering* intrinsic motivation rather than raising it (the documented downside of points/levels/leaderboards when they function as extrinsic incentives or controlling comparisons; see the gamification-and-intrinsic-motivation literature synthesised in [Li, Hew & Du, 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11423-023-10337-7)).
- **Toda, Valle & Isotani's (2018)** review of the *negative* effects of gamification in education built a taxonomy from the empirical record and found that **the leaderboard was the most-cited game element linked to harm, and "loss of performance" the most frequently observed negative effect**, alongside indifference, undesired behaviour, and a "declining effect" where gains fade over time ([Toda, Valle & Isotani, 2018](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-319-97934-2_9)). A public ranking of who has memorised or revised more of the Quran is, against this evidence, indefensible — and is already forbidden by PRD R3.

### 4. The mere act of measuring an activity reduces enjoyment of it — even with no rewards at all

- **Etkin's (2016)** six experiments in the *Journal of Consumer Research* are the sharpest finding for our purposes: **measuring an activity increases how much of it people do, but reduces how much they enjoy it and lowers subsequent intrinsic motivation and well-being — even when no external reward is attached.** Drawing attention to output "can make enjoyable activities feel more like work," which in turn decreases continued engagement and subjective well-being ([Etkin, 2016](https://doi.org/10.1093/jcr/ucv095)).
- The crucial nuance Etkin identifies is our escape hatch: measurement is *less* harmful when it is an **expected, integral part** of the activity (as score is to a video game). The design implication is not "never show numbers" but "**never turn the worship into the thing being scored.**" A retention heat-map that diagnoses where the Quran is decaying is integral, diagnostic information; a "pages revised this week" vanity counter is exactly the output-fixation Etkin warns against.

### 5. Streaks manufacture anxiety, guilt, and compulsion through loss aversion

- Streak mechanics work by **loss aversion** — losses are felt roughly twice as intensely as equivalent gains — so a long streak becomes a possession to be defended, and a missed day is experienced as a *loss/punishment* even though nothing tangible is taken away ([loss-aversion and streak design analysis, UX Magazine, 2026](https://uxmag.com/articles/the-psychology-of-hot-streak-game-design-how-to-keep-players-coming-back-every-day-without-shame)). The emotional residue is guilt, anxiety, and regret — manufactured by the design, not by the user's actual conduct.
- The qualitative case study by **Hadi Mogavi et al. (2022)** ("When Gamification Spoils Your Learning," ACM L@S '22) analysed nine years of Duolingo forum data plus interviews with 15 international users and documented **"gamification misuse"** — users becoming fixated on gamification and distracted from the actual learning — driven by **competitiveness, overindulgence in playfulness, and herding**, with negative effects on learning performance, well-being, and ethics ([Hadi Mogavi et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1145/3491140.3528274)). The same product's own evidence is telling: Duolingo's "Streak Freeze" exists because streaks were generating enough **anxiety** that relieving it measurably reduced at-risk-user churn — the company had to build an antidote to a stressor it manufactured.
- The harm is asymmetric and falls hardest on the conscientious. For a ḥāfiẓ — for whom losing hifz already carries genuine spiritual weight — a broken-streak shame mechanic does not motivate; it **weaponises an existing fear**. This is precisely the move PRD R3 forbids ("never 'You'll lose your hifz'").

### 6. Even "shallow" gamification — just *framing* something as a game — changes the psychological relationship to it

- **Lieberoth's (2015)** experiment in *Games and Culture* (N = 90) stripped a game down to its surface: one group used real game mechanics, another used the same artefacts *with no mechanics at all* (pure framing), a third was a plain control. **Framing the activity as a game produced almost as much of the interest/enjoyment shift as the full mechanics did** ([Lieberoth, 2015](https://doi.org/10.1177/1555412014559978)). The implication cuts both ways: cosmetic "playfulness" is not harmless decoration — it changes how the user relates to the task. For revising the Quran, *game framing itself* (cute mascots, "level up your juz," celebratory confetti) risks trivialising a sacred act, independent of any underlying scoring.

### 7. Notifications are a saturated, stress-correlated channel — engagement nags have a real cost

- In-situ logging of users' phones found an average of **63.5 notifications per day**, and **higher notification volume correlated with stress and feeling overwhelmed** ([Pielot, Church & de Oliveira, 2014](https://doi.org/10.1145/2628363.2628364)). Any reminder competes inside an already-stressful stream; an engagement-farming notification ("Don't lose your streak!") spends the user's scarce calm to serve the app's metrics.
- Convergent recent work characterises push notifications as triggering rapid attention shifts and a physiological stress/hyperarousal response that hinders the very reflection a contemplative practice requires ([alert-fatigue and well-being mixed-methods study, 2025](https://hal.science/hal-05432711v1)). A revision reminder is justifiable only as a single, calm, user-set, easily-silenced nudge — which is exactly what PRD §14 specifies.

### 8. Calm technology: the positive design philosophy — inform without demanding attention

- The counter-tradition is **calm technology**, named by **Weiser & Brown (1995)** at Xerox PARC: technology "that which informs but doesn't demand our focus or attention," designed to live mainly in the user's **periphery** and move to the centre only when needed ([Weiser & Brown, 1995](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calm_technology); [Weiser & Brown, "Designing Calm Technology"](https://calmtech.com/papers/designing-calm-technology.html)).
- **Amber Case (2015)** formalised this into eight principles in *Calm Technology: Principles and Patterns for Non-Intrusive Design* (O'Reilly). The ones that bind our design hardest: **"Technology should require the smallest possible amount of attention," "Technology should inform and create calm," "Technology should make use of the periphery,"** and **"The right amount of technology is the minimum needed to solve the problem"** ([Case, *Calm Technology* — principles](https://calmtech.com/)). This is a design ethic of *restraint* — the same instinct as *iḥsān* expressed in interaction terms.
- Calm technology and the SDT/anti-gamification evidence converge on one target: **a tool that does its job and then gets out of the way**, leaving the user's attention for the Quran rather than capturing it for the app. Engagement-maximisation and calm are opposing objective functions; we choose calm.

### 9. For religious practice specifically, transactional/gamified design provokes discomfort

- A study of **11 Islamic lifestyle applications** plus interviews with devoted Muslim users found that existing apps frequently **fail to foster autonomy, competence, and relatedness** — the SDT needs — and surfaced user discomfort with monetised, transactional spiritual engagement ([Kabir, Kabir & Islam, 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.02061), preprint of the *International Journal of Human-Computer Interaction* paper). The peer-reviewed version reports participants finding paywalled worship features "disheartening" and urges design that prioritises **respect for religious sensibilities and user trust over aggressive monetisation/engagement** (the same finding noted in the project's `RESEARCH-FINDINGS.md` §4).
- The education literature on gamifying Qur'anic learning reaches the matching conclusion: the **overjustification effect threatens the intrinsic spiritual motivation** that the practice depends on, and a points/badges/leaderboards model alone is insufficient and risks **reducing spirituality to a transactional reward system** — sustainable motivation comes instead from autonomy, acknowledged effort, and personal meaning. This is the academic statement of a fiqh-level intuition: worship is *lillāh*, not for points.

## Implications for Hifz Companion

1. **No streaks, ever — not even "gentle" ones.** Streaks operate by loss aversion (§5) and manufacture guilt, anxiety, and compulsion; the harm falls hardest on the conscientious huffaz we serve. We never count consecutive days, never say "don't break your chain," never show a flame/counter. The optional continuity indicator allowed by PRD §12.5 is **private, opt-in, and framed as information, not a possession to defend** — and it must be silenceable to zero presence. Enforce in `components.md` (no streak component exists) and `voice-tone-religious-adab.md`.
2. **No badges, points, XP, levels, or trophies on recitation or revision.** Tangible, expected, performance-contingent rewards undermine intrinsic motivation (§1) and are forbidden by PRD C6/R3. There is no `reward.*` token, no celebration animation tier, no "juz completed" trophy. *Reaching* a milestone may be acknowledged with calm, factual copy ("This juz is now in long-cycle revision"), never a prize.
3. **No leaderboards or social comparison of worship — full stop.** Leaderboards are the element most linked to harm and demotivate exactly the strugglers (§3). Multi-profile (teacher/family) is local bookkeeping, never a ranking. No "you vs. others," no percentile, no shareable score.
4. **Diagnose, don't score: the heat-map informs, it does not gamify.** Measurement reduces enjoyment when it fixates on *output* (§4), but is benign when it is *integral, diagnostic information*. The whole-Quran retention heat-map (PRD §12.5) is framed as **honest decay diagnosis** ("where is your Quran weakening?"), using calm sequential color + non-color encoding (`color.heatmap.*`, `data-visualization-heatmap-uncertainty.md`) — never as a "score to improve" or a green-completion game. No "pages this week" vanity counters.
5. **Feed autonomy and competence; never manufacture controlling pressure.** Per SDT (§2) and CET (§1), keep feedback *informational*, not *controlling*. The app states what is due and why (competence/clarity, autonomy-preserving) and **never pressures** ("you must," "you're falling behind"). Named, user-chosen cycles (PRD §7.11, §15.1) are an autonomy feature by design; the "Pure-cycle mode" exists so even algorithmic reordering is opt-in. Tokens: copy follows `voice-tone-religious-adab.md`; motion stays low-arousal (`motion.calm.*`).
6. **Refuse game *framing*, not only game *mechanics*.** Shallow gamification still changes the relationship to the task (§6) and risks trivialising a sacred act. No mascots, no cartoon celebration, no "level up your hifz," no confetti on a khatm (explicitly barred by PRD R3). Visual language is reverent and quiet — `islamic-identity-and-adab.md` governs; `motion-and-haptics.md` caps celebratory motion at "absent."
7. **One calm notification, opt-in, user-timed, neutral — never an engagement nag.** Notifications are a stress-correlated channel (§7); the *only* permitted reminder is the single neutral "Your revision for today is ready" (PRD §14), off-by-default-able, with no escalation and no guilt/fear copy. The "catch-up ready" note is framed as help. No "you haven't opened the app," no streak-at-risk push.
8. **Design to the periphery: do the job, then get out of the way.** Adopt calm-technology restraint (§8) as a measurable standard: the daily session is **short, finite, and capped** (PRD §12.2), then it is *done* — the app does not invent reasons to keep the user inside it. Success metric is the user's trust and the Quran's retention, **not** daily-active-use or session length. "The minimum technology needed to solve the problem" (Case's principle) is the altitude check for every feature.
9. **Make missed days a re-spread, not a shame-pile.** Because guilt mechanics are both unkind and counter-productive (§5), the catch-up flow re-flows the backlog calmly (PRD §7.9) and never renders a red overdue pile or a "you failed" state. Calendar gaps are never flagged as failures; copy is warm and assumption-free.
10. **State the religious case in the science screen.** The in-app science/about surface should make the non-coercion argument explicitly and citably (this is a CLAIMS-register entry under *Motivation & adab*): gamifying worship risks the overjustification effect on intrinsic, *lillāh* motivation (§1, §9). Saying so plainly is part of earning huffaz' and scholars' trust — and is honest about *why* the app deliberately lacks the features users may expect.

### Anti-patterns — we will never:

- Ship a streak counter, a "don't lose your streak" notification, or a broken-streak shame state.
- Award points, XP, badges, levels, or trophies for reciting, revising, or completing any portion of the Quran.
- Show a leaderboard, percentile, or any public/social ranking of memorisation or revision.
- Fire confetti, fanfare, or celebratory animation on completing a juz, khatm, or session.
- Use guilt- or fear-based copy ("You'll lose your hifz," "You're falling behind," "You haven't opened the app in N days").
- Send more than one calm, user-set, easily-silenced daily reminder, or any engagement-farming notification.
- Optimise any surface for daily-active-use, session length, or "engagement" as a success metric.
- Frame revision as a game (mascots, "level up," cartoon rewards) or otherwise trivialise the muṣḥaf.
