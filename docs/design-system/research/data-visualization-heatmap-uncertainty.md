# Data Visualization: the Retention Heat-map & Uncertainty

**Topic:** How to visualize a whole-Quran retention state — 604 pages / 30 juz of memory strength and decay over time — *honestly, calmly, and accessibly*. Covers: the heat-map as the right encoding for "see your whole Quran at a glance"; perceptually-ordered color scales (single-hue lightness ramps, colorblind-safe colormaps, why rainbow is the wrong default); how to make a page's decay *visible without alarm*; how to show that a strength estimate is *uncertain* rather than a false-precise number; and the hard "never color alone" accessibility floor.
**Compiled:** 2026-06-16 · for the Hifz Companion design system (`design-system/08-data-visualization.md` distills this note).

> Sibling notes: `color-emotion-and-theming.md` (the psychophysics of calm — why low saturation lowers arousal, and the palette this constrains), `calm-non-gamified-design.md` (why the heat-map must never become a streak or a scoreboard), `accessibility-rtl-inclusive.md` (contrast, color-vision deficiency, RTL), and `islamic-app-design-patterns.md` (adab — the muṣḥaf is never decorated or trivialized). The heat-map is, per the PRD, the app's central emotional surface ("keep your Quran green," PRD §12.5) and the embodiment of one of the design pillars — *honest about decay; nothing silently rots* (PRD §7.12, §3-R3). Every source below was verified against its primary publication (publisher page, DOI/PubMed/ACM record, or the standards body's own page).

## What the evidence says

### 1. The heat-map is the correct encoding for "show me my whole Quran in one glance"

The retention heat-map is a *cluster heat map* of a known kind: a rectangular tiling where each tile (here, a muṣḥaf page or juz) is shaded on a color scale to encode the value of one matrix cell ([Wilkinson & Friendly, 2009, *The American Statistician*](https://www.datavis.ca/papers/HeatmapHistory-tas.2009.pdf)). Wilkinson & Friendly trace it as "the most widely used of all bioinformatics displays" precisely because a dense grid of color-coded cells lets the eye take in a large matrix at once and find structure (clusters, hot spots) without reading any number. For our problem — 604 pages whose individual values nobody can hold in mind — this "whole corpus at a glance" property is exactly the requirement: the user must *feel* the state of the entire Quran, then drill into the weak juz.

The interaction model is the canonical one. Shneiderman's **Visual Information-Seeking Mantra — "overview first, zoom and filter, then details-on-demand"** ([Shneiderman, 1996, *IEEE Symposium on Visual Languages*](https://www.cs.umd.edu/~ben/papers/Shneiderman1996eyes.pdf)) maps directly onto: the whole-Quran heat-map is the *overview*; tapping a juz *zooms* to its pages; tapping a page reveals its exact strength, due date, and history *on demand* (PRD §12.5). This is why the heat-map should be the surface of the Progress screen and the numbers should live behind a tap — not the reverse.

The familiar **calendar-style contribution grid** (GitHub's commit calendar and its many clones) is the most recognized consumer instance of this pattern: each cell is one day, color intensity encodes a value, and a year reads in one glance ([react-calendar-heatmap, inspired by GitHub's contribution graph](https://github.com/kevinsqi/react-calendar-heatmap); a Flutter port exists — [`flutter_heatmap_calendar`](https://github.com/devappmin/flutter_heatmap_calendar)). The pattern is proven and platform-available; the design work is in the *scale* and the *framing*, not the mechanism.

### 2. Encode strength as a *single-hue lightness ramp*, because lightness is read as ordered magnitude

A retention value is *ordered* data (more strength → less strength), so it calls for a **sequential** color scheme, and the defining property of a good sequential scheme is that **lightness steps dominate**: light colors for low values, dark for high (or vice-versa), so the ramp is read as a monotonic order ([Brewer, Hatchard & Harrower, 2003, *Cartography and Geographic Information Science*](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1559/152304003100010929); [Harrower & Brewer, 2003, *The Cartographic Journal*](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1179/000870403235002042)). ColorBrewer's six **single-hue** sequential schemes (including Greens and Greys) work by holding one hue and varying chroma + luminance together. A single-hue lightness ramp has three properties we need at once: it is unambiguously *ordered* (no "is teal more or less than orange?" ambiguity); it survives conversion to greyscale; and it survives color-vision deficiency, because the order is carried by luminance, not hue. This is the empirical basis for the constraint already recorded in `color-emotion-and-theming.md`: **encode page health by a lightness ramp of our base green, not by a red→green gradient.**

### 3. The rainbow / "jet" colormap is the wrong default — it is unordered, has uncontrolled luminance, and invents false boundaries

The most cited warning in the field: the rainbow color map "confuses viewers through its lack of perceptual ordering, obscures data through its uncontrolled luminance variation, and actively misleads … through the introduction of non-data-dependent gradients" ([Borland & Taylor, 2007, *IEEE Computer Graphics and Applications*](https://www.sci.utah.edu/~kpotter/Library/Papers/borland:2007:RCCH/index.html)). Because hue is not perceived as ordered, a viewer cannot say whether green is "more" than yellow without consulting the legend; and the sharp luminance kinks (e.g. at yellow) create *artificial* edges that read as data features that are not there. The modern nuance — that rainbow maps are "not all bad" and have a few legitimate niche uses (e.g. emphasizing fine local contrast for expert audiences) — does not rescue them for our case ([Ware, Stone, Szafir & Rhyne, 2023, *IEEE Computer Graphics and Applications*](https://doi.org/10.1109/MCG.2023.3246111)). For a calm, lay-audience, ordered "how strong is this page" display, an unordered, high-arousal rainbow is exactly wrong.

### 4. If a multi-hue scale is ever used, use a *perceptually-uniform, CVD-safe* colormap — not an ad-hoc gradient

When more than one hue is genuinely needed (e.g. to add resolution at the dark end), the evidence points to **perceptually-uniform** colormaps — viridis and its family — which were engineered so that equal steps in data map to equal *perceived* steps in color, and which remain interpretable under the common forms of color blindness ([viridis package vignette, CRAN](https://cran.r-project.org/web/packages/viridis/vignettes/intro-to-viridis.html)). The strongest primary source is **cividis**, a colormap explicitly *optimized* for color-vision deficiency: it is "perceptually uniform in hue and brightness, and increases in brightness linearly," so it reads correctly for viewers with and without red-green color blindness ([Nuñez, Anderton & Renslow, 2018, *PLOS ONE*](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0199239)). The lesson is not "use viridis on the heat-map" — a single calm green ramp (finding 2) is better for *our* low-arousal, single-variable case — but rather the principle these colormaps embody: **perceived uniformity and CVD-safety are design requirements, and any scale we ship must be checked against them**, never hand-picked by eye.

### 5. Avoiding alarm is not a soft preference — red is a measured threat/avoidance cue that raises arousal

The PRD forbids a shame-pile and forbids ever telling a ḥāfiẓ a page is "safe to drop" (PRD §7.12, R3). The color science says the *visual* form of that rule is to keep saturated red off the heat-map. Red is not a neutral "bad" color: in the **color-in-context** framework, red carries learned threat meaning and, in achievement/evaluation contexts, primes **avoidance motivation** and caution ([Elliot & Maier, 2014, *Annual Review of Psychology*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4383146/)). Empirically, the color red **captures and holds attention specifically in emotionally-valenced contexts** ([Kuniecki, Pilarczyk & Wichary, 2015, *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4413730/)). Combined with the psychophysics in the sibling note — **arousal rises strongly with saturation** ([Valdez & Mehrabian, 1994](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1995-08699-001); `color-emotion-and-theming.md`) — a grid of saturated-red "rotting" pages would do precisely the two things the PRD prohibits: it would spike arousal/anxiety and it would frame revision as fear-driven avoidance. The honest-but-calm encoding is therefore a **desaturated cool/neutral** for the decaying end, not an alarm red — decay made *visible* (the green simply recedes) without being made *frightening*.

### 6. Negative, loss-framed displays can boost short-term effort but harm wellbeing — the wrong trade for a worship tool

There is a real temptation to make the heat-map scary because **loss-framed** incentives can be motivating: in a controlled study, people were *less* motivated when an incentive was framed as a gain to accrue than when the identical incentive was framed as a loss to avoid ([Goldsmith & Dhar, 2013, *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Applied*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24059820/)). But the broader framing literature finds the trade-off: avoidance-framed, fear/anxiety-driven goals are associated with **increased stress and decreased wellbeing**, and chronic negative framing undermines, rather than sustains, motivation. For an app built as *ṣadaqah* whose explicit thesis is *peace of mind, not engagement farming* (PRD §3-R3, §6-C6; `calm-non-gamified-design.md`), trading the user's tranquility for a short-term effort bump is the wrong bargain. The heat-map should make decay *honestly visible* (so nothing rots silently) but frame the act as quiet maintenance — "these pages are ready for revision" — never "you are losing your Quran" (PRD §14, R3).

### 7. Show *uncertainty* in the strength estimate — the app's numbers are predictions, not measurements

The retention value the app shows is a model output: retrievability `R` predicted by the FSRS-style curve from priors and self-ratings the PRD itself flags as low-confidence (`sourceConfidence ≈ 0.5`, PRD §7.3, §8.1). Presenting that as a crisp "87%" is **false precision**. The uncertainty-visualization literature is consistent that surfacing uncertainty, rather than hiding it, improves both decisions and trust — and that hiding it backfires when the point estimate is later wrong (a directly transferable result from the forecasting domain; see the sibling-domain CycleVault note and the canonical Joslyn & LeClerc / Spiegelhalter findings). The mechanism most relevant to a *heat-map* is the **Value-Suppressing Uncertainty Palette (VSUP)**: instead of encoding value and uncertainty as two fighting channels, a VSUP **suppresses the value signal (desaturates / collapses toward grey) where uncertainty is high**, so an uncertain cell literally looks less vivid and less definite, and a crowdsourced study found this makes people **weight uncertainty more heavily and decide more cautiously** ([Correll, Moritz & Heer, 2018, *CHI '18*](https://www.domoritz.de/papers/2018-VSUPs-CHI.pdf)). For us this is almost literal: a page graded only by a shaky self-rating, or seeded from a cold-start prior never yet recited, should render **muted/greyed** — "we are not sure yet" — and grow more saturated-green only as real, confident reviews (especially teacher sign-offs) accumulate. This is the visual analogue of the PRD's conservative-priors rule (PRD §7.10).

### 8. Never communicate state with words alone, and never with color alone — pair color with a number, label, and pattern

Two independent constraints converge on *redundancy*. First, accessibility: about 8% of men have red-green color-vision deficiency, and **WCAG 2.2 SC 1.4.1 (Use of Color) forbids color as the only means of conveying information**, while SC 1.4.11 sets a **3:1 contrast floor for graphical objects / non-text UI** like heat-map tiles and their boundaries ([W3C: WCAG 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/); [WebAIM: Contrast and Color Accessibility](https://webaim.org/articles/contrast/)). A heat-map tile must therefore carry its state in at least one non-color channel too — a value, a label, a texture/pattern, or a tap-through detail. Second, *honesty about likelihood*: the uncertainty literature shows verbal probability words ("likely," "weak") are interpreted with wide, person-specific, overlapping meanings, biased toward 50/50, and are only pinned down when paired with a number/range (Budescu et al.; Wallsten et al., established in the CycleVault uncertainty note). So "weak" on a page must come with a concrete retrievability range and a due date, not stand alone. The two rules reinforce each other: **every state on the heat-map is encoded redundantly — lightness *and* a number *and* a label — which is simultaneously the colorblind-safe design and the honest one** (PRD §18).

### 9. Position-and-grid beats fancy: keep the layout faithful to the muṣḥaf, read values by tile, drill for exactness

Two perception results discipline the layout. Cleveland & McGill's ranking of elementary perceptual tasks puts **position along a common scale as the most accurately decoded channel, with color saturation/shading far down the list** (established in `uncertainty-dataviz.md` / `color-emotion-and-theming.md`). The implication for a heat-map: color shading is fine for the *at-a-glance gestalt* (finding 1), but it is a *low-accuracy* channel for reading an exact value — so exact strength must be available by position/number on tap-through, not estimated from a tile's shade. And the heat-map grid should mirror the structure the ḥāfiẓ already has in mind — **juz → pages, in muṣḥaf order, laid out RTL** — so the spatial position of a "hot spot" *is* its location in the Quran (PRD §13.2). Tufte's **small-multiples** principle reinforces this: once the user learns to read one juz block, all thirty are instantly legible ([Tufte, *The Visual Display of Quantitative Information*, via Small multiple, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_multiple)). The design value is faithfulness and consistency, not novelty: a calm, ordered grid in Quran order, not an inventive chart.

### 10. The juz roll-up must lean to the *weakest* page, not the average — the honest aggregate

A subtle but load-bearing visualization-honesty point from the PRD itself: a juz's health color is a **roll-up of its pages**, and the PRD specifies a **min-leaning aggregate** — "one weak page is what fails you in ṣalāh — surface the weakest link" (PRD §10.3). This is the correct, honest choice and it has a visualization rationale: a *mean* over 20-odd pages would let one strong-average juz hide a single rotting page, which is exactly the silent-decay failure the whole product exists to prevent (PRD §7.12). So the heat-map's juz tiles must color toward their weakest constituent page (or show the weakest-page badge), never average it away. Aggregation method is not a cosmetic choice here; it is the difference between an honest display and one that lies by smoothing.

## Implications for Hifz Companion

1. **Make the whole-Quran heat-map the surface of the Progress screen, with details-on-demand behind a tap.** Render all 604 pages (grouped by 30 juz) as a calm grid that reads in one glance (findings 1, 9). Follow Shneiderman's mantra: overview (whole Quran) → zoom (juz) → details (page strength, due date, history). Numbers live behind the tap, not on the face (PRD §12.5).

2. **Encode strength as a single-hue *lightness ramp* of the base green — never a red→green gradient.** Strong pages are a deeper, calm green; weakening pages fade toward a light, desaturated cool/neutral (findings 2, 5). This is ordered, greyscale-survivable, and colorblind-safe by construction, and it satisfies the palette constraint already locked in `color-emotion-and-theming.md`. Token the scale as states (`color.health.strong … color.health.decaying`), not as `success`/`danger`.

3. **Ban the rainbow/jet colormap and any hand-picked multi-hue gradient.** Rainbow is unordered, luminance-uneven, and invents false edges (finding 3). If a second hue is ever needed for resolution, it must come from a **perceptually-uniform, CVD-safe** family (viridis/cividis) and be verified for uniformity and color-blind legibility, never chosen by eye (finding 4).

4. **Decay must be *visible* but never *alarming* — keep saturated red off the heat-map.** Red is a measured threat/avoidance cue that raises arousal and primes fear-driven avoidance (findings 5, 6); a grid of red pages would violate the PRD's "no guilt/fear" and "calm loss-prevention" rules (PRD R3, §6-C6). Show decay as the green *receding* toward a muted neutral, paired with a plain "ready for revision" framing — honest, not frightening.

5. **Render *uncertain* strength as muted/greyed, growing vivid only with confident reviews.** Adopt the VSUP principle (finding 7): a page seeded from a cold-start prior (never yet recited) or graded only by low-confidence self-rating renders **desaturated** — "not sure yet" — and saturates toward full green as real reviews, especially teacher sign-offs (`sourceConfidence = 1.0`), accumulate. This makes the PRD's conservative-prior honesty (PRD §7.10, §8.1) *visible* and discourages false confidence.

6. **Encode every state redundantly: color *and* number *and* label.** Per WCAG SC 1.4.1 and the verbal-vagueness evidence (finding 8), no tile conveys state by color alone. Each tile/detail carries a retrievability value (localized numerals), a plain label, and — where helpful — a texture or pattern for the decaying end, so the map is fully legible in greyscale and to colorblind users (PRD §18). Keep tile borders/contrast at the WCAG **3:1** non-text floor (SC 1.4.11).

7. **Color a juz tile toward its *weakest* page, never the average.** Implement the PRD's min-leaning roll-up (finding 10; PRD §10.3): a single rotting page must be able to "fail" its juz's color, or surface a weakest-page badge. Averaging would let the display hide exactly the silent decay the product exists to prevent (PRD §7.12). Document this so no future contributor "improves" it into a mean.

8. **Lay the grid out in muṣḥaf order, RTL, as faithful small multiples.** Juz → pages in reading order, right-to-left, so a hot spot's position *is* its place in the Quran (findings 1, 9; PRD §13.2). One learned juz block makes all thirty legible (small multiples). Calm and consistent over clever; the muṣḥaf is rendered faithfully, never gamified (adab; `islamic-app-design-patterns.md`).

9. **Never reduce strength to a color alone, and never round predictions into false precision.** Where the app names a page "weak," pair it with a concrete retrievability range and a due date in the user's calendar/numerals (finding 8). Color is the gestalt; the number behind the tap is the truth — and it is shown as the estimate it is, not a measured fact.

10. **The heat-map must never become a streak, a score, or a scoreboard.** Its job is honest situational awareness — "where is my Quran today" — not engagement pressure (finding 6; `calm-non-gamified-design.md`; PRD R3). No leaderboards, no "100% green!" confetti, no color punishment for a missed day. The emotional pull is the quiet desire to *keep one's Quran green*, achieved through calm maintenance, not fear.
