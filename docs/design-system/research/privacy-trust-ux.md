# Trust & Privacy UX for a Fully Offline, No-Account App

**Topic:** Making structural privacy *felt* and perceptible, honest communication, and the refusal of dark patterns — for a free, fully-offline, account-free, telemetry-free Quran-retention app.
**Compiled:** June 2026. All sources verified against primary publication pages (ACM DL, USENIX, INFORMS, Springer, arXiv, Ink & Switch, and reputable journalism for the Muslim Pro incident). This note is the evidence dossier the synthesis doc `10-privacy-and-trust-ux.md` distills; it cross-links `09-accessibility-and-inclusivity.md` and `11-voice-and-tone.md`.

Hifz Companion is structurally private by construction: no account, no login, no PII, no telemetry, no analytics, no microphone, and no backend we operate (PRD §17, §1/C1, R5). The only network use is a one-time, checksum-verified HTTPS download of *public* Quran assets, after which the app runs in airplane mode permanently (PRD §11.1.1). The design problem is therefore **not** how to collect data responsibly — there is no data to collect. It is the harder UX problem the structure creates: a guarantee the user cannot see is a guarantee the user does not trust. This note assembles the evidence on (a) why this audience parses privacy claims critically, (b) how to make an invisible structural guarantee *perceptible* and *verifiable*, (c) how honest, specific communication beats both rhetoric and over-disclosure, and (d) the dark-pattern taxonomy we commit to never using.

---

## What the evidence says

### 1. This audience has been burned, and trust here is religious, not cosmetic

- The defining cautionary case is **Muslim Pro**, "the most popular Muslim app in the world" (~95 million downloads across 200 countries), which was found in 2020 to be sending users' precise location to the data broker **X-Mode**, whose data reached US defence contractors and the military. Network analysis showed both the Android and iOS builds transmitting to the X-Mode endpoint; critically, **the practice was not disclosed in the privacy policy and there was no install-time or first-open dialog explaining the transfer** ([Cox / Vice (Motherboard), 2020](https://www.vice.com/en/article/muslim-pro-location-data-military-xmode/); [Middle East Eye, 2020](https://www.middleeasteye.net/news/us-military-data-muslim-pro-apps-ties-severed)). For an app touching worship, the breach of trust was existential, not a line-item privacy infraction.
- A peer-reviewed HCI study of the category — [Kabir, Kabir & Islam (2024/2025), *Islamic Lifestyle Applications*](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10447318.2025.2595545) (also [arXiv:2402.02061](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.02061)) — reviewed 11 popular Islamic apps and interviewed ten devoted users, and concludes with an explicit call to action: there is an "urgent need for ethical and transparent data practices in religious technologies to maintain user trust and engagement." Privacy is named as a first-order design concern for this user, not a compliance afterthought.
- The broader at-risk-user lesson generalizes: protection cannot be left to the user's expertise. In a parallel intimate-data domain, [Mehrnezhad & Almeida (2021)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3411764.3445132) found 30 top fertility apps activating on average **3.8 third-party trackers immediately on first launch — before the user engages any consent dialog** — and argued such data demands a higher duty of care because users *cannot* see or stop the leak. The architectural conclusion holds for our domain too: trust is earned by removing the collection surface, not by promising restraint over it.

### 2. The hardest problem is that an invisible guarantee is an untrusted guarantee

- Users reason about privacy through **mental models / folk theories** of how a system works, and those models drive both concern and protective action. The foundational study, [Wash (2010), *Folk Models of Home Computer Security*](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/1837110.1837125) (SOUPS 2010; 33 semi-structured interviews), shows non-technical users hold incomplete, often wrong models of threats and defences, and that those folk models — not the technical reality — determine their behaviour. The corollary for us: if a user's mental model of "an app on my phone" assumes a server somewhere is logging them, the *absence* of that server is invisible and the default assumption — distrust — wins by inertia. Structural privacy that is never surfaced is, to the user's mental model, indistinguishable from ordinary surveillance.
- This is why the design goal must be **perceptibility**, not merely correctness. A true claim the user cannot verify is psychologically weak; the burden is on the interface to make the structural fact *experiential* (it works in airplane mode — try it now) and *checkable* (the code is open; the asset URLs are public).

### 3. Surfacing privacy at the decision moment changes behavior — invisible privacy does not

- The foundational experiment is [Tsai, Egelman, Cranor & Acquisti (2011)](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/abs/10.1287/isre.1090.0260): when privacy information is made **salient and concise at the point of decision**, people preferentially choose privacy-protective vendors and even pay a premium. In their study, participants bought far more often from sites rated "high privacy" (47.4%) than "no privacy" (5.6%) and would spend extra for the protection. Privacy quality is invisible by default; making it salient at the moment of choice is what converts it into trust and action.
- [Balash, Ali, Kanich & Aviv (2024)](https://www.usenix.org/conference/soups2024/presentation/balash) tested Apple App Store privacy labels with **1,505 participants (Prolific, January 2023)**: label contents sharply move risk perception and willingness to install. Collecting *sensitive info used for cross-app tracking* made participants dramatically less willing to install (a very large effect), while — the directly relevant finding — a **"Data Not Collected" label measurably *increased* willingness to install**. For an app that can truthfully declare "Data Not Collected," that declaration is itself a high-value, behavior-moving trust asset.
- But labels and metadata alone do not land: the same body of work and [Zhang, Feng, Yao, Cranor & Sadeh (2022)](https://petsymposium.org/popets/2022/popets-2022-0106.php) document that lay users **misunderstand privacy-label terminology** ("linked to you," "tracking," "identifiers," "diagnostics") and find labels disconnected from real controls. The guarantee must be *re-stated in plain language inside the app*, not delegated to store metadata the user cannot parse.

### 4. Honesty must be specific and verifiable — rhetoric reads as evasion, and so does over-disclosure

- Generic reassurance backfires with a privacy-literate audience. Discourse analysis of how period/fertility companies responded to a privacy shock ([Song, Hernandez, Kou & Gui (2024)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3613904.3642384)) shows firms leaning on value-talk and brand ("our users' privacy is paramount to us"); the authors show these statements function as *reputation management* and call for privacy communication that is **specific, verifiable, and accountable** rather than rhetorical. "We care about your privacy" is the phrasing of a company that collects data; "the app has no network code after setup — check airplane mode" is the phrasing of one that does not.
- The opposite failure is also real: a **transparency dilemma**. Acquisti and colleagues' work on the limits of transparency and control finds that notices and disclosures, by themselves, do not reliably resolve privacy concern, and excessive or framing-laden disclosure can reduce rather than build trust ([Acquisti, *The Economics of Privacy*, and the framing/transparency-limits literature](https://www.heinz.cmu.edu/~acquisti/economics-privacy.htm)). The implication is not "say less" but "say the few load-bearing, checkable facts plainly, once" — not a wall of legalese that signals something to hide.
- Verifiability has a concrete vehicle here: **open source**. Studies of OSS trust find that the ability to inspect and independently audit code is a real (if often delegated) basis for confidence — "why should people have confidence in my software?" is answered, in part, by *you can look* ([Wermke et al. (2022), *Committed to Trust*](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Committed-to-Trust:-A-Qualitative-Study-on-Security-Wermke-W%C3%B6hler/ca2925811878d47cd80113ece88c708cc4a594b0); the QualiPSo OSS trustworthiness model). For a *ṣadaqah* project whose Quran data already lives in a public repo (PRD §11.1, a form of *waqf*), open code and public, checksum-pinned asset URLs turn "trust us" into "verify us."

### 5. When a choice exists, make the safe path the default path — opinionated, not coercive

- Users do not read or fully understand security/privacy text, so behavior must be steered by **defaults and opinionated design**, not by comprehension. [Felt et al. (2015), *Improving SSL Warnings*](https://adrifelt.github.io/sslinterstitial-chi.pdf) redesigned Chrome's warning around warning-science principles — brief, non-technical, specific text; visually promote the safe choice; put the risky option behind an extra step — and raised adherence to the safe choice by ~30 percentage points (the redesign shipped as Chrome's warning), even though full comprehension remained unmet. The transferable rule: the **default path must be the safe path**, the safe action the visually primary one, one decision per screen, concrete consequences, no jargon.
- Defaults are powerful precisely because of status-quo bias, which is why the same mechanism is weaponized as a dark pattern (pre-ticked consent, opt-out tracking) ([Acquisti et al. (2017), *Nudges for Privacy and Security*](https://www.heinz.cmu.edu/~acquisti/papers/AcquistiAdjeridBalebakoBrandimarteCranorKomanduriLeonSadehSchaubSleeper-ACMCS-2017.pdf)). The same review catalogs the legitimate nudge levers — information, presentation/salience, defaults, timing, reversibility — that an honest app uses to help (not trick) the user. Our few real choices (notifications, optional reciter-audio download, optional backup encryption) inherit this: protective default, reversible, explained in one plain sentence at the point of choice.

### 6. Permissions and network actions must be asked in context, with an honest reason

- Android/iOS runtime permissions are most trusted when requested **in context, at the moment the feature needs them, with a truthful rationale** — not up front in a batch ([Android Developers: *Request runtime permissions*](https://developer.android.com/training/permissions/requesting); the contextual-integrity field study, [Wijesekera et al. (2015)](https://people.eecs.berkeley.edu/~daw/papers/perms-sec15.pdf)). A large-scale audit, [Liu et al. (2018), *Android Runtime-Permission Rationale Messages*](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/8506574/), found rationales are under-used (<25% of apps) and frequently *wrong* — so the bar is low and an honest, accurate rationale is itself a differentiator.
- For Hifz Companion the surface is unusually small and that is a feature to surface: it needs **no microphone** (recitation is judged by a human; PRD §8.3, R5 — this also protects women's privacy by construction), no contacts, no location, no camera. The only network action is the one-time core-pack download and optional reciter packs, each an HTTPS GET of a *public* asset URL carrying **no per-user data** (PRD §11.1.1, §17). Each such action should state plainly, in context, what is fetched and that nothing about the user is sent.

### 7. Calm, non-coercive interaction is part of trust — attention is a thing you can steal honestly or dishonestly

- The notification and reminder layer is where well-meaning apps slide into manipulation. The **calm technology** tradition ([Weiser & Brown, Xerox PARC, 1995; codified by Case (2016), *Calm Technology*](https://calmtech.com/)) holds that technology should demand the smallest possible amount of attention, live in the periphery, and never pull a person out of their task — "a person's primary task should not be computing, but being human." For a worship-adjacent app this is doctrinal, not stylistic: PRD R3/C6 forbid guilt/fear notifications, streak nags, and gamification of the sacred. A neutral daily "Your revision for today is ready," easily silenced, with no escalation (PRD §14), is the calm-technology pattern applied to *adab*.
- This converges with the at-risk-user and self-determination findings (§1): coercive engagement mechanics (streaks-as-pressure, loss-aversion nags) erode the autonomy and trust the app exists to serve. Honesty about attention — taking only what the task needs — is continuous with honesty about data.

### 8. The dark-pattern taxonomy we commit to never using

- [Gray, Kou, Battles, Hoggatt & Toombs (2018), *The Dark (Patterns) Side of UX Design*](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3173574.3174108) analyzed 118 practitioner-collected examples and named five high-level strategies that recur: **nagging** (repeated interruption toward the designer's goal), **obstruction** (making a path — e.g. cancelling, exporting — artificially hard), **sneaking** (hiding or delaying relevant information), **interface interference** (manipulating visual hierarchy to privilege one option), and **forced action** (gating a desired thing behind an unrelated requirement). The 2024 synthesis, [Gray, Santos, Bielova & Mildner (2024), *An Ontology of Dark Patterns Knowledge*](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3613904.3642436), unifies 11 prior taxonomies into a shared high/meso/low ontology defining these as designs using **manipulation, coercion, or deception to undermine user autonomy**.
- Prevalence shows this is the default gravity of the industry, not a fringe: an automated audit of 240 popular mobile apps found that **95% contained at least one dark pattern** and the average app had around seven distinct types, with most users unable to perceive them ([Di Geronimo et al. (2020), *UI Dark Patterns and Where to Find Them*](https://dl.acm.org/doi/fullHtml/10.1145/3313831.3376600)). Naming the taxonomy explicitly lets us hold the design to a checkable standard: every screen is auditable against these five strategies, and any instance is a defect.

### 9. Store privacy disclosures are widely untrustworthy — which is why our verifiability must be structural, not self-reported

- The disclosure layer users are told to rely on is itself unreliable. Mozilla's [*See No Evil* study (2023)](https://www.mozillafoundation.org/en/blog/mozilla-study-data-privacy-labels-for-most-top-apps-in-google-play-store-are-false-or-misleading/) compared the Google Play **Data Safety** labels of top apps against their own privacy policies and found discrepancies in **roughly 80%** of them — labels that are "false or misleading," with loopholes (e.g. the "service providers" exemption) that let data-sharing go undeclared. A self-reported "we don't collect data" form is therefore *weak evidence*; an audience that has read coverage like this discounts it. The trust must come from a property the user (or a third party) can independently check, not from a claim the developer types into a form.
- Open source supplies that property, but only if the claim about the *running binary* can be verified — "source code does not run on computers; binaries do." [F-Droid's *Trust, Privacy, and Free Software* (2019)](https://f-droid.org/en/2019/05/05/trust-privacy-and-free-software.html) and its [reproducible-builds program](https://f-droid.org/docs/Reproducible_Builds/) make the point precisely: smartphone users are "forced to operate on faith," and **reproducible builds** close the gap by letting independent parties rebuild the published source and confirm bit-for-bit that the shipped APK matches it (the only allowed difference being the signature). For a *ṣadaqah* OSS project whose code, Quran data, and checksum-pinned asset URLs are all public (PRD §11.1, §11.1.1; engineering doc 13), reproducible builds + an F-Droid release path convert "trust us" into "anyone can verify us" — the strongest possible answer to the structural-privacy-is-invisible problem of §2.

## Implications for Hifz Companion

1. **Lead with the structural fact, stated as a checkable claim — never as a feeling.** Onboarding and the About/Privacy screen should say what is *true by construction*, in plain language: no account, nothing leaves this phone, no microphone, works in airplane mode. Prefer "the app has no network code after setup" over "we value your privacy" (Song et al. 2024; Acquisti transparency-limits). This directly answers the Muslim Pro failure mode — the breach there was undisclosed transfer with no honest dialog (Vice 2020).
2. **Earn and showcase a truthful "Data Not Collected" / no-tracking posture in the store and in-app.** It measurably raises willingness to install and trust (Balash et al. 2024). Keep the build free of any analytics/ads/backend SDK (PRD §19.3 already asserts a build-time check for this), so the truthful label is *forced* by the architecture, then reference it where the user decides to trust.
3. **Make privacy *perceptible*, not just correct.** Because invisible guarantees lose to default distrust (mental-models literature §2), give the user a felt proof: an onboarding line that invites turning on airplane mode and watching the app still work; a one-screen "what stays here / what we send (a public asset URL, nothing about you) / what we can see (nothing)" panel. Perceptibility is the design deliverable, not a disclaimer.
4. **Surface the one-line guarantee at every decision moment, not only once in a policy.** Salient, concise privacy info at the point of choice changes behavior (Tsai et al. 2011). Show the structural guarantee at first run, on the core-pack download screen, on the optional reciter-audio screen, and on backup/export — exactly where the user is weighing trust.
5. **Translate it into plain language in-app; do not rely on store metadata.** Users misparse "linked to you," "tracking," "identifiers" (Zhang et al. 2022). The Privacy screen is one short, jargon-free screen: what is stored (on this phone), what leaves it (only a request for public Quran files), what we can see (nothing), what happens if the phone is lost (only your own backup file can restore it).
6. **Be specific and verifiable; resist both rhetoric and over-disclosure.** Pair each claim with a way to check it — open-source repository link, public checksum-pinned asset URLs (PRD §11.1.1), the airplane-mode demo (Song et al. 2024; OSS-trust literature §4). Do **not** lean on the store's self-reported "Data Safety" form as proof: ~80% of top apps' labels are misleading and a literate audience discounts them (Mozilla *See No Evil* 2023, §9). But also keep it to the few load-bearing facts; a wall of legalese erodes trust (transparency dilemma, §4). Say the true, checkable things once and plainly.
7. **Frame the few network actions honestly and in context.** The core-pack and reciter-pack downloads each get an in-context line: "Downloading the Quran files once from a public open-source repository. This sends nothing about you." (Android rationale guidance; Liu et al. 2018; PRD §11.1.1). Never a vague "connecting…".
8. **Make the absence of the microphone a stated protection, not a silent gap.** The app structurally has no audio recording (PRD §8.3, R5). Say so: recitation is judged by you or your teacher, and the app cannot listen — which also protects women's privacy by construction. A removed capability, named, is a trust signal (mirrors the "Data Not Collected" effect, §3).
9. **Treat local-only, user-held backup as a privacy *feature* and explain the tradeoff.** Backups are user-initiated local files the app moves nowhere (PRD §16). This removes the cloud-snooping channel entirely — but it also means the user is responsible for the file, so state both halves honestly: "Your backup is a file you keep; we never upload it, and only you can restore from it." (local-first ownership thesis below; over-disclosure caveat, §4).
10. **Default to the safe path with opinionated, non-coercive design for the few real choices.** Notifications, reciter-audio download, and backup encryption follow Felt et al. (2015): protective default, the safe choice visually primary, the consequential action behind one deliberate step, one decision per screen, no jargon. Every such default is reversible and explained in a single sentence (Acquisti et al. 2017 legitimate-nudge levers).
11. **Ground the offline posture in the local-first ownership ideal — and say what it gives the user.** The seven local-first ideals ([Kleppmann, van Hardenberg, Wiggins & McGranaghan (2019)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3359591.3359737)) include *the network is optional*, *security and privacy by default*, and *you retain ultimate ownership and control*; their thesis is that cloud users become mere "borrowers" of their own data. Hifz Companion's user is an owner: their hifz record lives on their device, survives the project's disappearance, and is theirs to export or erase. Frame privacy as *ownership*, which is both true and motivating.
12. **Make erasure real, immediate, and honest.** One action wipes all local data — right-to-be-forgotten by construction (PRD §16). Because there is no server, "delete" actually deletes, with nothing recoverable elsewhere. State this plainly; it is a guarantee most apps cannot truthfully make.
13. **Keep the reminder layer calm and peripheral.** Apply calm-technology principles (Weiser & Brown 1995; Case 2016) and PRD R3/§14: one neutral, optional daily reminder, no guilt/fear framing, no streak pressure, no escalation, trivially silenced. Attention taken only as the task requires is continuous with data taken only as the task requires.
14. **Audit every screen against the five dark-pattern strategies, as a release gate.** Hold the UI to Gray et al. (2018): no **nagging** (no repeat upsell — there is nothing to sell), no **obstruction** (export, erase, and disabling reminders are one-tap and easy), no **sneaking** (no hidden network calls, no pre-checked options), no **interface interference** (honest visual hierarchy; the privacy-protective option is never visually buried), no **forced action** (no feature gated behind an unrelated requirement; the only required step is the one-time, clearly-explained asset download). Any instance found is a defect, logged and fixed.
15. **Publish the "why we built it this way" page and cite this evidence.** Because this audience parses privacy claims critically and has been burned (Muslim Pro; Kabir et al. 2024), an in-app, plain-language page explaining the structural choices — and pointing to the open repo and this citation list — is itself a trust signal: specific, checkable, and respectful of a privacy-literate, faith-motivated user.
16. **Make the binary independently verifiable, not just the source.** Aim for **reproducible builds** and an **F-Droid** release so a third party can confirm the shipped app matches the public source, because "binaries run, not source" and self-reported store labels are widely untrustworthy (F-Droid 2019/reproducible-builds; Mozilla *See No Evil* 2023, §9). This is the engineering counterpart of every claim on the Privacy page (engineering doc 13), and it is the strongest available answer to the "invisible guarantee" problem (§2): not "trust us," but "rebuild it yourself."
