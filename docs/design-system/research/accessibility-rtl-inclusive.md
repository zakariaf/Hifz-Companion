# Accessibility for an RTL Multilingual App

**Topic:** Inclusive, accessible design for Hifz Companion — a fully offline, no-AI, RTL (Persian / Sorani-Kurdish / Arabic) Flutter Quran-revision app: WCAG 2.2 conformance, screen readers (TalkBack / VoiceOver), dynamic text size, color-independence, and the RTL-specific accessibility concerns that generic guidance routinely misses.
**Compiled:** 2026-06-16

This note is the evidence dossier behind the design-system synthesis doc `09-accessibility-and-inclusivity.md` (and the RTL/localization sibling `12-localization-and-rtl.md`). It distills the normative standards (WCAG 2.2) and the platform mechanics (Flutter, Material 3, iOS/Android) into specifics that can be implemented and tested. The driving fact for this app: our users recite the Quran daily, often quickly, in three RTL scripts, and many are older huffaz with presbyopia or reduced color vision. Accessibility here is not compliance theater — it is *adab* toward the user and toward the muṣḥaf. Excellence (*iḥsān*) is the standard because the work is *lillāh*.

---

## What the evidence says

### 1. WCAG 2.2 is the conformance baseline; it is a superset of 2.1, so we target 2.2 Level AA

WCAG 2.2 (W3C Recommendation, 5 October 2023) is the current stable standard and adds **nine new success criteria** over 2.1 while keeping everything 2.1 required; conforming to 2.2 means conforming to 2.1 ([W3C: WCAG 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/)). Mobile native apps are not literally "web content," but WCAG is the de-facto and legally-referenced bar for mobile accessibility, and its criteria map cleanly onto native UI. The criteria most load-bearing for a touch-first, text-dense RTL reading app are:

- **1.4.1 Use of Color (Level A)** — color is never the *only* visual means of conveying information ([W3C: Use of Color](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/use-of-color.html)).
- **1.4.3 Contrast (Minimum) (Level AA)** — text contrast ≥ 4.5:1, or ≥ 3:1 for large text ([W3C: Contrast Minimum](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/contrast-minimum.html)).
- **1.4.4 Resize Text (Level AA)** — text resizable to 200% without loss of content or function ([W3C: Resize Text](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/resize-text.html)).
- **1.4.10 Reflow (Level AA)** — content reflows without two-dimensional scrolling down to a 320-CSS-px width ([W3C: Reflow](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/reflow.html)).
- **2.5.8 Target Size (Minimum) (Level AA, new in 2.2)** — pointer targets ≥ 24×24 CSS px ([W3C: Target Size Minimum](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/target-size-minimum.html)).

Two of the nine new 2.2 criteria are effectively *free* for us by architecture: **3.3.7 Redundant Entry** and **3.3.8 Accessible Authentication (Minimum)** both concern login / re-entering data, and the app has **no account and no authentication at all** (PRD §17) — there is simply no cognitive-function test to fail ([Vispero: New SC in WCAG 2.2](https://vispero.com/resources/new-success-criteria-in-wcag22/)).

### 2. Color-independence (1.4.1) is a hard requirement for our retention heat-map, not a nicety

SC 1.4.1 states: *"Color is not used as the only visual means of conveying information, indicating an action, prompting a response, or distinguishing a visual element"* (Level A). Its intent is explicit: *"some users have difficulty perceiving color. People with partial sight often experience limited color vision, and many older users do not see color well"* ([W3C: Use of Color](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/use-of-color.html)). The population scale is significant: red-green color vision deficiency affects roughly **8% of men (and ~0.5% of women)** of Northern-European descent, and ~95% of all color-vision deficiencies are red-green ([News-Medical: Color Blindness Prevalence](https://www.news-medical.net/health/Color-Blindness-Prevalence.aspx); [Birch 2012, worldwide prevalence](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/223985289_Worldwide_prevalence_of_red-green_color_deficiency)). A retention heat-map that signals "weak vs strong page" by a red→green ramp alone is precisely the classic 1.4.1 failure: a deuteranope cannot tell a decaying juz from a solid one. The fix is redundant encoding — pair hue with a non-color channel (lightness/saturation, a numeric retention %, an icon, or a texture/pattern), and ensure the underlying lightness contrast carries the signal on its own ([W3C: Use of Color](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/use-of-color.html)). The PRD already mandates this (§18: *"never rely on color alone (use labels/patterns)"*); this is the evidence behind that rule.

### 3. Contrast minimums are precise numbers, and the heat-map's graphical objects need 3:1 too

SC 1.4.3 requires text contrast of at least **4.5:1**, relaxing to **3:1 for large-scale text** — defined as **18pt, or 14pt bold** (or equivalent CJK size) — with exceptions only for logos, inactive/disabled components, pure decoration, and invisible text (Level AA) ([W3C: Contrast Minimum](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/contrast-minimum.html)). Separately, **non-text graphical objects** (the heat-map cells, decay indicators, chart strokes) and the states of interactive components require **3:1** contrast under SC 1.4.11 Non-text Contrast ([accessibilityassistant.com: WCAG 2.2 contrast](https://accessibilityassistant.com/blog/accessibility-insights/how-to-apply-wcag-22-colour-contrast-accessibility/)). Material 3 restates the same numbers for native UI: its accessible color pairings provide a **minimum 3:1 contrast** for UI component containers, alongside the **4.5:1 / 3:1** text floors ([Material 3: Accessibility designing](https://m3.material.io/foundations/designing/structure)). The implication: a calm, low-arousal palette (our `color.*` tokens) must still clear these floors in **all three themes** — light, sepia, and dark — and the sepia theme in particular is where soft earth tones quietly drop below 4.5:1.

### 4. Dynamic text size: respect the OS scale, don't fight it, and verify up to 200%+

Both platforms let users enlarge text system-wide: **iOS Dynamic Type** (Settings → Accessibility → Display & Text Size → Larger Text, with five extra "AX" accessibility sizes beyond the default) and **Android font scale** (independent font-size and display-size controls on Android 13+) ([Dept: iOS Dynamic Type](https://engineering.deptagency.com/ios-accessibility-part-1-dynamic-type); [A11Y Project: Text resizing in iOS and Android](https://www.a11yproject.com/posts/text-resizing-in-ios-and-android/)). WCAG 1.4.4 requires text to resize to **200% without loss of content or functionality**, and Flutter's own guidance is that *"the UI should remain legible and usable at very large scale factors for text size and display scaling"* ([W3C: Resize Text](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/resize-text.html); [Flutter: Accessibility](https://docs.flutter.dev/ui/accessibility)).

Flutter mechanics: text scale is read from `MediaQuery.textScalerOf(context)` and applied automatically by `Text`/`RichText`. The modern API is **`TextScaler`** (e.g. `TextScaler.linear(2.0)`), which **replaced the deprecated `textScaleFactor`** to support **Android 14's non-linear font scaling** — where already-large text scales at a *lesser* rate so it doesn't blow up ([Flutter: Deprecate textScaleFactor](https://docs.flutter.dev/release/breaking-changes/deprecate-textscalefactor); [Flutter: Android 14 non-linear text scaling](https://docs.flutter.dev/release/breaking-changes/android-14-nonlinear-text-scaling-migration)). If we must bound runaway scaling for a specific dense layout, the correct tool is **`MediaQuery.withClampedTextScaling`** (clamp a min/max), *not* hard-coding font sizes or disabling scaling — disabling it is itself an accessibility failure ([Flutter: withClampedTextScaling](https://api.flutter.dev/flutter/widgets/MediaQuery/withClampedTextScaling.html)). Critical caveat for us: the **Quran text is never OS-scaled** — it is rendered from fixed per-page KFGQPC glyph fonts (PRD §11), so muṣḥaf legibility is served by an **explicit in-app zoom control**, while *all UI chrome* (track chips, dates, buttons, settings) must honor the OS text scale. The two systems are deliberately separate.

### 5. Touch targets: the numbers differ by platform, and Flutter ships a test for each

WCAG 2.2's new **SC 2.5.8 Target Size (Minimum)** sets a floor of **24×24 CSS px**, with a *spacing* exception (an undersized target passes if a 24-px-diameter circle centered on it doesn't intersect a neighbor's) ([W3C: Target Size Minimum](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/target-size-minimum.html)). But the platform conventions are stricter and are what we should actually build to: **Material 3 / Android: 48×48 dp** (~9 mm, with ≥8 dp spacing between targets); **Apple HIG / iOS: 44×44 pt** ([Material 3: Accessibility designing](https://m3.material.io/foundations/designing/structure); [Flutter: Accessibility — "at least 48x48 pixels"](https://docs.flutter.dev/ui/accessibility); [LogRocket: accessible touch target sizes](https://blog.logrocket.com/ux-design/all-accessible-touch-target-sizes/)). Flutter encodes both as automated test guidelines: `meetsGuideline(androidTapTargetGuideline)` enforces **48×48**, `meetsGuideline(iOSTapTargetGuideline)` enforces **44×44**, `labeledTapTargetGuideline` checks that tappable nodes have labels, and `textContrastGuideline` checks contrast ([Flutter: Accessibility testing](https://docs.flutter.dev/ui/accessibility/accessibility-testing); [Flutter API: AccessibilityGuideline](https://api.flutter.dev/flutter/flutter_test/AccessibilityGuideline-class.html)). This matters most on the **recite/grade flow** (PRD §12.2), which is tapped many times daily, often quickly — the Again/Hard/Good/Easy buttons and the per-line stumble taps must clear 48×48 with comfortable spacing.

### 6. Screen readers work in Flutter through the Semantics tree; standard widgets populate it automatically

Flutter maintains a **semantics tree** parallel to the widget tree and exposes it to **TalkBack (Android)** and **VoiceOver (iOS)**; standard Material/Cupertino widgets generate accessibility nodes automatically, and custom rendering is annotated with the **`Semantics`** widget ([Flutter: Accessibility](https://docs.flutter.dev/ui/accessibility); [Flutter: Assistive technologies](https://docs.flutter.dev/ui/accessibility/assistive-technologies); [Flutter API: Semantics](https://api.flutter.dev/flutter/widgets/Semantics-class.html)). Supporting widgets: **`MergeSemantics`** collapses a subtree into one spoken node (e.g. a page-card showing "Juz 7 · page 134 · weak" should be read as one phrase, not three), and **`ExcludeSemantics`** hides purely decorative elements. For state changes the screen reader wouldn't otherwise notice — "catch-up plan ready," "page graded" — use **`SemanticsService.announce(message, textDirection)`**; note its signature *takes a `TextDirection`* precisely so the bridge speaks the announcement in the correct reading direction, and that on newer Flutter (post v3.35) it is superseded by `sendAnnouncement` ([Flutter API: SemanticsService.announce](https://api.flutter.dev/flutter/semantics/SemanticsService/announce.html)). The testable rule (per Flutter docs): *"The screen reader should be able to describe all controls on the page when you tap on them, and the descriptions should be intelligible"* ([Flutter: Accessibility](https://docs.flutter.dev/ui/accessibility)) — which means **every** icon-only control (mirrored back/next arrows, the heat-map cells, the sign-off toggle) needs a localized semantic label in fa/ckb/ar.

### 7. RTL is where mainstream accessibility advice quietly breaks — and we are RTL-only

Most accessibility guidance is written for LTR and silently assumes it. The RTL-specific failures are well-documented: when an LTR layout is served to RTL users, *"primary content is on the wrong side, focus order does not match visual order, and directional icons (back arrows, chevrons, progress indicators) point the wrong way"* ([accessibility-test.org: RTL considerations](https://accessibility-test.org/blog/support/rtl-right-to-left-website-accessibility-considerations/)). Three concrete requirements:

- **Reading / focus order must follow visual order.** Screen-reader traversal and keyboard/switch focus must run right-to-left, top-to-bottom, matching the RTL visual flow; a focus order that contradicts the layout disorients the user ([accessibility-test.org: RTL](https://accessibility-test.org/blog/support/rtl-right-to-left-website-accessibility-considerations/); [SimpleLocalize: multilingual a11y](https://simplelocalize.io/blog/posts/website-accessibility/)). In Flutter, app-wide `Directionality(textDirection: TextDirection.rtl)` drives both layout and semantic order together, which is why the PRD mandates it app-wide (§13.2).
- **Mirror directional icons — but only directional ones.** Material's bidirectionality guidance: icons that imply direction (back/forward arrows, chevrons, progress, send) should be **mirrored** in RTL, while icons that reference a real-world object or a fixed convention — **media playback (play points the same way), clocks, phone, numbers, the muṣḥaf glyph itself** — must **not** be mirrored ([Material 3: Bidirectionality & RTL](https://m3.material.io/foundations/layout/bidirectionality-rtl); [Material: Bidirectionality](https://m2.material.io/design/usability/bidirectionality.html)). Mirroring everything is as wrong as mirroring nothing.
- **Use logical, not physical, directionality everywhere.** Layout, padding, and icon decisions must use start/end (logical) properties, never hard-coded left/right, so a single `Directionality` flip produces a correct mirror ([Material 3: Bidirectionality & RTL](https://m3.material.io/foundations/layout/bidirectionality-rtl); PRD §13.2).

### 8. Mixed-direction text (numbers and Latin tokens inside RTL) is an accessibility bug, not just a visual one

Persian/Kurdish UI legitimately contains LTR runs — page numbers, "Juz 7," a version string, a file name in backup. Without **bidirectional isolation**, the bidi algorithm reorders these incorrectly (the infamous "page 7 of 30" rendering as "30 of 7"), and a screen reader can read them in the wrong order too. The fix is Unicode bidi isolation (`FSI`…`PDI`) around embedded runs, surfaced in Flutter via the `Directionality`/`Bidi` utilities and `TextSpan` direction handling ([accessibility-test.org: RTL](https://accessibility-test.org/blog/support/rtl-right-to-left-website-accessibility-considerations/); PRD §13.2). Two further RTL-multilingual details: (a) screen readers need the correct **language** set per text run so the right pronunciation voice is chosen — Flutter exposes `TextSpan.locale` and `MaterialApp.locale` for this ([Flutter: Assistive technologies](https://docs.flutter.dev/ui/accessibility/assistive-technologies)); and (b) **numerals must render in the locale's digit set** — Extended Arabic-Indic (۰۱۲۳…) for fa/ckb, Arabic-Indic (٠١٢٣…) for ar — via `intl` `NumberFormat`, never raw ASCII spliced into a localized string (PRD §13.3), both for correctness and so the screen reader speaks them naturally.

### 9. Offline + no-AI is an accessibility *advantage*, and our design floor already exceeds compliance

Two of our hard constraints help disabled users by construction. **No account / no authentication** removes the cognitive-load and CAPTCHA barriers that WCAG 2.2 added 3.3.7/3.3.8 to fix ([Vispero: New SC in WCAG 2.2](https://vispero.com/resources/new-success-criteria-in-wcag22/)). **No microphone / no speech recognition** (PRD §C2, R5) means there is no voice-input dependency that would exclude users with speech differences or those who cannot recite aloud at a measured pace; grading is human and self-paced. And the calm, non-gamified design (no flashing confetti, no streaks, no time-pressure animations) inherently avoids WCAG seizure/motion triggers (2.3.1, 2.3.3) and the anxiety load that harms users with cognitive and attention differences. The remaining work is therefore not architecture — it is disciplined execution on contrast, labels, scaling, target size, and RTL order, all of which are testable.

## Implications for Hifz Companion

1. **Adopt WCAG 2.2 Level AA as the explicit conformance target**, documented in the design-system README, with native equivalents (Material 3 + Apple HIG) where mobile differs from web. We claim 2.2, not 2.1.

2. **The retention heat-map must never encode meaning by hue alone.** Combine the color ramp with (a) a non-color channel — lightness/saturation that survives grayscale, (b) a numeric retention % on tap, and (c) the weak-page list (PRD §12.5) as a fully text-based equivalent. Verify the heat-map in a grayscale/deuteranope simulation. This satisfies SC 1.4.1 and the PRD §18 rule.

3. **Enforce contrast floors in tokens, in all three themes.** `color.*` text/background pairs ≥ 4.5:1 (≥ 3:1 only for large text), and heat-map cells / decay indicators / chart strokes ≥ 3:1 (SC 1.4.3 / 1.4.11). Add a CI gate using Flutter's `meetsGuideline(textContrastGuideline)`. Audit the **sepia** theme specifically — its earth tones are the likely failure point.

4. **Separate the two scaling systems explicitly.** UI chrome honors the OS text scale via `TextScaler` (use `MediaQuery.withClampedTextScaling` only where a dense layout truly needs a ceiling, never disable scaling); the muṣḥaf gets its own dedicated zoom control because its glyph fonts are fixed (PRD §11/§13). Test every screen at 200% (and the iOS AX sizes), confirming no clipped buttons and no horizontal scroll (SC 1.4.4 / 1.4.10).

5. **Size and space the recite/grade controls generously.** Again/Hard/Good/Easy buttons and per-line stumble taps at ≥ 48×48 dp (Android) / 44×44 pt (iOS) with comfortable spacing, since this flow is the most-tapped, often-rushed surface. Gate it with `meetsGuideline(androidTapTargetGuideline)` and `iOSTapTargetGuideline` in widget tests.

6. **Give every control a localized semantic label in fa/ckb/ar**, especially icon-only controls (mirrored arrows, heat-map cells, the teacher sign-off toggle). Use `MergeSemantics` to read a page-card as one phrase; `ExcludeSemantics` for decoration; `labeledTapTargetGuideline` as the CI check. Treat missing/English-only labels as a release blocker, alongside the existing ARB-coverage gate (PRD §20.5).

7. **Make RTL focus/reading order a tested invariant, not an assumption.** App-wide `Directionality.rtl`; verify TalkBack/VoiceOver traversal runs right-to-left, top-to-bottom on Today, Muṣḥaf, and Progress. Add this to the per-locale RTL golden-screenshot suite (PRD §20.5).

8. **Curate an icon mirroring table** in the design system: mirror back/next/chevron/progress/send; do **not** mirror media-playback, clock, phone, numerals, or the muṣḥaf glyph. Drive everything from logical start/end properties so one flag flips the whole app correctly.

9. **Isolate every embedded LTR run** (page numbers, "Juz 7," version strings, backup filenames) with Unicode FSI/PDI bidi isolation, and render all numerals in the locale digit set via `intl`. This is both a visual-correctness and a screen-reader-order fix.

10. **Bank the architectural wins and state them plainly:** no auth ⇒ 3.3.7/3.3.8 are non-issues; no microphone ⇒ no voice-input exclusion; calm/no-motion design ⇒ no seizure or anxiety triggers. List these in the accessibility synthesis doc so reviewers see the floor we start from.

11. **Test on real assistive tech, in our real languages, as a release gate.** A manual pass with TalkBack and VoiceOver in Persian, Sorani-Kurdish, and Arabic — covering the cold-start → first day → review → catch-up path — added to the §20 accessibility audit. Automated `meetsGuideline` checks catch the easy 80%; the RTL/multilingual 20% only shows up with a screen reader in-language.
