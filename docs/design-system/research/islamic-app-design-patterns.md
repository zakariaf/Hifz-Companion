# Design Patterns in Well-Made Islamic / Quran Apps — Research Note

**Topic:** Muṣḥaf-reading UX, reverence/adab in digital Quran presentation, what users value, and what to avoid (gamifying sacred content) — the design patterns that distinguish a respectful Quran app from a disrespectful one.
**Compiled:** 2026-06-16

This note gathers the evidence that grounds the design-system pillars *Reverence first (adab)*, *Calm, not cute*, and *Tradition is the interface*. It is the dossier behind `01-design-principles.md`, `11-voice-and-tone.md`, and `13-islamic-identity-and-adab.md`; it is cross-referenced by `calm-non-gamified-design.md` (the general behavioral-design case against streaks) and `arabic-persian-kurdish-typography.md` (the script-rendering case). Two kinds of source feed it: peer-reviewed HCI and motivation research on what helps or harms, and the observable design choices of the most-trusted Quran products (Quran.com / Quran Foundation, Greentech Apps Foundation, Ayah, the QUL/KFGQPC rendering stack). Where a religious point is involved, it is sourced to identifiable scholarship and scoped to *methodology*, never to a ruling.

---

## What the evidence says

### 1. The muṣḥaf page is the unit of reverence and the unit of memory — render it, don't re-typeset it

- The most-trusted Quran apps converge on the same rendering decision: replicate the **printed muṣḥaf page faithfully** rather than reflow the text. The standard production stack is **glyph-based per-page fonts** — the King Fahd Glorious Qur'an Printing Complex (KFGQPC) "QPC/QCF" fonts, distributed through the Quranic Universal Library (QUL). Each of the **604 pages of the Madinah muṣḥaf has its own font**, and each glyph is a **whole pre-drawn word/ligature**, not a character the OS shaper assembles. QUL states plainly that glyph-based fonts represent "each Quranic word as a unique glyph, which is a visual representation of an entire word," "designed on a page-by-page basis, with each font corresponding to a specific page of the Quran," so that "you'll need 604 fonts files to render the whole Quran," and that they are "typically handcrafted and are often used in printing Quranic manuscripts" ([QUL: Glyph-Based Fonts](https://qul.tarteel.ai/docs/glyph-based)).
- The reason this matters is not only aesthetic — it is **fidelity insurance**. Asking the OS text shaper to lay out Uthmani text is the layer where broken ligatures and dropped or duplicated diacritics appear; a single wrong diacritic in sacred text is unacceptable. The glyph-per-word approach removes the shaper from the path entirely (the font *is* the typeset page), which is why it is the convention for print-grade muṣḥaf reproduction ([QUL: Glyph-Based Fonts](https://qul.tarteel.ai/docs/glyph-based); [Quran Foundation: Font Rendering tutorial](https://api-docs.quran.foundation/docs/tutorials/fonts/font-rendering/)).
- The **same page layout also carries memory**. Greentech Apps Foundation, a UK charity with 10M+ downloads, ships a dedicated "Mushaf Mode" precisely because "consistency in mushaf layouts helps memorization by building **photographic memory**," and is "quite popular among the Arab demographic, students of knowledge and people doing hifz," offering "the familiar comfort of your favourite hard-copy mushaf in digital form" ([GTAF: Mushaf Mode](https://gtaf.org/blog/mushaf-mode-in-quran-app/)). For a ḥāfiẓ, *where on the page* a verse sits is part of the retrieval cue; a reflowing reader destroys that cue, while a fixed page preserves it.

### 2. "Reverence" in a digital Quran is concrete adab, and contemporary scholars locate it in *intent and presentation*, not ritual purity of the device

- Classical jurists ruled on touching the **physical muṣḥaf** without wuḍūʾ (citing Qur'an 56:79, "None touch it except the purified," and the related hadith). Contemporary fatāwā widely hold that **reading Quran from a screen does not require wuḍūʾ**, because "touching a screen is touching glass and circuits, not the sacred text itself," and the displayed text is understood as an electronically rendered image rather than the bound muṣḥaf ([Alhannah: Reading Quran Online Without Wudu](https://blog.alhannah.com/can-you-read-the-quran-online-without-wudu-understanding-islamic-etiquette-in-the-digital-age/); [Mizan ul Muslimin: Do You Need Wudu for the Quran?](https://www.mizanulmuslimin.com/2025/03/do-you-need-wudu-for-quran.html)). *(These are summaries of scholarly positions; the app must surface methodology and never issue a ruling — madhhab/sect-neutral, "needs scholarly review.")*
- The practical implication for our design is the **inversion**: because the device is not itself the muṣḥaf, the burden of reverence shifts from the object to the **presentation and the user's state of mind**. The same sources stress that performing wuḍūʾ and approaching the text mindfully remains "highly recommended… as a sign of respect," creating "a mindful and reverent environment" ([Mizan ul Muslimin](https://www.mizanulmuslimin.com/2025/03/do-you-need-wudu-for-quran.html)). A retention app whose primary act is **reciting from memory** sits even further from the touching-the-muṣḥaf question — recitation from memory requires neither touching nor wuḍūʾ by consensus — which frees the design to focus reverence on tone, framing, and the dignity of the page rather than on gating access.
- Adab therefore becomes a set of **design choices the app can get right or wrong**: never decorate or animate the sacred text, never interrupt it with ads or pop-ups (see §5), never trivialize completion of a juz, and never let chrome (badges, counters, mascots) sit *on top of* the words of Allah.

### 2b. The most-admired Quran interfaces are clutter-free, book-like, and put the text first — "no dashboard"

- **Ayah**, winner of the **Kuwait International Prize for the Holy Quran**, is repeatedly cited as having the most beautiful interface of any Quran app, and its stated philosophy is austerity, not ornament: "Ayah's clean, intuitive interface makes for a relaxing reading experience **without any visual clutter**," presenting "the noble Quran itself **with no dashboard**," in a page-turning format that "mimics the elegance of a physical Mushaf" ([Ayah — App Store listing](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/ayah-quran-app/id706037876); [Spark/MWM: Ayah, "clutter-free experience"](https://spark.mwm.ai/us/apps/ayah-quran-app/706037876)). The lesson is that in this category, perceived quality correlates with **removing** UI, not adding it — the highest compliment is that the interface disappears and only the muṣḥaf remains.
- This is the design-pattern equivalent of adab: the chrome defers to the text. It also aligns with the broader finding that distraction-free, focused presentation is what "students of knowledge and people doing hifz" actually use ([GTAF: Mushaf Mode](https://gtaf.org/blog/mushaf-mode-in-quran-app/)).

### 3. Trust in this category is built by being free, private, and non-transactional — and is broken by paywalling worship

- The trust-and-scale layer of the Quran-app market is occupied by **free, donation-funded, ad-free** products that frame *use itself* as ṣadaqah jāriyah. Quran.com, run by **Quran Foundation** (a US 501(c)(3) nonprofit), is "free of cost, ad-free, and accessible to all," sustained by donations on the premise that donors earn "continuous reward (Sadaqah Jariyah) for every letter read, memorized, and listened to" ([Quran Foundation](https://quran.foundation/); [Donate to Quran.com](https://donate.quran.foundation/)). Greentech Apps Foundation is a registered UK charity whose Quran app is "entirely free" with 10M+ downloads ([GTAF: Mushaf Mode](https://gtaf.org/blog/mushaf-mode-in-quran-app/)). These set a strong cultural anchor: **good Quran tools are expected to be free**, and charitable framing *builds* trust rather than merely permitting a business model.
- The peer-reviewed HCI evidence confirms the inverse harm. **Kabir, Kabir & Islam (2024)** evaluated 11 Islamic lifestyle apps (using self-determination theory and the technology-as-experience framework) plus 10 in-depth user interviews, and documented explicit discomfort with "the transactional model of spiritual engagement": users found essential features behind paywalls "disheartening," and one **uninstalled an app after five days** because most functionality required an upgrade — the perceived spiritual return did not justify paying. The same study reports users disturbed by ads in religious contexts (e.g., "indecent advertisements may pop up… while you are reciting the Holy Quran"), and concludes by urging "ethically aligned design… prioritizing respect for religious sensibilities, user trust, and inclusivity over aggressive monetization" ([Kabir et al. 2024, arXiv:2402.02061](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.02061)).
- For Hifz Companion — built free as ṣadaqah, fully offline, no accounts, no telemetry — this is not a constraint to mourn but the **strongest trust asset available**, and one users can *feel*: no paywall on worship, no ad ever beside an āyah, no data leaving the device. Privacy here is religious trust, not a compliance checkbox.

### 4. Gamifying sacred content risks corroding the very motivation it tries to boost — the overjustification effect

- Streaks, points, badges, and "ḥasanāt counters" are the standard engagement playbook, and Quran apps use them: Tarteel ships "streaks and heatmaps," lets users "track their streak to stay motivated," and runs "a gamified system that rewards users with badges and hasanat" ([Tarteel — App Store listing](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/tarteel-ai-quran-memorization/id1391009396)). But the motivation literature warns that bolting **extrinsic rewards onto an already intrinsically meaningful act can reduce intrinsic motivation** — the *overjustification effect*.
- The definitive evidence is the meta-analysis by **Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999)**, reviewing **128 experiments** in *Psychological Bulletin*: engagement-contingent, completion-contingent, and performance-contingent tangible rewards **significantly undermined free-choice intrinsic motivation** (d = −0.40, −0.36, −0.28 respectively), as did "all tangible rewards" and "all expected rewards"; tangible rewards were **more detrimental for children than for college students**. Crucially, the one thing that *helped* was non-controlling **positive feedback** (free-choice d = +0.33; interest d = +0.31) ([Deci, Koestner & Ryan 1999, *Psychological Bulletin* 125(6)](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/A-meta-analytic-review-of-experiments-examining-the-Deci-Koestner/8ad9801baea65b40fbbe6fc56e34b2b7be47d0ba)). Translated: a badge for finishing a juz risks **replacing** the worshipper's intrinsic reason with the app's token; honest, calm feedback ("this page is solid; that one is decaying") does not.
- This is grounded in **self-determination theory** (Ryan & Deci 2000, *American Psychologist*), which holds that durable motivation comes from satisfying **autonomy, competence, and relatedness**, and that **controlling** approaches (rewards, pressure, surveillance) erode initiative and learning quality ([Ryan & Deci 2000](https://selfdeterminationtheory.org/SDT/documents/2000_RyanDeci_SDT.pdf)). The Islamic-app HCI study applies exactly this lens and finds that "most applications **lack features that foster autonomy, competence, and relatedness**" ([Kabir et al. 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.02061)) — i.e., the field over-invests in extrinsic gamification and under-invests in the needs that actually sustain practice. For worship specifically, where the intended motive is *seeking the pleasure of Allah*, substituting a points economy is both psychologically risky and spiritually backwards.

### 5. What users value, what they resent, and what trivializes the sacred — a concrete catalogue

- **Valued:** a faithful, familiar muṣḥaf page (positional memory, "comfort of your hard-copy mushaf") ([GTAF](https://gtaf.org/blog/mushaf-mode-in-quran-app/)); a calm, clutter-free, "no dashboard" reading surface ([Ayah](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/ayah-quran-app/id706037876)); free and ad-free access framed as ṣadaqah ([Quran Foundation](https://quran.foundation/)); reading comfort options — **dark and sepia themes** are a near-universal request, and the accessibility literature notes sepia/beige backgrounds suit many readers (including those with astigmatism, ~half the population) better than stark white-on-black or black-on-white ([GTAF: night/dark mode](https://gtaf.org/support/how-to-read-quran-in-night-dark-mode/); [a11y-blog: sepia mode](https://a11y-blog.dev/en/articles/is-sepia-mode-essential/)).
- **Resented:** paywalled worship features and the transactional framing of spirituality; intrusive or "indecent" ads beside scripture; over-sensitive automated correction; progress that silently resets ([Kabir et al. 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.02061); competitor analysis in `research/RESEARCH-FINDINGS.md` §2). Reviewers of memorization apps also note that a human teacher "provides accountability that streaks and badges can only **imitate**" — i.e., gamified accountability is felt as a hollow substitute for the talaqqī relationship ([Tarteel — App Store reviews](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/tarteel-ai-quran-memorization/id1391009396?see-all=reviews&platform=iphone)).
- **Trivializing (avoid):** confetti or celebratory animation on completing a juz; XP/points or badges attached to āyāt; public leaderboards ranking worship; mascots, cartoon characters, or playful sound effects layered over the text; guilt/fear notifications ("you'll lose your hifz") that weaponize the spiritual weight of forgetting. The emerging field of **Islamic HCI** argues that faith-based design should be "grounded in a distinct value system… Islamic epistemology," foregrounding "faith-based reasoning, ethical accountability, and communal responsibility" in how technologies are designed and evaluated — a frame under which gamifying an act of worship is not a neutral engagement choice but an ethical one ([Decoding Islamic HCI, CHI 2026](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3772318.3791954)). Concretely, the sacred-content design literature stresses that gamification must not **trivialize the Quran's sacred nature**, and that any metric should honor "spiritual connection and divine pleasure" rather than substitute a token economy for it.

### 6. "Calm, not cute" has a formal design lineage — calm technology — that fits a retention app exactly

- The discipline that names what a reverent worship app needs is **calm technology**, coined by Mark Weiser and John Seely Brown at Xerox PARC: technology "that informs but doesn't demand our focus or attention," operating mainly in the **periphery** and moving to the center only when truly needed, so that "a person's primary task should not be computing, but being human" ([Weiser & Brown 1996, *The Coming Age of Calm Technology*](https://calmtech.com/papers/coming-age-calm-technology); [Wikipedia: Calm technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calm_technology)). Amber Case's modern restatement gives implementable principles: **"technology should require the smallest possible amount of attention,"** "the minimum needed to solve the problem" feature set, graceful failure, and respect for the user's life rather than capture of it ([Case 2015, *Principles of Calm Technology*](https://calmtech.com/)).
- This is the antithesis of the **attention-economy / engagement-farming** model that streaks, badges, and push-nags implement. A ḥāfiẓ does not want an app that maximizes daily-active-use minutes; they want a tool that hands them today's revision, gets out of the way while they recite to themselves or a teacher, and then disappears. Calm technology is the named, citable foundation for the design-system pillar *Calm, not cute* — low-arousal, peripheral, attention-respecting — and it converges precisely with the adab argument in §2 and the "no dashboard" aesthetic in §2b. The two literatures (one HCI, one Islamic) point to the same interface.

### 7. The "faithful book" vs "modern scroll" split — and why a hifz tool sits firmly on the faithful-page side

- Quran-reading apps cluster into two design philosophies. **Skeuomorphic, book-faithful** readers (e.g. Ayah's Madani-muṣḥaf page-turning, "Quran Flip OFFLINE" with "lifelike page-flipping animations… exact mushaf copy") reproduce the physical codex, including the page as the atomic unit ([Ayah — App Store](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/ayah-quran-app/id706037876); [Quran Flip OFFLINE — App Store](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/-/id6745195544)). **Modern continuous-scroll** readers (e.g. "QuranScroll," "Quran with Auto Scroll") flow verses in an infinite feed optimized for passive listening and casual reading ([QuranScroll — App Store](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/quranscroll/id6745479453)).
- For a **memorization-retention** product the choice is not aesthetic preference but functional necessity. Serial-recall research and traditional hifz pedagogy both establish that the muṣḥaf page is the recited unit and that *where a verse sits on the page* is part of the retrieval cue (`research/RESEARCH-FINDINGS.md` §3; GTAF's "photographic memory" point in §1 above). An infinite scroll that detaches text from its fixed page position **destroys the positional cue a ḥāfiẓ relies on** and breaks the bulk-recitation flow. Hence Hifz Companion is unambiguously a fixed-page product — not for nostalgia, but because the page *is* the memory structure. (Light page-feel skeuomorphism — clean turn, sober palette — is acceptable; sound effects and ornamental flourishes are not, per §2 and §6.)

## Implications for Hifz Companion

Concrete, testable rules. Each traces to the evidence above.

**Rendering & the page**

1. **Render every page through bundled KFGQPC per-page glyph fonts; never the OS shaper.** The font is the typeset page; overlays (weak-line highlight, mutashābihāt anchor, error position) are drawn as coordinates *over* the glyph layer, never by re-typesetting or storing reconstructed text. This is fidelity insurance, not styling. (§1; QUL glyph-based fonts; PRD R1/§11.2)
2. **Keep the muṣḥaf page fixed; never reflow.** Line breaks and page boundaries come only from the bundled layout dataset. Positional/photographic memory is a real retrieval cue for a ḥāfiẓ; preserving it is a *retention* feature, not a nostalgia feature. (§1; GTAF mushaf mode)
3. **The reader is "no dashboard."** When a page is shown, the words of Allah dominate; chrome recedes. Zoom, dark, and sepia themes transform the rendered layer, not the text. Sepia is a first-class theme, not an afterthought, for reading comfort and astigmatism. (§2b, §5)
3b. **Fixed page, never infinite scroll.** The muṣḥaf is paged; a clean RTL page-turn is allowed, infinite continuous scroll is not — the page position is a retrieval cue, and breaking it harms retention. Page-feel skeuomorphism stays sober: no flip sound effects, no ornamental borders on the sacred text. (§7, §1)

**Reverence (adab) as design**

4. **Never decorate, animate, or gamify the sacred text itself.** No badge, counter, mascot, or celebratory effect ever sits on top of an āyah or marks "completing" a juz. Markers are diagnostic (weak/decaying), never decorative or congratulatory. (§2, §4, §5)
5. **Frame adab as intent and presentation, not device-gating.** The app does not require wuḍūʾ to open (the primary act is recitation from memory), but it may offer a calm, optional opening — e.g. a quiet `taʿawwudh`/basmala framing and a neutral, distraction-free entry — that invites a mindful state without lecturing. Any etiquette guidance is surfaced as *methodology*, attributed, sect-neutral, and flagged "needs scholarly review"; the app issues **no fiqh ruling**. (§2)
6. **State the riwāyah and never present the muṣḥaf as "the Quran" in the absolute.** "Ḥafṣ ʿan ʿĀṣim — Madani muṣḥaf," shown in-app, is itself an act of adab and neutrality. (PRD R2; §1 context)

**Trust, privacy, and money**

7. **Free, ad-free, offline, account-free — and make it perceptible.** No paywall on any act of worship or revision; no ad ever beside an āyah; no telemetry; works in airplane mode. Onboarding states this plainly ("we never charge, never record audio, never send your data anywhere"). This is the category's strongest trust asset, and users can feel it. (§3; PRD §17)
8. **Privacy is religious trust, by construction.** No microphone, no recording, no accounts — recitation is judged by a human (self or teacher), which also structurally protects women's privacy. Say so. (§3; PRD R5)

**Motivation without coercion**

9. **No streaks-as-pressure, no XP, no badges on āyāt, no leaderboards, no confetti.** Replace the entire extrinsic-reward apparatus with **honest, non-controlling competence feedback**: the calm retention heat-map ("keep your Quran green"), weakest-pages surfacing, and a feasible daily plan — the one form of feedback the meta-analysis shows *helps* intrinsic motivation. (§4; Deci/Koestner/Ryan 1999; Ryan & Deci 2000)
9b. **Design to calm-technology principles: smallest possible attention, minimal feature set, peripheral by default.** The daily flow hands over today's revision and gets out of the way; the app does not farm engagement, does not nag, and is happy to be closed. A single optional, calm daily reminder is the only push. "Calm, not cute" is the citable design stance, not a vibe. (§6; Weiser & Brown 1996; Case 2015)
10. **Notifications are calm and supportive, never guilt or fear.** "Your revision for today is ready," never "you'll lose your hifz." After missed days, a re-spread catch-up plan framed as help, never a red shame-pile. (§4, §5; PRD R3/§14)
11. **Support autonomy, competence, and relatedness deliberately** — the needs the field neglects. *Autonomy:* named traditional cycles and a pure-cycle mode, never a coercive "optimal" the user can't refuse. *Competence:* visible, truthful page/juz health. *Relatedness:* first-class teacher sign-off (talaqqī) that honors the human chain the app can only serve, never replace. (§4; Kabir et al. 2024; Ryan & Deci 2000)

**Servant to the teacher**

12. **Gamified accountability is a hollow substitute for talaqqī — so don't fake it; defer to it.** Teacher sign-off overrides the machine; copy consistently frames the app as an aid to revision, never a replacement for oral correction and the sanad. (§5; PRD R6)
