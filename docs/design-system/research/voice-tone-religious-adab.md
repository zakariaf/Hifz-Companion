# Voice, Tone & Religious Adab — Writing for the Ḥāfiẓ With Reverence and Without Coercion

**Topic:** How Hifz Companion should *speak* — the voice and tone of its product copy across Persian (fa), Arabic (ar), and Kurdish Sorani (ckb): respectful, calm, non-guilt, non-coercive wording; *adab* in how the app addresses the user and the Quran; and keeping that one voice consistent across three RTL languages without flattening it into literal translation. This is the research dossier behind design-system principle 1.11 ("Voice & tone") and PRD requirements R3 ("no guilt or fear-based notifications"), R6 ("servant to the talaqqī chain"), C4 (three RTL languages), and C6 ("no gamification of worship").

**Compiled:** June 2026. Every source below was verified against a primary or authoritative page — peer-reviewed venues (*Human Communication Research*, *Journal of Pragmatics*, *Digital Health*, *Frontiers in Communication*, *Cogent Arts & Humanities*, the *International Journal of Human-Computer Interaction*), Nielsen Norman Group, Yaqeen Institute, Sunnah.com, and Quran.com — and the authors, year, and venue confirmed before inclusion. Where a peer-reviewed venue paywalls the full text, the open record (publisher abstract, DOI, or repository copy) is cited and noted. Sibling notes: `calm-non-gamified-design.md` (the evidence against streaks/guilt mechanics), `behavior-habit-design.md` (habit without coercion), `islamic-identity-and-adab.md` (reverence in visual identity), `localization-and-rtl.md` (the RTL/numeral/calendar mechanics), and `privacy-trust-ux.md` (trust as structure). The science-side mirror is `science/research/motivation-habit-noncoercive.md`.

This note governs *words*, not pixels. Its conclusion is that the app's voice is itself an act of *adab* — toward Allah, toward the muṣḥaf, and toward a ḥāfiẓ who already carries the spiritual weight of what they hold — and that the same restraint the rest of the design system applies to motion and color must apply, even more strictly, to language.

## What the evidence says

### 1. Adab is the governing concept: speech itself is worship-adjacent, and gentleness is a religious obligation, not a UX nicety

- *Adab* in the Islamic tradition is not mere politeness; it is "refinement, good manners, morals, decorum, decency, humaneness," a wide-ranging ethic covering greetings, gracious speech, the avoidance of insult, and the whole comportment of a person — and, distinctively, it "transcends mere politeness and embodies a profound spiritual practice centered around respectful conduct, humility, and devoted service" ([*Adab (Islam)*, overview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adab_(Islam))). For an app whose subject is the Quran, adab is therefore the *governing* design constraint on language, ranking above any conversion- or engagement-optimised copy convention.
- The classical scholarship frames adab in seeking knowledge as a discipline aimed ultimately at knowledge of God (*maʿrifah*), built on three relations — toward the **self**, the **teacher**, and **knowledge itself** — and it explicitly instructs the *teacher's* side of that relationship to be gentle: a teacher should be "kind and compassionate towards his students, just as the Prophet ﷺ was kind and compassionate towards his companions," guiding "gently, always encouraging them but never stifling them" ([Yasien Mohamed, Yaqeen Institute, 2023](https://yaqeeninstitute.org/read/paper/etiquette-as-spiritual-nourishment-the-adab-of-the-student-according-to-al-ghazali-and-al-isfahani)). The app stands, in the revision relationship, on the teacher's side of that ethic — it is the thing that "tells the student what to revise" — so this is the directly applicable model: encourage, never stifle; correct, never shame.
- The obligation of gentleness is anchored in the Sunnah: ʿĀʾisha (raḍiya Allāhu ʿanhā) reported that the Prophet ﷺ said, "Verily, gentleness (*al-rifq*) is not found in anything except that it beautifies it, and it is not removed from anything except that it disgraces it" — *inna al-rifqa lā yakūnu fī shayʾin illā zānahu wa lā yunzaʿu min shayʾin illā shānahu* ([Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim 2594a, Sunnah.com](https://sunnah.com/muslim:2594a)). Read as a copy principle: gentleness is what makes any message *better*, and its absence makes any message *worse* — there is no register in which harshness improves the product.
- The Quran prescribes the *manner* of invitation directly: "Invite to the way of your Lord with wisdom (*ḥikmah*) and good instruction (*al-mawʿiẓa al-ḥasana*), and argue with them in the way that is best" (Q 16:125). The classical tafsīr reads this as method-tiered gentleness — wisdom for those of understanding, good counsel for the general listener, and even debate conducted "with kindness, gentleness and good speech" ([Q 16:125, Quran.com](https://quran.com/en/an-nahl/125)). This is the scriptural warrant for a calm, instructive, never-hectoring voice.

### 2. Tone is a measurable design variable with four dimensions — and our targets on all four are fixed by adab

- Nielsen Norman Group's standard model (Kate Moran, 2016; updated 2023) decomposes the tone of any piece of copy into **four dimensions, each a spectrum**: *Formal ↔ Casual*, *Serious ↔ Funny*, *Respectful ↔ Irreverent*, and *Matter-of-fact ↔ Enthusiastic* ([Moran / NN/g, 2016/2023](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/tone-of-voice-dimensions/)). The value of the model is that it makes "tone" auditable: a string can be scored on each axis and checked against a target.
- For Hifz Companion the four targets are not open design choices — adab fixes them. The voice sits **respectful** (never irreverent: no quirk, no edge, no jokes about the muṣḥaf), **matter-of-fact** (not enthusiastic: NN/g notes enthusiasm is signalled by emotive language and exclamation marks, which the blueprint's tone rule and PRD R3 forbid in product copy), and **serious** (not funny). The one axis with genuine latitude is *Formal ↔ Casual*, and even there the floor is "respectful and warm," never flip. NN/g's own caution that irreverence should target *subject matter*, not the reader, doubles as our prohibition: there is no subject here — the Quran, hifz, the user's effort — that it is permissible to be irreverent toward.
- The same framework warns that tone differences are real but *small* in magnitude (≈0.5–1 point on a 5-point scale in their tests). The lesson is consistency over flourish: a single, quiet, dependable voice across every screen and all three languages does more than any clever turn of phrase — and is exactly what a reverence-first product wants.

### 3. Controlling language ("you must," "you should," "don't") provokes reactance and message rejection — the opposite of what a revision aid needs

- Psychological Reactance Theory (Brehm, 1966) holds that people become "aversively aroused when perceived freedoms are threatened by overtly persuasive messages," and respond by rejecting the message, derogating the source, or doing the opposite ([Reynolds-Tylus, *Frontiers in Communication*, 2019](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/communication/articles/10.3389/fcomm.2019.00056/full)). Reactance "is more likely to occur when messages are controlling (containing orders and words like 'should,' 'have to,' and 'must')" and offer no choice.
- The canonical experiment is **Miller, Lane, Deatrick, Young & Potts (2007)** in *Human Communication Research*: manipulating the level of controlling language in promotional health messages to a young-adult audience, they found "a number of negative outcomes associated with the use of controlling language," and more positive outcomes when messages instead used **restoration-of-freedom postscripts** (a brief reminder that the choice belongs to the reader) ([Miller et al., 2007, HCR 33:219–240](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1468-2958.2007.00297.x); [ERIC EJ755929](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ755929)). Controlling wording alienates; "the choice is yours" framing repairs the threat.
- Reynolds-Tylus's 2019 review consolidates the strategies that *reduce* reactance: **implicit/qualified language** ("perhaps," "possibly"), **restoration postscripts** ("the choice is yours"), **provision of choice** (offer options rather than mandate one), **message-induced empathy**, and **narrative** framing that lowers perceived persuasive intent ([Reynolds-Tylus, 2019](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/communication/articles/10.3389/fcomm.2019.00056/full)). Each maps cleanly onto a Hifz Companion surface: suggest rather than command; remind the user the cycle and budget are theirs; offer "raise budget / lengthen cycle / pause new sabaq" rather than dictating one fix.
- **Honest nuance (an [OBS]-grade null result we will not overstate):** a randomized online trial by **Altendorf, van Weert, Hoving & Smit (2019)** in *Digital Health* tested "could" vs. "should" wording and provision of choice in a tailored alcohol-reduction message and found **no significant differences** in perceived autonomy support or reactance — reactance was low across all conditions ([Altendorf et al., 2019, *Digital Health*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6393822/)). The lesson is calibration, not abandonment: in already-cooperative, low-threat contexts the *marginal* gain from softer wording may be small, but the *downside* of controlling wording is well-documented and asymmetric, so the safe default for a reverence-first product remains autonomy-supportive phrasing — adopted on principle (adab) even where the measured persuasion delta is modest.

### 4. Guilt and fear appeals are not just unkind — they reliably backfire, and empathy is the better lever

- Guilt and fear appeals reliably provoke a "boomerang effect": the more overtly commanding and explicit the appeal (words such as *don't*, *should*, *ought*; vivid loss imagery), the higher the reactance it triggers, and the reader responds by discrediting the source and reasserting agency — often by doing the opposite ([Isaac, *Why Guilt and Fear Appeals Backfire*, Marketing for Change, 2019 — practitioner synthesis](https://marketingforchange.com/insights-center/why-guilt-and-fear-appeals-backfire/)). A notification that says "You'll lose your hifz" is exactly the kind of high-intensity, overtly-pressuring loss appeal most likely to backfire — and to be rejected.
- The mechanism connecting guilt/fear to backfire is reactance: loss-framed, negatively-valenced appeals can raise guilt, shame, or fear, which can spill into anger and disgust and thereby *increase* reactance, producing boomerang effects ([Reynolds-Tylus, 2019](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/communication/articles/10.3389/fcomm.2019.00056/full)). For our users this is acute: a ḥāfiẓ for whom forgetting already carries real spiritual weight does not need that fear amplified; amplifying it manufactures the very anxiety PRD R3 forbids.
- The constructive alternative is empathy. **Shen (2010)** in *Human Communication Research* showed that **message-induced empathy mitigates psychological reactance** and contributes to persuasion *above and beyond* a message's affective and cognitive content, partly by reducing anger and counterarguing ([Shen, 2010, HCR 36(3):397–422](https://academic.oup.com/hcr/article-abstract/36/3/397/4107508)). The design translation: where the app must deliver hard news (a juz is decaying; a backlog has built up), it should lead with understanding — "life interrupts; here is a calm way back" — not with blame. Empathy is both the kinder and the *more effective* register.

### 5. For religious apps specifically, transactional and pressuring framing produces documented discomfort

- A study reviewing **11 popular Islamic lifestyle applications** through self-determination theory and the technology-as-experience framework, with devout-Muslim user input, found that **most of these apps lack features that foster autonomy, competence, and relatedness** — the three motivational needs that determine whether a tool feels supportive or controlling ([Kabir, Kabir & Islam, 2024, *Islamic Lifestyle Applications: Meeting the Spiritual Needs of Modern Muslims*; preprint arXiv:2402.02061](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.02061)). An app that addresses worship is judged on whether it *supports* the user's autonomy, not on engagement metrics; copy that commands or pressures attacks the very autonomy need this audience expects to be honoured.
- The market-side evidence converges: the project's own `RESEARCH-FINDINGS.md` §4 records a separate peer-reviewed HCI study (the *Islamic Lifestyle Applications* market analysis) documenting explicit user discomfort with the "transactional model of spiritual engagement" — participants found paywalled or pressuring worship features "disheartening." Taken together with the adab evidence (§1) and the reactance evidence (§3–4): in a worship context the autonomy-supportive, non-transactional, gentle register is not merely preferred on taste — it is what the audience experiences as *respect*, and its absence is experienced as a violation. The voice is part of the trust contract the project depends on.

### 6. Addressing the user: pronoun, register, and never speaking *for* the Quran

- The app addresses a *single* private user reciting to themselves or to a present teacher — there is no audience, no social feed, no third party. The correct stance is second-person, direct, warm, and singular ("your revision for today is ready"), consistent with NN/g's guidance to address the reader plainly and the reactance literature's preference for non-distant, non-bureaucratic language ([Moran, 2016/2023](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/tone-of-voice-dimensions/); [Reynolds-Tylus, 2019](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/communication/articles/10.3389/fcomm.2019.00056/full)).
- Adab also constrains *what the app may claim to be*. PRD R6 makes the app a "servant to the talaqqī chain"; the voice must never assume the authority of a teacher or a muftī. It states methodology and surfaces information; it never issues a ruling, never says a page is "safe to drop," and never speaks *on behalf of* the Quran or pronounces on the user's spiritual state. The classical adab framing — the app on the gentle, encouraging *teacher* side, the Quran and the user's relationship with Allah held above the software — is the boundary ([Yasien Mohamed, 2023](https://yaqeeninstitute.org/read/paper/etiquette-as-spiritual-nourishment-the-adab-of-the-student-according-to-al-ghazali-and-al-isfahani)).
- Reverence is also typographic-adjacent in copy: the muṣḥaf is named honestly and specifically ("Ḥafṣ ʿan ʿĀṣim — Madani muṣḥaf," PRD R2), Allah's name and Quranic terms are rendered correctly, and honorific phrases (ﷺ after the Prophet's name, raḍiya Allāhu ʿanhu) appear where a name appears — small markers that signal the app knows whose text it handles.

### 7. The same voice across three RTL languages requires transcreation, not literal translation

- Maintaining one brand voice across languages "requires more than direct translation; it demands a strategic framework," because "direct translations rarely capture the tone, style, and intent of your original message," and a phrase that lands in one language "may lose its meaning — or worse, sound awkward or inappropriate — in another" ([Smartling, transcreation vs. translation](https://www.smartling.com/blog/six-ways-transcreation-differs-from-translation); [*Transcreation*, overview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcreation)). Transcreation recreates content so it "evokes the same emotional response and intent as the original" — here, the same calm reverence — rather than mapping words one-to-one. The remedy is a single voice charter as the source of truth, with each language free to vary surface wording to preserve the *feeling*.
- Pronoun and register do not map across these languages, so a literal translation would silently change the tone. **Persian** carries a grammaticalised politeness/honorific system bound up with *taʿārof* and *face*: the formal second person *šomā* and honorific verb forms are conventionally polite, but Izadi (2015) shows honorifics "may not necessarily lead to positive evaluations" — politeness is evaluated *in interaction*, so register must be chosen as a deliberate social stance (warm-respectful), not assumed from a dictionary form ([Izadi, 2015, *Journal of Pragmatics* 85:81–91](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378216615001794)). For a calm private companion, Persian copy should read as respectful-but-warm — neither the cold over-formality nor the over-familiar *to*.
- **Arabic** likewise has no single "you": the second-person pronoun splits by number and gender (and address terms, kinship terms, and titles add further layers), so English's one egalitarian "you" cannot be carried over directly, and politeness/power dynamics drive which form is appropriate ([Al-Hamzi, Nababan, Santosa & Anis, 2024, *Cogent Arts & Humanities* 11(1)](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/23311983.2024.2359764)). Arabic copy must also soften the imperative: a bare command verb can read as curt, so directives are mitigated (a calm statement of readiness rather than an order). **Kurdish Sorani** has its own register conventions and — per PRD §13.4 — its religious-revision vocabulary (sabaq/manzil equivalents) is regional and still pending native-speaker + scholarly review, so its tone cannot be assumed from Persian or Arabic and must be set by native reviewers against the same voice charter.
- The practical consequence: tone is QA-able per locale. The voice charter defines the four NN/g targets once (§2); each language's strings are then reviewed by a native speaker *and*, for religious terminology, a scholar (PRD §13.4, §21), checking that the *feeling* — calm, reverent, non-coercive — survived the move, not merely that the words are accurate.

## Implications for Hifz Companion

Concrete, testable rules. Each traces to the evidence above and is enforceable in the ARB string files and a `voice-tone` review gate.

1. **Adopt one voice charter with fixed NN/g targets, in all three languages.** The voice is **respectful, matter-of-fact, serious, and warm** (formal-leaning but never cold). No humour about the muṣḥaf or hifz; no irreverence toward any subject; no exclamation marks in product copy (per blueprint §5). The charter is the single source of truth; fa/ar/ckb strings are *transcreated* to preserve this feeling, never literally translated. (§2, §7; Moran 2016/2023; Smartling/transcreation)
2. **Never use controlling language; phrase as invitation and information.** Ban "you must," "you have to," "you should," "don't," and imperative scolding from default copy. Prefer statements of readiness and gentle suggestion: "Your revision for today is ready" (PRD §14), "You can change this anytime." Where guidance is given, offer options, not commands. (§3; Miller et al. 2007; Reynolds-Tylus 2019)
3. **Restoration-of-freedom by default: the cycle, budget, and pace are the user's.** Surface choice explicitly — "raise budget / lengthen cycle / pause new sabaq" (PRD §12.2) — and let copy remind the user that the schedule belongs to them (Pure-cycle mode, named cycles). This is the autonomy-supportive antidote to reactance and the design expression of *adab* toward the user. (§3; Miller et al. 2007)
4. **Zero guilt or fear copy — release-blocking.** Never "You'll lose your hifz," "You're falling behind," "You haven't opened the app in N days," "Don't break your streak." Such loss-framed appeals backfire via reactance and weaponise an existing spiritual fear (PRD R3, §14). A copy lint/test asserts no banned phrases ship in any locale. (§4; Reynolds-Tylus 2019; guilt-appeal backfire research)
5. **Lead with empathy when delivering hard news.** Decay warnings and missed-day catch-up open with understanding, then a calm path forward: "You missed 3 days — here is a 5-day catch-up plan that still completes your cycle" (PRD §7.9), never blame. Empathy both respects the user and measurably reduces resistance. (§4; Shen 2010)
6. **Speak as a gentle teacher's aide, never as an authority over the teacher or the Quran.** Copy consistently frames the app as a servant to talaqqī (PRD R6): it never issues a fiqh ruling, never says a page is "safe to drop" (PRD §7.12), never speaks for the Quran, and never pronounces on the user's spiritual standing. Where scholarly sign-off is pending (terms, mutashābihāt, endorser), copy stays "aid to revision" and flags "needs scholarly review." (§1, §6; Yasien Mohamed 2023)
7. **Address the user directly, singular, warm — with locale-correct register.** Second person throughout. **Persian**: respectful-warm register (*šomā*/honorific verb forms as a deliberate calm stance, never cold over-formality nor over-familiar *to*). **Arabic**: choose number/gender-appropriate forms and *soften imperatives* into statements of readiness rather than bare commands. **Kurdish Sorani**: register and religious vocabulary set by native reviewers against the charter; do not infer from Persian/Arabic. (§6, §7; Izadi 2015; Al-Hamzi et al. 2024)
8. **Render the sacred with reverence in copy.** Name the muṣḥaf specifically ("Ḥafṣ ʿan ʿĀṣim — Madani muṣḥaf," never "the Quran" in the absolute — PRD R2); render Allah's name and Quranic terms correctly; include honorifics (ﷺ, raḍiya Allāhu ʿanhu) where names appear; use correct transliteration (ḥāfiẓ, murājaʿa, mutashābihāt, talaqqī, muṣḥaf, riwāyah). Reverence is communicated in the small details. (§1, §6)
9. **No transactional or pressuring framing — worship is served, not managed.** No "upgrade," no urgency manufactured for engagement, no copy that treats revision as a debt to the app. Copy must actively *support* autonomy, competence, and relatedness — the three needs Islamic apps most often fail to honour (§5). The user is a guest being served (PRD intent: built lillāh as ṣadaqah). (§5; Kabir et al. 2024)
10. **Make tone a per-locale QA gate.** Every user-facing string is reviewed in fa/ar/ckb by a native speaker, and religious terminology additionally by a scholar (PRD §13.4, §21), checking that the calm-reverent-non-coercive *feeling* survived transcreation — not merely that the translation is literal-accurate. Banned-phrase linting and an RTL voice-screenshot pass run per locale in CI (PRD §20.5). (§2, §7)

### Anti-patterns — we will never:

- Ship controlling/command copy ("you must," "you should," "don't fall behind") or scolding imperatives in any locale.
- Use guilt-, fear-, or loss-framed wording — above all "You'll lose your hifz" or any broken-streak shame state.
- Make the app speak *for* the Quran, issue a ruling, declare a page "safe to drop," or claim authority over a teacher.
- Use exclamation marks, hype, cheer, mascots, or jokes in product copy about the muṣḥaf or hifz.
- Literally translate strings across fa/ar/ckb without transcreation review, letting register/tone drift silently.
- Assume Persian, Arabic, and Kurdish Sorani share a register, or infer Sorani religious vocabulary from the other two.
- Frame revision as a transaction with, or a debt owed to, the app.
- Manufacture urgency, pressure, or engagement at the cost of the user's calm.
