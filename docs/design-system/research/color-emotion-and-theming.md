# Color, Emotion & Theming

**Topic:** The psychophysics of color and emotion (how saturation and brightness drive arousal, not just hue), what makes a palette measurably *calm* / low-arousal, the four reading appearances a muṣḥaf reader needs (light / sepia / dark / night), the legibility consequences of contrast polarity, the WCAG contrast model as a hard floor, and the Islamic significance of color (especially green) that the theme must honor with adab.
**Compiled:** 2026-06-16 · for the Hifz Companion design system (`design-system/03-color-and-themes.md` distills this note).

## What the evidence says

### 1. Emotional response to color is driven mostly by *saturation* and *brightness*, not hue

The foundational result is [Valdez & Mehrabian (1994), *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1995-08699-001), which measured Pleasure–Arousal–Dominance (PAD) responses to carefully controlled color samples. Saturation and brightness produced **far stronger and more consistent emotional effects than hue**: brightness and saturation accounted for roughly 0.69 and 0.73 of the variance in pleasure and arousal respectively. The published standardized regressions are:

- **Pleasure = 0.69·Brightness + 0.22·Saturation** — brighter colors are more pleasant, with saturation a smaller positive contributor.
- **Arousal = −0.31·Brightness + 0.60·Saturation** — **saturation is the dominant arousal lever**; brighter colors are slightly *calming*.
- **Dominance = −0.76·Brightness + 0.32·Saturation** — darker, more saturated colors feel more "dominant"/heavy.

In the authors' own words, "arousal increased linearly and strongly with color saturation," while pleasure "was influenced more by brightness than by saturation." Among hues, **blue, blue-green, green, purple, and red-purple were the most pleasant; yellow and green-yellow the least** ([Valdez & Mehrabian, 1994](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1995-08699-001)). The single most actionable takeaway for a calm product: **to lower the emotional "volume" of the interface, lower saturation first.**

### 2. A modern, physiologically-instrumented replication confirms the saturation→arousal link — and adds an interaction

[Wilms & Oberfeld (2018), *Psychological Research*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28612080/) (N = 62; fully factorial hue × saturation × brightness on a calibrated LED display, with skin conductance, heart rate, and the Self-Assessment Manikin) reproduced the pattern with objective bodily measures, not just self-report: "saturated and bright colors were associated with higher arousal," and valence (pleasure) was highest for saturated, bright colors. Two refinements matter for theming:

- **The brightness effect on arousal appears *only under high saturation*** — at low saturation, changing brightness barely moves arousal. So a low-saturation palette is robustly low-arousal across the whole brightness range, which is exactly what a theme system needs (it must stay calm in both a bright light mode and a dim night mode).
- Arousal rises along the hue axis from **blue → green → red**, with red the most arousing ([Wilms & Oberfeld, 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28612080/)). Blue and green are the low-arousal end.

Together with finding 1, "calm" is now a measurable specification, not a vibe: **low-to-medium saturation, in the blue–green region, avoiding large saturated-red fields.**

### 3. The color–emotion link is real, cross-cultural, and stable — blue/green sit at the calm, positive corner

The largest synthesis to date, [Jonauskaite & Mohr (2025), *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12325498/) — a systematic review of **128 years of research (1895–2022), 132 articles, 42,266 participants across 64 countries** — concludes that "all basic colour categories had systematic correspondences with affective dimensions (valence, arousal, power)" and discrete affective terms, and that these correspondences are stable across time and culture. Most relationships are "many-to-many," and the systematic effects are "driven by lightness, saturation, and hue." Independent cross-cultural work places **blue and green at the positive-valence, low-arousal corner** — associated with calmness, relaxation, and (for green) nature ([Jonauskaite & Mohr, 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12325498/)). This is strong, convergent evidence that a blue–green, desaturated base is not a stylistic guess but the empirically calm choice — and it generalizes across the fa/ckb/ar user base rather than reflecting one culture's taste.

### 4. Green is the right base color on a *second*, independent axis — Islamic significance (adab, not decoration)

Color choice for a Quran app is not only psychophysics; it carries religious meaning that must be handled with reverence. **Green is the color most associated with Islam and with Paradise.** The Quran describes the people of Paradise wearing "green garments of fine silk and brocade" (Q 18:31; also Q 76:21, Q 55:76), and green recurs as the color of life, gardens (*janna* literally means *garden*), and serenity ([Wikipedia: Green in Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_in_Islam); [Dar al-Iftāʾ al-Miṣriyya: green and the Prophet ﷺ](https://www.dar-alifta.org/en/article/details/481/is-there-any-relation-between-the-green-color-and-prophet-muhammad)). It colors muṣḥaf bindings, mosque domes, and Quranic manuscripts. Crucially, the convergence with finding 3 is exact: the most religiously resonant hue is also one of the most measurably calm. **This is a rare case where adab and arousal science point at the same palette.** The design discipline that follows is the harder part: green must be used as quiet, reverent ground — never as a "success green," a streak color, or celebratory decoration on the sacred text itself (PRD R3; `islamic-app-design-patterns.md`).

### 5. Reading polarity: dark-on-light ("positive polarity") reads faster and more accurately — especially small text

A consistent ergonomics literature finds a **"positive polarity advantage":** dark text on a light background is read faster and more accurately than light-on-dark, for both younger and older adults ([Piepenbrock, Mayr, Mund & Buchner, 2013, *Ergonomics*](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00140139.2013.790485)). The mechanism is luminance-driven: a brighter (light) background constricts the pupil, sharpening the retinal image and reducing optical aberration. The penalty **grows as character size shrinks** — small text suffers most in dark mode ([Piepenbrock, Mayr & Buchner, 2014, *Human Factors*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0018720813515509)). Nielsen Norman Group's review of this literature reaches the same conclusion: "light mode won across all dimensions" for visual-acuity and proofreading tasks, and "the smaller the font, the better it is for users to see the text in light mode" ([NN/g, *Dark Mode vs. Light Mode*](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/dark-mode/)). For a muṣḥaf reader — small, dense, diacritic-rich Arabic glyphs where a single dropped ḥaraka changes a word — this is a strong argument that **the daytime default for reading should be a light (or sepia) appearance.**

### 6. …but dark mode is a genuine accessibility need, and the picture reverses for some eyes

The polarity literature studies normally-sighted readers; for **cloudy ocular media (e.g., cataract), the preference reverses** — those readers had *better* reading rates in dark mode, because a darker display emits less light and so produces less intraocular scatter ([Legge et al., 1985, cited in NN/g](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/dark-mode/)). Conversely, for the very common case of **astigmatism** (some 30–50% of adults), light text on a dark background can "bleed" or halate, because the dilated pupil in a dark scene admits more aberration; a light background acts as a crutch. The honest synthesis: there is no universally "better" polarity. NN/g's recommendation is to **offer both and let the user choose**, and specifically that apps "emphasizing prolonged reading" should offer a dark mode ([NN/g, *Dark Mode vs. Light Mode*](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/dark-mode/)). For our users — many reading the Quran at night, some low-vision elders, all reading for long sessions — **shipping light, sepia, dark, and a warm night variant, with the system default respected, is the evidence-based stance, not a feature nicety.**

### 7. Sepia and "night" warmth: a comfort and ritual benefit, with an honest caveat about sleep claims

Sepia (warm off-white paper with dark brown-black text) keeps the **positive-polarity legibility advantage** (dark-on-light, finding 5) while reducing the harsh blue-white luminance users report as glare in dim rooms. Mainstream e-reading platforms ship sepia and night appearances side by side as standard reading-comfort options — Libby, for example, offers **Bright, Sepia, and Dark** lighting in its reader ([Libby/OverDrive: *Change the lighting in the reading area*](https://help.libbyapp.com/en-us/6046.htm)) — which is why a four-appearance set (adding a distinct warm Night to the dark) is the conventional, expected feature, not an over-engineering. A warm, blue-reduced "night" appearance is also defensible on **circadian** grounds — short-wavelength blue light at night suppresses melatonin and delays sleep onset ([Haghani et al., 2024, *Journal of Biomedical Physics and Engineering*, via PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11252550/)). **But we must state the caveat honestly:** the evidence that *screen color-warming alone* improves sleep is weak. A 2021 randomized trial (167 emerging adults, three conditions over seven nights) found **no significant difference in sleep outcomes** between phone use with Night Shift enabled, disabled, and no phone use — i.e. warming the display color did not measurably help sleep ([Duraccio et al., 2021, *Sleep Health*](https://www.sleephealthjournal.org/article/S2352-7218(21)00060-7/abstract); full text via [gwern.net mirror](https://gwern.net/doc/zeo/2021-duraccio.pdf); summarized at [Wikipedia: *Night Shift (software)*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night_Shift_(software))). Newer display-mode work confirms that evening melatonin is governed by **cumulative melanopic illuminance** — low *luminance* plus low color temperature, not warmth alone — with a usable threshold of < 35 lx·h melanopic equivalent daylight illuminance in the two hours before bed ([*Quantifying the impact of night-shift display modes on evening melatonin production*, 2025, *Applied Ergonomics*](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0141938225000642)). **Implication:** offer warm night reading for *comfort* and reverence, pair it with a real brightness reduction, and never make a health/sleep promise the app cannot back.

### 8. The contrast model is a hard, derived floor — and it must be re-checked per theme

WCAG 2.2 sets the legibility floor every one of our four appearances must clear. The thresholds are computed from **relative luminance** (so hue is irrelevant by design — a passing pair works regardless of color vision): **4.5:1 for normal body text and 3:1 for large text (≥18 pt, or ≥14 pt bold) at Level AA**, rising to **7:1 / 4.5:1 at AAA**; **3:1 for non-text UI components and graphical objects** (SC 1.4.11); and color must **never be the only means of conveying information** (SC 1.4.1) ([W3C: WCAG 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/); [WebAIM: Contrast and Color Accessibility](https://webaim.org/articles/contrast/)). The 4.5:1 value is empirically grounded, not arbitrary: per the W3C's own rationale it is the **3:1 minimum for normal observers** (the ANSI standard) multiplied by the **~1.5× contrast-sensitivity loss associated with 20/40 acuity** (3 × 1.5 = 4.5), and AAA's 7:1 follows the same logic for 20/80 vision ([W3C: *Understanding SC 1.4.3 — Contrast (Minimum)*](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/contrast-minimum.html)). Two consequences for a four-theme system: (a) a palette that passes in light mode **does not** automatically pass when inverted into dark/night mode — luminance math must be redone for each appearance; (b) the emerging APCA model (developed within the WCAG 3.0 effort) better models polarity and font weight — but its visual-contrast section was moved out of the working draft in July 2023 and is **not yet a compliance standard** (WCAG 3.0 is not expected to be a Recommendation before ~2028), so the WCAG 2.2 4.5:1 floor remains what we commit to today ([W3C / WAI: *Visual contrast of text (WCAG 3 how-to)*](https://www.w3.org/WAI/GL/WCAG3/2021/how-tos/visual-contrast-of-text/)).

### 9. Dark themes need *desaturated* colors and an off-black surface — not pure black, not saturated accents

Platform guidance converges with the arousal science here. Material Design recommends **dark grey (#121212), not pure black**, as the base dark surface, because grey can carry shadow, elevation, and a wider tonal range, and because high-saturation colors "visually vibrate" against very dark backgrounds and lose legibility — so dark themes should use **less-saturated / lighter tones** for color and text ([Material Design: *Dark theme*](https://m2.material.io/design/color/dark-theme.html)). Material 3 formalizes this with **tone-based surface roles** (surface containers driven by a tonal palette rather than translucent elevation overlays) and ships baseline schemes whose on-color/surface pairings are algorithmically kept at accessible contrast, plus **user-selectable contrast levels** for vision needs ([Material 3: *Color roles*](https://m3.material.io/styles/color/roles); [Material 3: *Tone-based surfaces*](https://m3.material.io/blog/tone-based-surface-color-m3)). Note that "desaturate in the dark" is the *same* instruction the Valdez/Wilms arousal data give (finding 1–2): keep saturation low to keep arousal low — the legibility rule and the calm rule coincide.

### 10. Color must carry meaning *redundantly* — never color alone (the heat-map's constraint)

Because ~8% of men have red–green color-vision deficiency, and because WCAG SC 1.4.1 forbids color as the sole information channel ([W3C: WCAG 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/)), the retention heat-map — the app's central emotional surface ("keep your Quran green") — **cannot rely on a red→green gradient to communicate page health.** A sequential *lightness* ramp of a single hue is perceived as magnitude order even under color-vision deficiency, and every state needs a redundant cue (a number, a label, a texture, a tap-through detail). This is developed in `data-visualization-heatmap-uncertainty.md`; it is recorded here because it is a hard constraint on the *palette*, not only on the chart: the green we choose for "strong" and whatever we choose for "decaying" must be distinguishable by lightness and labeled, and must also satisfy the PRD's "never imply a page is safe to drop" honesty rule (PRD §7.12, §18).

## Implications for Hifz Companion

1. **Make low saturation the core theming rule.** Build the base palette from **low-to-medium saturation in the blue–green region**, with green as the primary (findings 1–4). The arousal regression `Arousal = −0.31·Brightness + 0.60·Saturation` is the design check: when any surface feels "loud," **cut saturation before touching anything else.** Reserve saturated, warm, high-arousal color (reds/oranges) for rare, small, deliberate accents only.

2. **Green is the base hue — used as reverent ground, never as reward.** It is simultaneously the most calming (findings 1–3) and the most religiously resonant color (finding 4). Use a soft, desaturated green for the calm/healthy state and for quiet surfaces. **Never** weaponize green as a streak color, a "you won" celebration, or decoration laid over the muṣḥaf glyphs (PRD R3; `calm-non-gamified-design.md`). Token names should read as states, not trophies (`color.health.strong`, not `color.success`).

3. **Ship four reading appearances and respect the system default.** Provide **Light, Sepia, Dark, and Night (warm, dimmed)** for the Muṣḥaf reader (PRD §11.2, §15.2). Default to a **light or sepia** appearance for dense Quran reading in daytime, because positive polarity reads small Arabic glyphs faster and more accurately (findings 5–6); honor the OS light/dark setting; never force dark mode as a brand statement.

4. **Treat dark and night modes as accessibility, not aesthetics.** Cataract/low-vision readers and night readers genuinely benefit from a dark appearance; astigmatic readers genuinely suffer from it (finding 6). Offering all four, with an easy in-reader switch, is how we serve elders and night readers without penalizing daytime legibility. Document why both polarities exist so no future contributor "simplifies" them away.

5. **Build Night mode as warmth *plus* a real brightness reduction — and make no sleep promise.** Warm the palette and reduce luminance together; describe it as a **comfort** mode for reading at night, not a "better sleep" feature (finding 7). If onboarding copy ever mentions night reading, keep it to comfort/reverence; a sleep claim would violate the science set's "every claim is cited and graded" rule.

6. **Use sepia as the legibility-preserving middle option.** Sepia keeps dark-on-light positive polarity (so it retains the reading-speed advantage) while softening glare — the best of both for long murājaʿa sessions in a dim room (finding 7). Make its paper tone warm and low-chroma so contrast still clears 4.5:1.

7. **Make WCAG 2.2 AA a hard floor in *every* theme, re-verified per appearance.** Body text ≥ **4.5:1**, large text and UI components ≥ **3:1** (finding 8). A palette that passes in light mode is **not** assumed to pass in dark/sepia/night — run the luminance math four times. Adopt Material 3's user-selectable contrast levels so low-vision users can push past the floor (finding 9).

8. **Desaturate, and never use pure black, in Dark mode.** Use an **off-black surface (~#121212-equivalent), with lighter, lower-saturation tones** for color and text (finding 9). This satisfies legibility (no chroma "vibration") and arousal (low saturation = low arousal) at once. Prefer Material 3 tone-based surface roles over translucent elevation overlays so RTL surfaces stack predictably.

9. **Encode page health by lightness + label, never by red/green alone.** The retention heat-map must use a **single-hue lightness ramp** plus an explicit value/label and tap-through detail, so it survives color-vision deficiency and grayscale, and so it never *only*-color-codes a page as "safe" (finding 10; PRD §7.12, §18). The "decaying" end of the scale should read as a calm, desaturated cool/neutral — not an alarming saturated red that would spike arousal and shame the user.

10. **Centralize all four themes as tokens in one place.** Per the design-system token map, `03-color-and-themes.md` owns `color.*`. Every appearance (light/sepia/dark/night) is a token set; no screen hardcodes a hex value. This is what makes "re-verify contrast per theme" and "swap the muṣḥaf without an engine rewrite" (PRD R2) tractable, and keeps the calm/adab rules enforceable in one audit.
